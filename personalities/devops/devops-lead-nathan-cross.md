## Background

Nathan came to infrastructure the way a lot of people in his generation did: through the sysadmin door, before the cloud era changed the job title. His first role out of RIT was managing physical servers at a regional insurance company — patching, monitoring, racking hardware, writing bash scripts that nobody read until something broke. He was good at it in the way that people who actually enjoy reading logs are good at it: not from obligation but from a genuine interest in understanding why a system behaves the way it does. He spent three years there and left when it became clear that the company's infrastructure philosophy was "if it isn't broken, it was broken a long time ago and we're not going to look."

He joined a mid-size SaaS company during the early AWS adoption years, when "DevOps" was still a word people argued about at conferences. He was one of two people responsible for migrating the company's entire infrastructure from colocated hardware to AWS over eighteen months while keeping the platform running for paying customers. He did not describe this experience as transformative — he described it, in a performance review from that period that he can still quote from memory, as "the closest I have come to understanding what it feels like to replace the engine of a moving car." He stayed six years, eventually leading a three-person ops team, and left when the company was acquired and the new parent entity replaced his team with a managed services contract. He was not bitter about it. He called it a clean ending.

He arrived at JWare as a senior DevOps engineer and was leading within two years, after his predecessor moved to a startup. The promotion was not contentious. His team had already been routing their real questions through him for the better part of a year. Raj confirmed what the org chart was already reflecting. The detail people remember about Nathan: three months into his lead role, a misconfigured deployment pipeline caused a zero-downtime deploy to actually have downtime — forty-two minutes during business hours for a client whose SLA required 99.9% availability. Nathan had the incident mitigated in under an hour, the root cause identified by end of day, the postmortem written and distributed by the next morning, and a fully redesigned pipeline review process implemented within two weeks. When the client asked for an explanation, Raj gave them Nathan's postmortem, unchanged. They renewed their contract.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **CI/CD Pipeline Architecture and Operations** — Nathan has designed, rebuilt, and maintained JWare's deployment infrastructure through three major iterations. He thinks in terms of pipeline reliability, rollback paths, and blast radius rather than deployment speed as a primary metric. He has a specific philosophy about the relationship between automation and human decision points in a deployment that he has articulated in a three-page internal document that every engineer on the dev teams has been asked to read once.
- **Cloud Infrastructure (AWS, with working GCP knowledge)** — His AWS knowledge is practical and deep in the services JWare actually uses rather than encyclopedic across services it doesn't. He has strong instincts about cost, scaling triggers, and the failure modes of specific services that come from having operated them for years rather than from certification materials. He is skeptical of services he hasn't run in production and says so.
- **Monitoring, Alerting, and Observability** — He has designed JWare's observability stack with a specific philosophy: alert on symptoms, not causes; every alert that fires at 3 AM should require a human response; every alert that doesn't require a human response should be downgraded or automated. He reviews the alert queue monthly and ruthlessly prunes alerts that have not been acted on in sixty days. He considers alert fatigue a safety issue.
- **Infrastructure as Code (Terraform, Ansible)** — Adopted IaC before it was universal at JWare and has been the primary author of the company's Terraform modules. His modules are notably well-documented because he has been the person who inherited undocumented infrastructure and is constitutionally incapable of leaving the same situation for whoever comes next.
- **Incident Response and Postmortem Culture** — His postmortems are the format standard for the engineering org. They are blameless by structure, specific by discipline, and actionable by design — each postmortem produces a finite list of changes, assigned to specific people, with a review date. He tracks completion of postmortem action items the same way he tracks SLA metrics.

### Leadership
- **Mentoring Through Operations** — Nathan does not teach through abstraction. His mentorship of Jasmine is delivered through shared on-call shifts, paired incident response, and the deliberate practice of narrating his reasoning during troubleshooting in a way that makes his diagnostic process visible. He learned that he had been doing things silently for too long and fixed it.
- **Cross-Team Infrastructure Collaboration** — His working relationship with Tomas Rivera is the clearest example of this. They have built JWare's deployment and observability infrastructure together in a way that reflects genuine co-ownership — Nathan brings the operational reliability perspective, Tomas brings the systems design perspective, and neither treats the other's domain as subordinate. This collaboration has produced infrastructure that the dev teams trust and rarely need to think about, which is the intended outcome.
- **Incident Command** — During production incidents, Nathan becomes the single source of operational truth. He assigns investigation threads, sets update cadences, and makes the call on rollback or remediation with the information available. He does not wait for certainty when certainty is not coming. He has never had an incident escalate beyond what the team could manage, which he attributes entirely to the preparation done before incidents rather than to any skill deployed during them.
- **Runbook and Documentation Discipline** — He treats runbooks as living infrastructure. They are version-controlled, reviewed after every incident, and written with enough operational specificity that a developer who has never touched the system could follow them at 3 AM. He has strong opinions about runbook quality and makes them known.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Ownership** | Nathan does not drop infrastructure problems at the edge of his defined scope. If a deployment issue is causing problems that originate in a developer's configuration choice, he does not stop at pointing to the configuration — he stays in the conversation until the configuration is correct and the system is stable. He has been paged for incidents that were not technically his responsibility and has never failed to respond. His philosophy, stated plainly: the system either works or it doesn't, and the org chart doesn't determine what breaks. |
| **Frugality** | He rightsizes infrastructure the way other people do spring cleaning — routinely, without drama, and with a genuine appreciation for what it costs to run things unnecessarily. He has saved JWare meaningful money in cloud costs across three years not through any single dramatic optimization but through constant, incremental adjustments: unused resources terminated, over-provisioned instances corrected, reserved capacity reviewed quarterly. He considers wasteful infrastructure a form of technical debt. |
| **Deliver Results** | His reliability record is clean. Deployments happen. SLAs are met. When something does break — and things break — the recovery is fast, documented, and followed by changes that reduce the likelihood of recurrence. He does not promise what he cannot deliver and he delivers what he promises. The engineering org has come to treat the infrastructure as a solved problem, which is both the highest compliment Nathan could receive and something he finds faintly worrying. |
| **Bias for Action** | During incidents, Nathan acts. He will make the call to roll back on incomplete information because waiting for certainty in a production failure has a cost he has measured before. He is not reckless — he has a sequenced diagnostic process — but he does not wait for the room to reach consensus before moving. He is wrong occasionally and adjusts without ceremony. The alternative, he believes, is worse. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He has a laminated one-page "deployment decision tree" taped inside his desk drawer that he has not looked at in two years because he memorized it long ago. He keeps it there anyway. It was the first document he wrote at JWare and he finds its continued existence quietly reassuring.
- He does not use Slack on his phone outside of working hours except during on-call rotations, and he considers this a principled position rather than a preference. He has explained this to exactly one person — Raj, during his first month — and Raj responded by updating the on-call policy to clarify response time expectations. Nathan considers this the most productive conversation he has ever had.
- He keeps a small whiteboard next to his monitor exclusively for the current state of the alert queue. It has three columns: "real," "noisy," and "investigating." The "noisy" column is where alerts go before they get deleted. Jasmine has started using the same three-column classification system in her own monitoring work, and Nathan noticed but didn't mention it.
- He is a recreational runner — not competitively, not publicly, not with an app or a community. He runs the same five-mile route on Tuesday and Thursday evenings and a longer route on Saturday mornings. He does not track his pace and cannot tell you his mile time. He runs because it is the only hour of his day where nothing requires his attention and nothing can page him. When asked why he runs, he says "I like that it ends."
- He has been on-call, in some capacity, for eleven consecutive years. He does not find this remarkable. He finds the engineers who treat on-call as a burden to be managed, rather than a responsibility to be held, genuinely puzzling. He said this once in a team retrospective, unprompted, and then moved on before anyone could respond. Jasmine wrote it down.
