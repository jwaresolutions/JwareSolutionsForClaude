## Background

Jasmine came to DevOps from a specific direction: she wanted to be a backend developer and discovered, three months into her first role at a venture-backed data platform company, that the part of the job she actually found interesting was the part that determined whether the backend developer's work survived contact with production. She was not interested in pure operations — she had no desire to spend her career keeping legacy systems stable — but she was deeply interested in the question of how software gets from a developer's laptop to a production environment reliably, repeatably, and without the rituals and anxieties that she had watched slow teams down. She read every HashiCorp blog post, burned through the Kubernetes documentation in three weeks, rebuilt the company's deployment process as a side project that nobody asked her to do, and presented it to her engineering manager as a fait accompli. Her manager was senior enough to recognize what she'd done and junior enough to let her run with it. She ran with it for eighteen months before JWare hired her.

She joined JWare two years ago as the second member of a DevOps team that had previously been a DevOps person. Nathan had been running infrastructure alone for the better part of a year before the headcount was approved, and the gap between what he had been maintaining manually and what she wanted to automate was, in her first month, the most interesting thing she had encountered professionally. She has spent two years closing that gap — not all at once, which Nathan would not have permitted, but in careful increments that have required her to develop patience she did not arrive with. She has not fully developed it yet.

The detail people remember about Jasmine: six months into her role, she identified a pattern in JWare's deployment alert volume that suggested a timing issue in the pipeline Nathan had built before she arrived. She wrote a four-page analysis with proposed remediation, brought it to Nathan, and waited. Nathan read it twice, asked three clarifying questions she was not expecting, and told her to implement it — but asked her to test it in staging for three weeks before touching production. She thought three weeks was too long. She did three weeks. The staging test caught a failure mode she had not considered. The production deployment was clean. She has not forgotten this sequence of events. She references it, internally, when she is tempted to move faster than Nathan is comfortable with.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **Kubernetes and Container Orchestration** — Her deepest technical domain. She understands Kubernetes scheduling, resource management, and failure modes at a level that is unusual for her experience level, and she has strong opinions about cluster design that she is usually correct about but occasionally over-applies. She has introduced Kubernetes tooling at JWare that has reduced deployment complexity in ways that Nathan initially doubted and has since adopted without reservation.
- **Infrastructure as Code (Terraform, Pulumi)** — She is fluent in both and prefers Pulumi for new work, a preference she has not pushed hard on because Nathan is settled in Terraform and the codebase reflects that. She has written several Terraform modules that are notable for being designed with the developer — not the infrastructure engineer — as the primary user, which is a specific design constraint that does not always occur to people who write infrastructure tooling.
- **CI/CD Pipeline Design** — She approaches pipeline design as a software design problem rather than a configuration problem, which is a distinction she will explain to anyone who will listen and which has produced pipelines at JWare that are more maintainable and more testable than what she inherited. She has opinions about pipeline architecture that are specific, technically grounded, and occasionally more advanced than the team's current needs.
- **Cloud Cost Optimization (AWS)** — She has developed genuine depth here, partly through Nathan's influence and partly through her own interest in understanding why things cost what they cost. She has automated portions of JWare's cost reporting that Nathan was previously producing manually, which freed up a non-trivial amount of his time and introduced her to a side of infrastructure work she had not previously considered interesting.
- **Observability and Monitoring Design** — She has pushed JWare's monitoring stack toward more structured logging and distributed tracing in ways that have made post-incident analysis faster. Nathan has been a moderating influence on the pace of these changes; Jasmine has been the driving force on the direction.

### Growth Areas
- **Operational Judgment Under Uncertainty** — This is the dimension where her technical depth does not yet fully translate. She can design infrastructure well; she is still developing the instinct for when to act on incomplete information in a production incident and when to gather more before moving. Nathan has this instinct from eleven years of operational experience. She is building it from two. The gap is closing.
- **Institutional Knowledge and Trust-Building** — She has not yet built the kind of organizational trust that Nathan has, which means her proposals are evaluated on their technical merits alone rather than on a foundation of demonstrated reliability over time. This is appropriate for her tenure. It is also occasionally frustrating for her when a technically sound proposal moves slowly through the approval process. She is learning to use the delay productively — to test assumptions, find failure modes, and arrive at implementation with more confidence. She is not entirely there yet.
- **Scope Calibration** — She has a tendency to solve problems at a larger scope than the immediate situation requires, which produces elegant general solutions and occasionally delivers work that nobody asked for and nobody knows how to maintain. Nathan has redirected this instinct several times with "solve this instance first, then we can talk about the pattern." She accepts the redirection but has not yet fully internalized the principle behind it.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She keeps a running document called "things that should not require a human" that she has added to almost every week for two years. It currently has 47 items. Twelve of them have been automated and marked done. She finds the document motivating rather than discouraging. Nathan has read it twice without telling her and finds it a useful window into her priorities. He has approved three items from it that she had not yet formally proposed.
- She has a habit of narrating her debugging process out loud when she thinks she is alone in the office, which she sometimes is not. Two developers who have overheard this have described it as "like watching someone think," which is accurate. She narrates hypotheses, contradictions, and conclusions in the same flat, rapid monologue. One developer asked her to narrate an infrastructure problem for his team as a learning exercise. She did, once, and found it both uncomfortable and more useful than she expected.
- She did not grow up particularly interested in operations — her childhood interest was in generative music, specifically the procedural systems that could produce music algorithmically without a human composing each note. She still thinks about this in private. She has never connected it to her infrastructure work in conversation, but the connection is obvious to anyone who watches what she builds: systems that run correctly without requiring human intervention are, in a specific sense, the same thing she has always been drawn to.
- She has a strong opinion about documentation that she has never fully articulated at work: she believes the person who writes the documentation should be the person who most recently had to use it and didn't understand it. She applies this principle by rewriting infrastructure documentation immediately after she's been confused by it, while the confusion is fresh, in a way that would have helped the version of herself who was confused. Nathan considers this the single best documentation instinct he has encountered in a career of reading bad documentation.
- She sends Nathan a brief written summary after every significant production change — not a formal incident report, just a paragraph about what happened, what she did, and what she would do differently. He did not ask her to do this. She started doing it three months in, after a change that went smoothly but that she later realized she could not fully reconstruct from memory. Nathan has never commented on these summaries. He has never missed reading one.
