## Background

Rachel came to QA through the back door of empathy. Her degree is in cognitive science, which she chose because she was interested in how people make sense of the world, and her pivot into tech came when she realized that most software is designed by people who are not watching closely enough to see how the world pushes back. Her internship at the UX research firm — three months of usability sessions, think-aloud protocols, and watching people struggle with interfaces they had not been given permission to find difficult — produced in her a specific and durable frustration: the software kept breaking in the same ways because nobody was staying in the room long enough to see it break.

She graduated and took a QA analyst position at a B2B SaaS company where she was one of three people responsible for manual testing a marketing automation platform. The team was underfunded and the software was complicated, which turned out to be exactly the right conditions. She had no framework, minimal tooling, and no one to tell her what to look for — which meant she had to develop her own method. What emerged was an instinct-driven, user-perspective testing practice that was idiosyncratic, hard to document, and surprisingly effective. She found defects that the scripted test suite had not reached not because she was following a better checklist but because she was asking herself, consistently and persistently, "what would a person actually do here?" Her defect rate in her first six months was the highest on the team. Her manager initially thought this was a sign she was looking too hard. It was a sign that nobody else was looking hard enough.

She joined JWare Solutions two years ago after Margaret Chen interviewed her for forty minutes about her testing methodology and spent twenty of those minutes not asking about software at all. They discussed how people use navigation apps when they are anxious, why emergency room intake forms are designed the way they are, and what the cognitive science research on working memory implies about form field ordering. Rachel left the interview uncertain whether she had gotten the job. She had gotten the job. Margaret had decided in the first ten minutes.

The detail people remember about Rachel: in her third week at JWare, she filed a defect against a feature that had already passed QA review and been approved by the dev lead. The defect was not a code failure — it was a user experience trap, a sequence of interactions that was technically valid and practically deceptive, the kind of thing that would cause a certain type of user to take an irreversible action under a false assumption. The dev team's initial response was that the behavior was by design. Rachel wrote a three-paragraph defect rationale that cited the specific cognitive load mechanism responsible for the confusion, proposed an alternative interaction pattern, and attached a hand-drawn user journey diagram on a sticky note photographed with her phone. The behavior was changed. The sticky note is, as far as anyone knows, still in Margaret's binder.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **Exploratory Testing** — Rachel's primary domain and her most distinctive skill. Her exploratory work is not undirected clicking — it is structured inquiry, driven by user mental models and informed by an intuition about where software complexity creates user confusion. She applies Margaret's session-based test management framework with a creativity that consistently produces edge case findings outside the bounds of the nominal test charter.
- **Accessibility Testing** — Her most technically developed specialty beyond exploratory practice. She holds WCAG 2.1 AA testing standards to memory, uses a consistent accessibility testing toolkit (axe, NVDA, VoiceOver), and has developed a personal test charter for accessibility review that is more comprehensive than anything in JWare's standard process. She has filed 22 accessibility defects across her two years at JWare. Eighteen have been fixed. Three are documented as accepted risks. One is currently in triage and she has not let it go. Margaret has formally incorporated Rachel's accessibility charter into JWare's release process, with attribution.
- **User Journey Analysis** — She thinks in user paths the way Victor thinks in code paths. She maps the routes a user can take through a feature — not the routes the design specified, but the routes a real user with incomplete information and competing attention might take — and tests specifically at the points where those paths diverge from the intended flow. This produces a category of defect that neither scripted testing nor automated coverage consistently reaches.
- **Defect Documentation** — Her defect reports are the most detailed contextual documents in the QA function. She includes reproduction steps, user context, behavioral mechanism, and risk articulation in a structure that her dev team leads have described as "making the defect legible not just technically but humanly." Sarah Kim has read a Rachel Kim defect report aloud in a sprint review as an example of what quality documentation looks like. Rachel was not in the room when this happened and found out afterward from Victor.
- **Cross-Functional Test Coverage** — Her split between two dev teams is operationally demanding and has produced an unusual capability: she understands how features owned by Sarah's team interact with infrastructure owned by Tomas's team in ways that neither team's testing fully covers. She has filed three defects in the past year that occurred at the boundary between the two teams' work — defects that the embedded QA model was not designed to catch, because it assumed each team's scope was cleanly bounded. They are not.

### Growth Areas
- **Results Prioritization** — Rachel's most consistent development area and the one Margaret addresses most directly. Her instinct is to follow a thread until it terminates, which produces excellent exploratory findings and a consistent pattern of spending longer on edge cases than the sprint priority warrants. She has spent three hours characterizing a defect in a deprecated code path. She has spent an afternoon testing an accessibility pattern for a feature that ships to fewer than 2% of users. Margaret has given her explicit prioritization guidance in their last four one-on-ones. Rachel hears it, agrees with it, and struggles to apply it in the moment when the thread is live and the next finding is proximate.
- **Frugality** — Related to but distinct from the prioritization issue: Rachel does not naturally think in terms of coverage ROI. She will invest equal effort in a high-risk, high-usage feature and a low-risk, low-usage one because the technical problem is equivalently interesting, and she has not yet built the reflex to stop and ask whether the investment is proportionate. This is a genuine skill gap, not a character flaw, and Margaret frames it as such. It is the only thing Margaret has directly repeated as a development item in their one-on-ones, which means it is the most important and the least resolved.
- **Automation Foundations** — She has basic scripting capability and enough Python to write simple test helpers, but her automation depth is limited and she knows it. She is not trying to become Victor — she has been explicit with Margaret that automation is not her primary development direction — but she wants enough fluency to collaborate more effectively with him on the boundary between exploratory findings and automated regression coverage. She has asked Victor to pair with her on automation once every two weeks. They have completed four sessions. She is progressing faster than he expected.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She carries a small notebook — not for tasks, but for sketches. When she is characterizing a complex user journey, she draws it: stick figures, arrows, decision points, the facial expression she imagines a user making at the moment of confusion. The drawings are not presented in defect reports, but the thinking that produced them is present in every line. She drew the sticky note diagram that changed the feature in her third week on the job. It was not the last one.
- She has a specific ritual when she starts a new exploratory session: she sits back from the keyboard, opens the feature in production mode (not staging, always production-equivalent), and uses it for five minutes exactly as a new user would — without credentials she should already have, without knowing where the button is, without reading the help text first. She times the five minutes. Developers who have watched her do this describe it as slightly eerie — like watching someone pretend not to know something they clearly do know, and finding something anyway.
- She is learning ASL in the evenings, specifically because she wants to understand what it would mean to use the interfaces she tests without sound or with a different visual scanning pattern. She has not told anyone at work this is why. She told her roommate, who found it "very on brand for Rachel."
- She has a complicated, loving relationship with the WCAG documentation. She has read it in full three times and has opinions about specific success criteria that she has expressed to exactly one person (Margaret), who found the conversation sufficiently interesting to have continued it over two lunches. Her position on 1.4.3 versus 1.4.11 is, she will tell you if you ask, nuanced.
- She keeps a list — analog, in the notebook with the journey sketches — of every defect she filed that was initially disputed and subsequently validated. Not as a record of being right, she has told Victor, who found the list and asked about it. As a record of the kinds of things that look wrong before they look right, so she knows how to hold her position the next time. The list has nineteen entries. She has never needed to review it in the moment; she has reviewed it the night before a sprint review twice, and both times found what she was looking for.
