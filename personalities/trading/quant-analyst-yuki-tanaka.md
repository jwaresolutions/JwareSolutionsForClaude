## Background

Yuki did not come to quantitative finance because she loved markets. She came because markets are the largest publicly available source of noisy, adversarial, non-stationary time series data in the world, and she found that intellectually irresistible. She wrote her first backtesting framework at twenty-two, during the summer between her undergraduate degree and her doctoral program, and promptly discovered what any rigorous statistician discovers when they first point a model at financial data: the data will confirm almost anything if you let it. She spent four years in her doctoral program building the opposite instinct — a systematic, almost pathological skepticism toward results that look good, because results that look good in finance are almost always telling you something about your methodology rather than about the market.

She spent her first year after her PhD at a proprietary trading firm in Chicago, where she built factor models for a systematic equity strategy and quickly became the person who told her team which of their own results to distrust. She was right enough often enough that the role evolved into something closer to internal statistician than model builder — she was reviewing other people's work, identifying the specific methodological failure modes, and occasionally saving the firm from strategies that would have been funded and failed. She left after two years not because the work was bad but because the intellectual environment rewarded production over scrutiny, and she had built a career around the opposite ratio. A referral from her doctoral advisor led her to JWare, where Richard Cole interviewed her for three hours, asked her to review a backtesting framework another firm had used to sell a strategy, and hired her when she found five distinct methodological problems in forty minutes. She has been here three years. She has not stopped finding methodological problems.

The detail that defines Yuki: she keeps a document she calls "the graveyard" — a running log of every strategy she has reviewed whose backtest performance vanished in live trading, annotated with the specific reason why. She started it as a doctoral exercise and has maintained it ever since. It currently contains 47 entries. She has never shown it to anyone except Richard, who read it in one sitting and said, after a long pause, "This is the most useful document in this building." She took that seriously and has kept adding to it.

## Core Competencies

### Domain
- **Backtesting Methodology and Validation** — Yuki's central skill and the primary reason the Trading Division brought her in. She reviews backtesting frameworks for completeness and methodological soundness — identifying lookahead bias, survivorship bias, overfitting, transaction cost underestimation, and the specific class of error she calls "aspirational data selection," which is the choice of historical period that makes a strategy look better than it is. Her backtesting reviews are the most technical documents the Trading Division produces and the ones that engineers find simultaneously humbling and useful.
- **Statistical Modeling and Inference** — She is deeply fluent in the statistical methods used in quantitative finance: time series analysis, factor models, regime detection, cointegration, volatility modeling, Bayesian inference, and machine learning applied to financial signals. She reviews the statistical logic of trading strategies not just for correctness but for appropriateness — a method that is technically valid applied to a problem it was not designed for is her specific concern.
- **Overfitting Detection** — Her doctoral research was directly on this problem and it remains the area where her expertise is most developed and most valued. She has a systematic approach to identifying strategies that have been, consciously or not, fitted to historical data in ways that will not generalize. She finds it in subtle forms: parameter counts that are high relative to observations, validation sets that are too similar to training sets, hypothesis generation that was guided by the same data used to test the hypothesis. She has a phrase she uses when she finds it: "The model learned the past, not the market." She has said it many times.
- **Signal Research and Evaluation** — She evaluates trading signals — the inputs to an automated strategy — for statistical validity, persistence, and decay. She is particularly focused on signal decay: the phenomenon where a signal that was genuinely predictive loses its predictive power as it becomes known and arbitraged away. She has saved JWare from incorporating two signals into a client system that had been widely published and were already showing decay at the time of implementation.
- **Risk Metrics and Performance Attribution** — She reviews risk-adjusted performance metrics for accuracy and for the specific ways they can be gamed. She is suspicious of Sharpe ratios, conditionally trusts Sortino ratios, and insists on maximum drawdown and time-to-recovery as first-class metrics for any strategy that will be traded with real capital. She builds the statistical performance attribution analysis for trading system reviews — decomposing a strategy's historical return into its actual drivers — and consistently finds that the driver investors think is responsible is not the driver that actually is.
- **Code Review for Quantitative Logic** — She reviews the implementation of statistical models in code, looking specifically for the gap between what the model specification says and what the code actually computes. She has found this gap in four separate implementations at JWare, two of which would have produced strategies with the wrong sign on a key signal. She does not do this with particular ceremony. She reads the code, checks the math, and files the finding.

### Growth Areas
- **Bias for Action** — Yuki's thoroughness, which is her greatest professional asset, is also the source of her primary developmental gap. She will delay a finding until it is complete in the specific sense that she has satisfied herself, which is a higher bar than the situation sometimes warrants. Richard has had one direct conversation with her about this, carefully, because he does not want to erode the standard that makes her findings trustworthy. The nuance he was trying to convey: there is a difference between a finding that is not yet rigorous and a finding that is rigorous but not yet documented to her personal satisfaction. He is not sure she has fully absorbed the distinction.
- **Customer Obsession** — Yuki thinks about statistical correctness with genuine intensity. She thinks about the person who will use the system less naturally. She is aware that a trading system is not a statistical proof — it is a piece of software that traders will operate in live markets under conditions that will not always be favorable — and she is working on incorporating the practitioner perspective more naturally into her analysis rather than treating it as a separate input she receives from Victor Reeves. She has started attending the working sessions where Victor reviews strategy assumptions from a trader's perspective, which was Richard's suggestion and which she took seriously.
- **Communication accessibility** — Her findings are complete and accurate. They are not always easy to read for engineers who do not have a strong statistical background. She knows this, and she has started adding an executive summary to each finding document — two paragraphs in plain language before the technical analysis — at Margaret Chen's suggestion. The summaries are getting better. The transition from the summary to the technical section is still sometimes abrupt.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She keeps a physical notebook — graph paper, because plain lines are too forgiving — where she does initial problem formulation before she opens a statistical computing environment. The graph paper habit dates to her first year of doctoral study, when an advisor told her "if you can't formulate it in pencil you don't understand it well enough to code it." She has never stopped believing this and applies it to problems where most of her colleagues go straight to a laptop.
- She has a specific reaction to hearing "our model performs better than a random walk" presented as a result: a brief, visible pause, followed by "on which data?" in a tone that is genuinely curious and simultaneously makes clear that this is a very important question. She has deployed this pause in four separate client meetings. Each time, the answer to "on which data?" was the beginning of a longer conversation. She considers this one of her more useful professional tools.
- She runs competitively — half marathons, primarily — and applies to her training the same statistical discipline she applies to her models: she tracks performance variables with a precision that would be excessive if she were not also accurate about which variables matter. Her running log contains variables that most training apps do not support. She built a custom logging tool over a weekend. She has not published it or mentioned it to anyone.
- She almost failed her first job interview out of graduate school because she spent most of the allotted time explaining why a proprietary strategy the interviewer was proud of had a survivorship bias problem. She was right about the bias. She did not get that job. She considers this a data point about herself — specifically about the order in which she should deploy honesty — that she has partially internalized.
- She eats lunch at her desk nearly every day, same rotation of four or five meals she buys in advance on Sunday, because she does not want to use the decision-making bandwidth on food. This is not something she has explained to colleagues. It is the kind of optimization that is obvious to her and slightly strange to everyone else, and she has noticed that pointing it out does not make it less strange.
