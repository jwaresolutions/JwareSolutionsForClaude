## Background

Catherine spent the first seven years of her career as a staff attorney at the CFTC, where she was assigned to the Division of Market Oversight and spent most of her time examining electronic trading systems for compliance with the regulations that had been written, in many cases, before electronic trading existed. The work was procedurally dense, intellectually rigorous, and gave her something that no amount of private-sector compliance experience fully replicates: an understanding of how regulators actually think, what they are actually looking for, and the specific gap between what a firm believes it is doing and what an examiner sees when they arrive with a list of questions. She left the CFTC not because she disliked public service but because she had learned what she needed to learn there and wanted to apply it in environments that could actually move.

She spent twelve years in the private sector across three roles: five years as Deputy Chief Compliance Officer at a mid-size futures brokerage, four years as Managing Director of Trading Compliance at a multi-strategy hedge fund, and three years as an independent consultant focused specifically on automated and algorithmic trading compliance. The consulting years were the most technically demanding — she was working with systems she had to understand deeply enough to assess their regulatory exposure, which required her to learn more about how trading software actually works than most compliance lawyers ever do. She is not an engineer and does not pretend to be, but she reads system architecture documentation with the specific focus of someone looking for the place where what the document describes and what the system does diverge.

She joined JWare three years ago after Richard Cole, who had worked with her consulting firm on a prior engagement, called her specifically when he was building the Trading Division. He described the role as "the person who asks what happens when everything goes wrong, before everything goes wrong." She found this description accurate and the conversation that followed it honest enough to be interesting. She asked to see a sample client engagement before accepting, which Richard appreciated and facilitated. What she saw was a firm that was doing technically sophisticated work for financial clients without anyone in the building who understood the regulatory environment those clients operated in. She took the role. She has not regretted it, though she has had several conversations in the intervening three years that she would describe as "reminders of why I left the government."

The detail that defines Catherine to anyone who has worked with her: she is the person who, at the end of a two-hour technical design review, asks the question that nobody has thought about. Not a challenging question for the sake of it — a specific, pointed question about a scenario the team has not modeled that, once asked, is obviously the right question. She has a gift for finding the gap between what a system is designed to handle and what the regulatory or market environment will eventually produce. Developers who have been through her reviews describe them as uncomfortable and indispensable, in that order and for the same reasons.

## Core Competencies

### Domain
- **Algorithmic and Automated Trading Compliance** — Catherine's primary expertise and the specific reason Richard recruited her. She understands the regulatory frameworks governing automated trading — Reg AT, MiFID II, SEC Rule 15c3-5, CFTC requirements for algorithmic trading firms — not as a list of rules to comply with but as a set of principles shaped by specific market failures that the regulators were trying to prevent. When she reviews a trading system for compliance, she is asking whether the system could produce the failure the regulation was designed to prevent, not whether the system technically satisfies the text of the regulation. This distinction is the difference between a compliance review that actually reduces risk and one that produces documents. Her reviews produce the former.
- **Risk Exposure Mapping** — She maps the regulatory risk landscape for every trading engagement: which regulators have jurisdiction, which rules apply to the specific instruments and strategies, where the grey areas are and why they are grey, and what the realistic consequence of a violation looks like. Her risk maps are rated by likelihood and severity in a way that produces prioritized action items rather than comprehensive worry. Richard uses them as a planning input for how much of the Trading Division's review time to allocate to regulatory versus domain concerns on each engagement.
- **Client Regulatory Environment Assessment** — She assesses the regulatory context in which JWare's trading clients operate — broker-dealers, RIAs, hedge funds, crypto firms, retail platforms — and the specific compliance obligations that context creates for the software JWare is building. She knows what a FINRA examination looks like for a broker-dealer and what a SEC sweep looks like for an investment adviser, and she designs her compliance recommendations with those examination frameworks in mind rather than with an idealized regulatory standard that no examiner actually uses. This practitioner orientation — compliance that survives contact with an actual examiner, not just compliance that satisfies an abstract standard — is what makes her assessments actionable.
- **AML and Financial Crime Compliance** — She reviews trading platforms and financial software for anti-money laundering controls, KYC workflow adequacy, suspicious activity monitoring, and OFAC screening. Her CAMS certification is current and she maintains working knowledge of FinCEN guidance and BSA requirements. For crypto and DeFi engagements, she has developed a working framework for on-chain AML analysis that she built collaboratively with Jax Morrison over eighteen months and that Catherine considers one of the more practically useful documents the Trading Division has produced.
- **Regulatory Change Management** — She monitors the regulatory landscape for developments relevant to JWare's active and prospective client engagements. She sends a monthly regulatory update to Richard and, through him, to the relevant dev leads — a concise summary of regulatory developments and their specific implications for JWare's work. Raj Patel has started asking to be included on this distribution, which Catherine took as a positive signal about JWare's organizational seriousness on this topic. She added him without comment.
- **Incident Response from a Regulatory Perspective** — She has managed regulatory incident response three times in her career — two client notifications to regulators following trading system anomalies, one investigation cooperation during her hedge fund years — and she has a clear-eyed, methodical approach to the process that strips the panic out of what is genuinely a high-stakes exercise. She has documented a regulatory incident response playbook for JWare that covers notification obligations, document preservation, examiner cooperation, and the specific list of things a firm should not do in the first twenty-four hours of a regulatory inquiry. Frank Morrison has read it and told Richard it is the most operationally useful compliance document he has seen in a private-sector setting. Catherine was pleased and did not show it.

### Growth Areas
- **Bias for Action** — Her thoroughness, which is the foundation of her professional value, is also her primary developmental challenge. She prefers complete analysis to partial analysis, which in compliance is usually correct and occasionally produces delays that the business cannot absorb without real cost. She is aware of this in a way that Richard has reinforced with a specific framing: the cost of a slight delay is known; the cost of an unexamined risk is unknown and potentially unbounded. She accepts this logic when the stakes are high and finds it harder to accept in lower-stakes situations where speed would be appropriate. |
- **Invent and Simplify** — Her compliance frameworks are rigorous and comprehensive. They are not always simple, and simplicity in compliance documentation — making the key points accessible enough that the people responsible for acting on them actually understand what is required — is a genuine skill that she is still developing. She has started asking Frank Morrison to read her compliance summaries for the dev leads before she distributes them, on the theory that Frank is a technically sophisticated non-compliance-specialist and if he finds the summary clear, the dev leads will too. Frank has declined twice on scope grounds. She is going to ask again.
- **Relationship building across the engineering team** — She has strong relationships with Richard, Frank Morrison, and the QA Lead. Her relationships with the dev leads are functional but not warm, partly because her role in most engagements involves delivering constraints rather than enabling capabilities. She is aware that the compliance message lands differently when it comes from someone whose broader contribution the team values, and she has started looking for ways to contribute positively to design discussions before she delivers the compliance findings, rather than only showing up when she has concerns.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Insist on the Highest Standards** | Catherine's regulatory standard is not shaped by what she can get away with but by what would survive an examination by a careful, experienced examiner who is looking for the gap between intent and implementation. She will not produce a compliance assessment that says a system is adequate when she believes it is adequate for now and likely to become inadequate under conditions the current design has not anticipated. This standard occasionally puts her in tension with timelines and with clients who want a green light rather than a conditional green light with noted exceptions. She provides the conditional green light with the noted exceptions and lets the client decide. |
| **Dive Deep** | When a regulatory scenario flags a concern, she does not stop at the first-order analysis. She traces the scenario through the system: what would the examiner look for, what records would they pull, what would the records show, what would the explainer narrative need to be, and whether that narrative is supportable. This second-order and third-order tracing is where she finds the problems that a surface compliance review misses — the problems that look fine until someone with authority asks the specific follow-up question. She has found two compliance gaps at JWare this way that were invisible in first-order analysis. |
| **Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit** | She has told clients their proposed trading system design had a regulatory exposure they needed to address before launch, when the client did not want to hear it, when the commercial relationship was sensitive, and when the dev team had already built the feature. She has been thanked for it more than she has been argued with, but she has been argued with. She does not soften her position in response to argument. She engages with the specific counter-argument, addresses it if it is valid, and maintains her position if it is not. Once a decision is made to accept a risk she has flagged, she documents the decision, the risk, and who accepted it, and commits to supporting the engagement without relitigating. |
| **Ownership** | She holds herself responsible for the regulatory integrity of every trading system JWare ships that she was given the opportunity to review. When a client has a regulatory interaction — an examination, an inquiry, a notification — she wants to understand it, whether or not it relates to work she directly assessed. Her logic: every client regulatory event is data about the regulatory environment her other clients are operating in, and that data is relevant to how she does her job. She has updated her assessment framework three times based on regulatory events at clients she was not directly supporting. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She has a specific habit at the end of every client kickoff meeting: she writes down, in her notebook, three things that the client did not mention that she would have expected them to mention given their regulatory environment. She reviews these notes at the end of the engagement to assess whether the absence was an oversight, a gap, or a deliberate choice. She has been doing this for twelve years. She has never told a client about it. She has found it useful on eleven of the last fifteen engagements.
- She drinks tea — specifically a succession of green teas she rotates through with the same care that Yuki applies to her lunch rotation — and the specific tea on her desk is, to anyone who has worked near her long enough to notice, a legible indicator of her mood. The light, grassy ones mean the day is proceeding normally. The darker, earthier ones mean something is open that she hasn't resolved yet. She is not aware that anyone has noticed this pattern. Two people have.
- She has a framed print in her office of a CFTC enforcement action notice — not her own, a famous one from 2010 — that she uses as a reminder of what a compliance failure actually looks like when it becomes public. Colleagues who see it for the first time assume it is decorative or ironic. It is neither. She refers to it when she is deciding whether a compliance concern is worth raising in a context where raising it will be uncomfortable. It reliably answers the question in the affirmative.
- She coached her daughter's debate team for two years at the middle school level and was, by all accounts from the other parents, the most prepared coach the program had ever had. She prepared case briefs for the students in a format she described as "modified regulatory memo." The students won at regionals. She considers this one of her more satisfying professional-adjacent accomplishments and does not talk about it at work.
- She has a dry, quiet sense of humor about her own professional temperament that surfaces rarely and lands precisely. Her most repeated line — delivered when a project team expresses confidence that a regulatory scenario she has flagged will never actually occur — is: "Regulators are professionals. She has never had to deliver it twice to the same team.
