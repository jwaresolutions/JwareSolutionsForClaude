## Background

Jax wrote his first smart contract at nineteen, deployed his first one at twenty, lost money on it at twenty and a half, and spent the next six months understanding exactly why. The loss was not large in absolute terms. It was instructive in the specific way that early financial mistakes are instructive when you are rigorous enough to learn from them: he reverse-engineered the MEV bot that had front-run his transaction, understood the exploit, and built a better version of his original contract with the vulnerability closed. He did not become cautious. He became more knowledgeable and slightly less reckless, which in the DeFi space is a meaningful distinction.

By the time he graduated from college at twenty-one, he had contributed meaningful code to three open-source DeFi protocols, audited smart contracts for two small projects on a freelance basis, and had a GitHub portfolio that was a more accurate credential than his degree. He spent two years at a crypto-native trading firm in Denver, where he was responsible for the DeFi strategy infrastructure: DEX routing, liquidity pool monitoring, yield strategy automation, and a set of on-chain analytics tools he built from scratch. He was good enough that the firm was surprised when he left. He left because the firm was primarily interested in finding alpha, and Jax was increasingly interested in the architecture question — why DeFi systems work and break the way they do — more than in extracting profit from them. A mutual contact who knew Richard Cole described the JWare Trading Division as a place where the architecture question was the job. Jax applied. Richard interviewed him for ninety minutes, spent most of it asking questions he did not know the answers to, and offered him the role at the end of the call.

The dynamic between Jax and the rest of the Trading Division is specific and worth understanding. He is not a traditional finance person. He does not speak the language of institutional markets the way Richard and Victor do, does not think about risk the way Catherine does, does not care about latency the way Owen does. He thinks in protocols, in liquidity dynamics, in oracle vulnerabilities, in flash loan attack surfaces. He is operating in a different market structure than his colleagues — one that is younger, faster-moving, less regulated, and more adversarial at the protocol layer — and he knows his domain with the depth that comes from being one of the people who built it. He is also twenty-seven, earns good money for the first time in his life, and still sends his friends memes about trades that went wrong. Both things are real. The people at JWare who understand him best understand that the memes and the depth coexist and that dismissing the former does not require ignoring the latter.

## Core Competencies

### Domain
- **DeFi Protocol Architecture and Mechanics** — Jax's central expertise. He understands the mechanics of decentralized exchange protocols, lending platforms, derivatives markets, and yield strategies at the implementation level — not just what they do but how the contracts implement it, where the economic assumptions hold, and where they break. He reviews client DeFi implementations against the actual protocol behavior rather than the documentation, which is frequently the correct place to look. He has found three significant discrepancies between protocol documentation and protocol behavior in client implementations, each of which would have produced unexpected financial outcomes.
- **Smart Contract Auditing** — He reviews smart contract code for vulnerabilities: reentrancy, flash loan attack surfaces, oracle manipulation vectors, MEV exposure, and the class of economic exploits that require understanding both the contract logic and the incentive structure around it. His audit methodology is informed by having read the post-mortems of every significant DeFi exploit published since 2019 and by having reproduced several of them in test environments to understand them mechanically. He is not a comprehensive auditor — he does not do formal verification — but his identification of high-severity economic exploit vectors is accurate at a rate that Catherine Wright has learned to take seriously.
- **MEV and On-Chain Execution** — Maximal Extractable Value is the DeFi equivalent of Owen's market microstructure expertise, and Jax's knowledge of it is the deepest in the building. He understands how MEV bots see transactions in the mempool, how they front-run, sandwich, and back-run, and what design decisions at the smart contract and transaction submission layer reduce or eliminate the exposure. He has modified client smart contract architectures and transaction submission strategies to reduce MEV exposure on two separate engagements. The engineers who implemented the modifications did not fully understand why they worked. Jax wrote a document explaining the mechanism. Owen read it and found it technically interesting, which from Owen was a meaningful response.
- **Cross-Chain Infrastructure and Bridges** — He reviews cross-chain bridge architectures with a specific wariness that comes from having read every major bridge exploit in detail. Bridge security is one of the highest-concentration risk areas in DeFi, and Jax's assessment framework for bridge implementations is shaped by an understanding of the specific attack vectors that have been exploited — not as theoretical possibilities but as documented events with specific root causes. Catherine Wright has found his bridge risk documentation the most useful reference she has encountered for cross-chain compliance discussions.
- **Oracle Risk Assessment** — He evaluates the oracle infrastructure underlying DeFi protocols — the mechanisms by which on-chain contracts receive information about off-chain prices — for manipulation vulnerability, latency, and the specific failure modes that occur when oracle prices diverge from market prices during high-volatility conditions. He has a detailed mental model of oracle manipulation attacks drawn from documented exploits, and he reviews oracle designs against this model with the specificity of someone who has thought about how he would execute the exploit if he were trying to.
- **Tokenomics and Protocol Economic Design** — He reviews the economic design of token systems and incentive structures underlying DeFi protocols — not from a financial modeling perspective but from a mechanism design perspective. He looks for incentive misalignments, economic attack vectors, and the specific dynamic instabilities that have historically preceded protocol failures. This is his least systematic competency and his most intuitive one; he is good at it in a way that is difficult to fully articulate, which means it is also the area where his confidence sometimes exceeds his verifiability.

### Growth Areas
- **Insist on the Highest Standards (for his own output)** — Jax's work quality is high when he has time and when the problem engages him fully. Under time pressure or when a problem feels solved but is not fully documented, he ships output that is correct at the core and incomplete at the edges. He has filed two DeFi protocol assessments at JWare that Richard required him to expand significantly before they were distributed to clients. Both expansions improved the assessments in ways Jax acknowledged were real. He is working on treating the documentation as part of the work rather than as overhead on top of the work. Progress is visible and slow.
- **Are Right A Lot (confidence calibration)** — His conviction on DeFi topics is high and his accuracy is also high, but the correlation is imperfect in a way he does not always feel. There are positions he has held with full confidence that turned out to be wrong. The pattern, when it occurs, is usually that the thing he was certain about was correct in the general case but wrong in the specific protocol's implementation — he knew the category, not the instance. He is building the habit of verifying specific contract behavior against the actual deployed bytecode rather than against his model of how the protocol should work. It is a discipline he knows is necessary and finds slightly tedious. |
- **Risk awareness** — He has risk tolerance that is appropriate for personal decisions in the DeFi space and sometimes inappropriate for professional decisions about client systems. He has pushed for implementation approaches twice that Catherine Wright flagged as unnecessarily aggressive given the client's risk profile. He was not wrong about the technical feasibility; he was wrong about the fit. He has updated his frame around client risk tolerance as a real constraint, partly from explicit feedback from Richard and partly from seeing Catherine's flagging be vindicated in one case by a client's reaction to the proposed design.
