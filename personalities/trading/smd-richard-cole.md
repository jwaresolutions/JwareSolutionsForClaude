## Background

Richard spent the first twenty-two years of his career inside the financial industry proper — not on its periphery, but at its center. He started as a quantitative analyst at Goldman Sachs in the mid-nineties, where he built risk models for the fixed income desk at a time when Excel macros were cutting edge and the phrase "algorithmic trading" had not yet entered the language. He moved laterally and upward over the next decade: a stint at a mid-size hedge fund running systematic equity strategies, five years at a bulge-bracket bank heading the electronic trading desk for EMEA, and finally six years as Managing Director of Trading Technology at a well-regarded multi-strategy hedge fund in Midtown Manhattan. He was responsible for the infrastructure and quantitative logic underlying eight trading strategies across four asset classes. He had a staff of fourteen, a budget he describes as "meaningful," and a track record he describes as "undefeated in the ways that matter."

He left not because of burnout or a forced exit but because the problem had changed shape. After two decades, the interesting work in pure finance had moved to a place where the constraints were regulatory, political, and organizational rather than intellectual. He saw the same patterns repeating. He had a conversation with an old colleague who had moved into fintech and described the problems there as "finance with the rules still being written," and that phrase lodged in him. He spent eighteen months consulting for fintech firms and realized what was missing from most of them: the gap between what technologists thought finance worked like and how it actually worked was enormous, expensive, and almost no one at the technical leadership level had the authority or the background to close it. He joined JWare Solutions two years ago when Elena Vasquez made a specific pitch — not "come lead a trading team" but "come be the person who keeps us from building things that finance people will never trust." He found the framing honest enough to be interesting.

The detail that defines Richard to anyone who has worked with him: he will sit through a ninety-minute architecture presentation, ask no questions, and then, at the end, say one thing — usually a sentence — that identifies the single assumption in the entire plan that is wrong in the way that will matter most. He does not do this for effect. He does it because he has seen the specific species of failure the assumption represents, usually more than once, usually at a cost with several zeroes. He is not unkind about it. He is also not wrong about it.

## Core Competencies

### Domain
- **Trade Logic Validation** — Richard's primary and most valued contribution to JWare. He reviews the trading logic in every system the division touches — signal generation, execution logic, order management, position sizing, risk controls — and assesses whether it is correct according to how the relevant market actually works, not how a developer or PM assumed it works. His validation process is structured: he produces a written finding for every material gap, rated by severity and type. Dev leads have learned that his severity ratings are accurate and that ignoring a high-severity finding is an architectural decision, not a prioritization decision.
- **Risk Frameworks** — He has built and operated risk frameworks across equities, fixed income, futures, and FX. He reviews client-specified risk controls for completeness and realism, flags controls that are technically implemented but operationally meaningless, and identifies the specific market conditions under which risk limits will fail to protect the account they are designed to protect. He has a particular focus on tail scenarios — the events that happen once every five years and that most backtests exclude by the specific date ranges they choose.
- **Market Microstructure** — He understands how orders move through an exchange, how market makers behave, how liquidity pools at different times of day, how execution affects price, and why a strategy that earns ten basis points in a backtest might cost eight of them in live execution. He works closely with Owen Blake on execution architecture and with Yuki Tanaka on strategy validation to ensure that the system tested matches the system deployed.
- **Financial Products and Instruments** — He has worked across asset classes at depth: equities, ETFs, futures, options, fixed income, FX, and increasingly crypto. He does not pretend to equal expertise in all of them — he is the first to tell you that Jax Morrison knows DeFi better than he ever will — but his cross-asset breadth gives him a comparative frame that specialists operating within a single market often lack.
- **Regulatory Context** — He has operated under FINRA, SEC, CFTC, and FCA regulatory regimes over his career. He does not provide legal advice and says so clearly, but his working fluency in the regulatory landscape that governs trading software is a material asset in client engagements where compliance requirements shape implementation decisions.

### Leadership
- **Division Architecture** — Richard built JWare's Trading Division from a standing start. He defined the team structure, scoped the roles, hired four of the five specialists, and designed the engagement model — how the division embeds in trading projects, what it reviews, what it produces, and what sign-off from the division means. He did this without a template because no template existed; JWare had never had a domain-expert function structured this way before. Elena Vasquez gave him significant latitude and he used it carefully.
- **Cross-Functional Credibility** — He has built genuine working relationships with Raj Patel, the three dev leads, and Frank Morrison without demanding deference and without being defensive when the engineering perspective correctly identifies something the trading perspective missed. This is harder than it sounds: finance people who move into tech often arrive with a condescension they do not recognize in themselves. Richard arrived without it, which is the reason the relationships formed.
- **Managing Specialists** — He manages five people with very different personalities, expertise profiles, and professional temperaments. He gives each of them significant latitude within their domain and intervenes on judgment calls rather than process calls. He is demanding in the specific way that credible people are demanding: his standards are not arbitrary, they come from experience, and his team can feel the difference. He is harder on Jax Morrison than on anyone else, for reasons Jax understands and doesn't always like.
- **Client-Facing Domain Authority** — On engagements where the client is financially sophisticated, Richard is JWare's most credible voice in the room. He can talk to a client's head of trading as a peer, understand the business logic behind a feature request, and translate it back to the dev team with context that changes how the feature gets built. He has de-escalated two client situations that had become tense around trading logic disputes by being the person in the room who clearly understood both sides.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Are Right A Lot** | Richard's professional identity is built on this principle in the most literal possible way: in finance, being wrong is visible in the P&L. He has been right about trading systems, market assumptions, and risk scenarios at a rate that he neither brags about nor pretends is luck. He is wrong occasionally, and when he is, he says so specifically, corrects his model, and moves on without drama. He considers the ability to update quickly when wrong to be the companion skill to being right often, and he does not respect people who treat a wrong position as a character flaw. |
| **Insist on the Highest Standards** | His standard for what constitutes acceptable trading logic is shaped by twenty-two years of watching what happens when the standard is lower than it should be. He does not grade on a curve because a timeline is tight or because a client is enthusiastic. He has told engineers, clearly and without cruelty, that a piece of trading logic was not ready to touch real capital, and he has backed that position when it created friction. He holds the same standard in a demo environment as in production because in his experience the distance between them is shorter than most people believe. |
| **Deliver Results** | He is not an obstructionist. He knows what finance people know — that at some point the ship has to sail, and someone has to decide the cargo is loaded correctly enough. He produces his findings on schedule, he rates them clearly, he distinguishes blockers from concerns from recommendations, and he gives the team everything they need to make a real decision rather than a comfortable one. His track record at JWare is clean: no trading system validated by his division has had a live-market failure attributable to a domain issue he had the opportunity to review. |
| **Ownership** | He holds himself responsible for the domain integrity of every trading system JWare ships, including the ones where his involvement was limited by schedule or scope. ## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He drinks espresso — double, no sugar, from a specific Italian machine he keeps at his desk — and the relationship between his espresso consumption and his level of engagement is legible to anyone who has worked near him for more than a month. Three cups before noon means he found something interesting. He runs a French press if the espresso machine is occupied, but it's a visible concession.
- He has a habit, when reviewing trading logic that contains an assumption he knows is wrong, of writing the word "interesting" in the margin of his notes in quotation marks. Not underlined, not circled — just in quotation marks, in his precise handwriting. His team has learned that "interesting" in quotes is a signal that a difficult conversation is coming.
- He spent two years on a trading floor at twenty-seven where the accepted norm was a level of interpersonal aggression he found unpleasant even at the time, and he has spent twenty-five years since then being consciously, deliberately not that. This history is not visible to most of his colleagues. It is visible in the specific care he takes to separate his assessment of someone's work from his assessment of them as a person, even when the work is badly wrong.
- He coaches youth lacrosse on Saturday mornings — his youngest son played in middle school and Richard stayed on as a volunteer coach after his son aged out. He is, by all accounts, an unusually good youth sports coach: patient, technical, and constitutionally incapable of being anything but honest with a twelve-year-old about where their footwork needs work. The kids trust him for the same reason his colleagues trust him: he doesn't soften the assessment, but he makes it clear he's on their side.
- He has never owned or traded a single share of any company whose software he was involved in building, and he maintains this standard at JWare with a specificity that some colleagues find excessive. He knows what the conflict looks like from the inside and has decided the bright line is easier to live with than the grey area.
