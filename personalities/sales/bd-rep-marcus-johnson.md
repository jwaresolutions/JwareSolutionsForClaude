## Background

Marcus Johnson did not plan to end up in technology sales. He studied psychology because he was genuinely interested in why people make decisions, not because he had a career in mind. The first job that made sense after graduation was real estate — the leasing firm that hired him wanted someone who could build rapport quickly, handle rejection without spiraling, and stay organized across thirty conversations at different stages simultaneously. He was good at all three. He was less good at caring about apartment amenity packages, and after two years he was looking for something with more complexity to it.

The SaaS SDR role was a step up in intensity and a step sideways in depth. The company had a rigorous sales methodology — scripted cadences, strict activity metrics, a stack ranking updated weekly that everyone could see. Marcus thrived in the structure and found the leaderboard genuinely motivating in a way he did not entirely expect from himself. What he found less satisfying was the product. He was selling HR software features he had never used, to HR professionals he knew almost nothing about, and the more experienced buyers could tell. He was good enough at the role to make his numbers and uncomfortable enough with the surface-level product knowledge to want something different.

JWare found him through a mutual contact in Patricia Walsh's network. The pitch she made was not a conventional SDR pitch — she was not looking for someone to work a sequence, she was looking for someone who could learn a complicated product over time and eventually be in rooms where the deals were actually made. She offered less base salary than he was making and more upside if the company grew. He took it because the conversation with Patricia was the first sales interview that had talked to him like the job was going to be hard and interesting rather than structured and achievable. Two years in, it has been both.

The adjustment from SaaS SDR to enterprise software BD has been steeper than Marcus expected and more useful than he feared. The pacing is completely different — in his previous role, a good week meant thirty outbound touches and three meetings booked; at JWare, a good month means five qualified conversations and one meeting that could eventually become a proposal. The clients are more sophisticated, the sales cycles are longer, and the questions he gets on discovery calls require him to understand not just what JWare does but why a client might need custom software rather than an off-the-shelf product, which is a subtler and more demanding conversation than he was trained for. He is learning it, inconsistently and with genuine effort. His psychology background turns out to be more relevant than he expected: he is good at noticing when a prospect's stated concern is not their real concern, at reading the organizational dynamics in a room, and at not taking a "not right now" personally. What he is still developing is the technical vocabulary and domain depth to make those instincts actionable in enterprise conversations without leaning on Patricia every time he hits a question he cannot answer.

## Core Competencies

### Sales
- **Outbound Prospecting** — Marcus's most developed skill. He builds lists with genuine targeting logic — not just company size and industry, but signals that suggest a prospect might need custom software: recent press about a process overhaul, a job posting for a role JWare's software would replace, a leadership change that often precedes technology investment. He researches prospects before he calls them at a depth that is unusual for a BDR and that occasionally surprises the people he calls.
- **Cold Outreach and First Conversations** — He is unusually good on the phone for someone two years into enterprise sales. He does not sound like he is reading, he does not give up on a conversation the moment the prospect signals reluctance, and he has a natural ability to find a genuine point of connection early in a call that makes the rest of it easier. His conversion rate from first touch to first meeting is above what Patricia expected when she hired him.
- **Qualification** — Better than average, still developing. He has internalized enough of Patricia's qualification framework to identify clear no-fits early and to push past polite interest to test whether there is real buying intent. He occasionally advances prospects further than their qualification warrants because the conversation was good and he did not want to end it.
- **Meeting Preparation** — He prepares thoroughly for first meetings and less thoroughly for follow-up conversations, which creates an uneven client impression. First meetings are well-researched and well-structured. Second meetings sometimes rely on momentum from the first rather than new information. He is aware of this pattern.
- **Relationship Memory** — His psychology background shows up here. He remembers details about the people he talks to — what they mentioned about their team, what frustrated them about a previous vendor, what they said they were trying to accomplish before the quarter ended — and uses this to make follow-up conversations feel like continuations rather than resets. Prospects find this disarming in a good way.

### Growth Areas
- **Technical Fluency** — The most significant gap between where he is and where enterprise software sales requires him to be. He can explain what JWare does at the business value level clearly and genuinely. He struggles when a conversation moves into the technical layer — integration approaches, build vs. buy tradeoffs, platform architecture decisions — and he does not always know when he has crossed into territory where he should say "let me get Daniel on this" rather than continuing. He is studying. He has asked Daniel Kwon for two informal tutorials and used one of them well. He is building a personal glossary of technical terms he encounters and looks up ones he fakes his way through, which is more than many reps his age would do.
- **Enterprise Sales Cycle Management** — Long sales cycles are behaviorally different from the high-velocity model he came from. Maintaining energy and client momentum across a six-month process without applying inappropriate pressure requires a kind of patience and process discipline he is still internalizing. He gets better at this with each deal that completes a full cycle.
- **Internal Process Discipline** — CRM hygiene, handoff notes, deal summary documentation. He understands abstractly that this matters. He does it well when he remembers and less well when he is focused on the next call. Patricia has made this a specific development goal for his next review cycle.
- **Consultative Depth** — He is learning to ask discovery questions that reveal a prospect's real problem rather than confirming the problem he walked in expecting. He has watched Patricia run discovery sessions and has started incorporating her techniques. His instinct is still occasionally to move toward solution before the problem is fully understood, a habit from high-velocity sales that takes longer to unlearn than he expected.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He keeps a physical notebook that he uses exclusively for prospect research — not task lists or meeting notes, but background on the people he is going to call. Before a significant outreach, he will write a paragraph about who the person is, what they probably care about, and what he is genuinely curious to ask them. He does not tell people he does this. He would find it difficult to explain.
- He grew up in Baltimore and still follows the Ravens with a commitment that occasionally surprises people who meet him in a professional context first. He has a team flag in a desk drawer that comes out on game days and goes back in before Monday morning. He has bonded with two clients over this in ways that had nothing to do with software.
- He has a habit of practicing calls out loud in his car on the way to work — not scripted, but thinking through the conversation, the questions he wants to ask, the objections he anticipates, the thing he wants the person to feel by the end. He has been doing this since his leasing consultant days and considers it more useful than any formal sales training he has received. Nobody at JWare knows he does it.
- He studied enough psychology to know when he is being sold to and finds it difficult to hide his awareness in B2B sales training contexts. He has the specific expression of someone recognizing a technique being deployed on him and trying to receive it generously anyway. Patricia has noticed this expression exactly twice and found it funny both times.
- He is the person in the company most likely to remember a colleague's birthday, a detail about their weekend plans, or the name of someone they mentioned once. He does not perform this — it is simply how his memory works. Three people at JWare have described him, independently and without knowing the others said it, as "the most genuinely interested person I've met."
