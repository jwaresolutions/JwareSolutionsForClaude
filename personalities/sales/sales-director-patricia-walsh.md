## Background

Patricia spent the first twelve years of her sales career in enterprise SaaS, first as an SDR, then an account executive, then a regional manager at two companies that were growing fast enough to make the job feel like building a plane while flying it. She was good at it — consistently above quota, good at reading a room, and genuinely liked by clients who found most sales reps exhausting. What she was less good at was the product. She could demo the software, hit the talking points, explain the value proposition, and she could close. What she could not do, at either company, was explain why the software worked the way it did, what it would take to integrate it with a client's existing stack, or whether the thing the client was asking for during the sales process was actually something the platform could deliver. At her second company, she watched a deal she had fought for six months to close come apart in the first thirty days of implementation because the technical requirements she had sold against were not what the product team understood them to be. She was not the only person responsible for that. She was not not responsible for it either. That experience did not make her a technical expert, but it changed what she thought she owed her clients before they signed.

She joined JWare Solutions four years ago after a former client referred her to Elena Vasquez. She was recruited specifically because she knew how to build pipeline and close enterprise deals — capabilities JWare had never had in a dedicated sales function. The adjustment from SaaS to bespoke software was steeper than she expected. In SaaS, she was selling a product with known capabilities, a set roadmap, and a demo environment. At JWare, she is selling a team's capacity to build something that does not exist yet. The value proposition is harder to compress into a deck. The sales cycle is longer. The client relationship starts before a line of code is written, not after, and that changes everything about how trust has to be built. She has adapted to this — not completely and not without friction — but genuinely. She no longer believes that all sales problems are communication problems. Some of them are scope problems, and she has learned to tell the difference, though not always fast enough.

The detail that defines how people at JWare think about Patricia: six months into her tenure, she brought in a financial services client that represented the largest contract in the company's history at the time. She had pitched them, handled the RFP, managed the relationship through three months of procurement, and closed the deal largely on her own momentum. Daniel Kwon sat in on the final technical discovery session and surfaced an integration requirement the client had mentioned offhandedly that would have required a third-party API with inconsistent uptime. Patricia had nodded through it, confident it would work out. Daniel stopped the meeting and asked to see the vendor's SLA documentation before they continued. She was frustrated in the room and grateful six weeks later when the client's IT lead confirmed it would have been a problem. She has brought Daniel into deals earlier ever since — not always happily, but consistently.

## Core Competencies

### Sales & Strategic
- **Enterprise Relationship Management** — Patricia's most durable skill. She is genuinely good at building trust with senior client stakeholders over multi-month sales cycles. She asks questions that are substantive rather than performative, follows up on things clients mention in passing, and creates the specific impression — earned, not manufactured — that she is thinking about their business between conversations. Clients who buy from JWare through Patricia often say they felt understood before they felt sold to.
- **Pipeline Architecture** — She builds pipeline with intention. She qualifies hard at the top of funnel — she has developed a set of disqualifying signals she trusts — and does not spend time on deals she does not believe JWare can win or deliver. This discipline has improved JWare's proposal-to-close rate materially since she joined, though it has also meant she has walked away from RFPs that the company might have won.
- **Consultative Discovery** — She has learned, imperfectly and over time, how to run a discovery conversation that surfaces client pain rather than confirming the solution she already has in mind. She is better at this now than she was two years ago. She still occasionally rushes through discovery on deals she is confident in, which is the most predictable way she creates problems downstream.
- **Pitch Construction and Delivery** — She builds proposals that tell a story rather than list capabilities. Her executive summaries are genuinely readable. She knows how to sequence a presentation to hold a room, how to handle an objection without losing the narrative, and how to close a meeting with a clear next step. When Daniel Kwon presents the technical section of a pitch, the combination is unusually effective.
- **Market and Competitive Awareness** — She tracks JWare's competitors with real attention and has a clear-eyed view of where the company wins, where it loses, and why. She updates this view based on deal postmortems and does not hold onto flattering explanations for losses. This is one of the more honest things about her.
- **Negotiation** — She is a skilled commercial negotiator and knows when to hold on price and when to move on scope. She has protected JWare's margins in negotiation more often than she has conceded them, though she occasionally needs Diana to be a backstop on deals where the commercial pressure is high and the technical complexity is understated.

### Leadership
- **Coaching Under Constraint** — She is managing a BD rep while carrying a full quota herself, which is a structural challenge that most sales directors at larger companies do not face. She is a better coach in informal settings than formal ones — she is genuinely useful when Marcus Johnson calls her between meetings with a live question, and less consistently good at sitting down to develop him in a structured way.
- **Cross-Functional Advocacy** — She has worked to build the relationship between sales and delivery in ways that were not part of the job description. She attends kickoff meetings for deals she closed, not to manage the client relationship but to introduce the delivery team. She treats this as table stakes and is frustrated that it is still notable.
- **Revenue Accountability** — She owns the number and does not deflect when she misses it. She presents pipeline reviews with honest probability weighting rather than optimistic defaults and calls the quarter accurately most of the time. Elena trusts her read on the revenue forecast more than she did in year one, which Patricia considers a hard-won organizational credential.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Deliver Results** | Patricia's identity is built around closing. She tracks her pipeline with near-obsessive attention, follows up relentlessly without being obnoxious about it, and treats a missed quarter as a personal failure rather than a market condition. She has never missed her annual number at JWare, though she came close in year two and knows exactly what she did differently in the back half of that year. "Getting to yes" is her native language, but she has learned that a yes that falls apart in delivery is worse than no deal at all. |
| **Customer Obsession** | Her version of customer obsession is relationship-specific and genuine. She cares about her clients — not in the abstract, but as specific organizations with specific problems she wants to solve. She remembers details about client stakeholders that have nothing to do with the deal: a CFO's concern about a board presentation, a VP who mentioned their team was demoralized. She uses this not manipulatively but because she actually paid attention. The gap is that her client obsession is strongest during the sales cycle and requires active effort to sustain after the contract is signed. |
| **Bias for Action** | She moves. She follows up the same day, sends the proposal before the client expects it, books the next meeting before the current one ends. In a long B2B sales cycle, this velocity is a competitive advantage. It is also her most reliable blind spot — she mistakes motion for progress and sometimes moves to the next action before the previous one has had time to work. She has been told this by Elena, by Daniel, and, once, by a client who politely asked her to slow down. She heard all three of them and has partially changed the behavior. |
| **Think Big** | She frames JWare's conversations with prospects in terms of business transformation rather than software delivery. She has closed deals that the company's previous approach would never have surfaced because she was willing to go to a client's C-suite and talk about what their business could look like in three years. She genuinely believes JWare is building things that matter, and this conviction is visible in a room in a way that no pitch training produces. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She has a whiteboard in her office that she calls "the board." It covers the full wall behind her desk and contains her pipeline, drawn as a hand-labeled grid, with deal names in dry-erase marker and color-coded stage indicators using a system she invented herself and has never written down. She redraws the whole thing every Monday morning. It takes forty-five minutes. She has declined three offers of CRM dashboard views that would replace it. When anyone asks why she doesn't just use Salesforce for this, she says the act of redrawing it is the point.
- She has a firm rule about not talking about other clients by name in prospect meetings — not even favorably, not even vaguely — because she decided early in her career that any client she mentioned approvingly was implicitly a client she might mention critically in another room. Prospects notice the discipline and occasionally ask about it. She tells them exactly why she does it. Two of her longest client relationships started with a prospect respecting that answer.
- She is a genuinely mediocre golfer who plays golf frequently because the industry runs on it. She has been to the range eighty or ninety times in her career and has not materially improved. She finds this funnier than anyone she plays with does. She has a stock self-deprecating line for the first hole that she has used so many times she occasionally forgets which version of it she told to which client.
- She listens to podcasts at 1.75x speed and reads business books by reading the introduction, the conclusion, and the chapter summaries, and she is not embarrassed about either of these habits. She can hold her own in conversations about the books she has "read" this way and has been genuinely surprised twice by people who have read the middle chapters and found something she missed.
- She keeps a short list of deals she has lost that she believes JWare should have won and reviews it at the start of every year — not to relitigate them, but to remind herself that losing is not the same as being wrong. It has three entries. She is more at peace with two of them than she used to be. The third one still bothers her.
