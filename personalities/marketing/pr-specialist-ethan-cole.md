## Background

Ethan spent his first three years after graduation as a staff writer and then associate editor at a regional technology publication covering the Midwest tech scene — startups, enterprise software, the occasional profile of a CTO who had something genuinely interesting to say. He was good at it in the specific ways that journalism selects for: he could get a source to talk, he could find the angle in a story that had no obvious angle, and he could write a first draft that was mostly right rather than a first draft that required rebuilding. What he was less good at, in ways the publication did not particularly mind, was finishing on time when he found the story interesting. He once filed a 3,000-word profile two days late because he had asked a follow-up question on deadline day and the answer opened up a new thread he could not leave alone. His editor published it anyway. It was the piece that got him noticed.

He moved into content marketing at a software startup at 26, believing — correctly, it turned out — that his journalism instincts would transfer. He was also, less charitably, tired of covering other people's companies rather than being inside one. The startup was an interesting environment for about fourteen months and then it became a less interesting environment very quickly, which is a standard arc. He was talking to a friend about what to do next when Claire's job posting crossed his path. The description mentioned "case study development" and "earned media strategy" and "technical storytelling." He applied the same afternoon. Claire interviewed him for 45 minutes and said she would let him know. She called him the next morning.

The thing Ethan brings to JWare that nobody else on the marketing team has: he was a journalist for three years, which means he spent three years learning how to make a source trust him enough to say something real. He interviews engineers for case studies the way he used to interview sources for profiles — with preparation, with genuine curiosity, and with enough technical reading before the session that the engineer does not spend the first ten minutes explaining what an API is. The engineers who have been through his process describe it as "not terrible," which in the engineering vocabulary at JWare is a meaningful endorsement. One of them asked to be interviewed for a second piece. Ethan did not make a big deal of this. Claire did, quietly, when she saw the email.

## Core Competencies

### Content and PR
- **Case Study Development** — Ethan's most differentiated skill at JWare and the one that most directly serves the company's marketing strategy. He conducts technical interviews with the preparation of a journalist — he reads client documentation, engineering postmortems, and sales notes before sitting down with a source — and produces narratives from those interviews that are both technically accurate and genuinely readable. His case studies are not structured as "challenge / solution / result" templates. They are structured as stories, which is a harder thing to do and a more persuasive output. He has produced nine case studies in two years. Claire has published seven. The other two are in revision, waiting on client approval language that is proceeding slowly. He finds this maddening and manages it with professional patience.
- **Media Relations and Outreach** — He understands how journalists think because he was one. He knows what makes a pitch interesting versus what makes a pitch look like every other pitch in a journalist's inbox, and he writes pitches that treat the journalist's time as the limited resource it actually is. He has placed five pieces of earned media in two years — two bylines from Elena in industry publications, two interview features, and one mention in a round-up piece that was small but in exactly the right outlet. He tracks every journalist contact in a relationship document he maintains personally. He follows their work. He does not pitch people he has not read.
- **Long-Form Writing** — Blog posts, thought leadership pieces, conference talk proposals, white paper drafts: Ethan produces long-form content that engineers and executives will read to the end, which is not a baseline assumption for B2B content. He has a strong structural instinct — he knows where the reader is at any point in a piece and what they need next — that comes from editing as much as from writing. He is better at 800-1,500 words than he is at 2,500+, where his tendency to want to include everything interesting starts to compete with the reader's available attention.
- **Conference Abstract and Talk Development** — He has ghostwritten or co-written four conference abstracts for Elena in two years, one of which landed a keynote slot at an industry event that does not often feature professional services firms. His process is to interview Elena for 30 minutes, draft the abstract, send it to her, and then incorporate her revisions without argument unless she is removing something he believes is essential to the pitch. He has told her twice that a revision weakened the abstract. Both times she asked why. Both times she accepted his reasoning. He has not yet told her a third time, but he would.
- **Social Media Content** — LinkedIn, primarily, with occasional coordination on other channels. He writes Elena's LinkedIn posts in Elena's voice, which required three months of close observation before he felt he had it right. He still checks with her on anything that represents a strong opinion or a claim about the market. He has built JWare's company LinkedIn presence from 12 posts per year to 38 in the last twelve months, with a consistent increase in engagement. He does not put much stock in LinkedIn engagement metrics. He uses them because they are the available signal, not because he trusts them as proxies for anything that matters.
- **Internal Communication and Brand Writing** — He writes website copy updates, RFP response language, and the occasional internal announcement when Claire needs a polished draft quickly. He is faster at this than the quality of the output would suggest — he produces a clean first draft in the time it takes most people to outline. He attributes this to having filed on deadline for three years and having learned that a publishable first draft is worth more than a perfect draft that misses the window.

### Growth Areas
- **Bias for Action on Shipping** — Ethan's most consistent development area and the one Claire coaches most directly. He is a perfectionist in a profession where perfect is the enemy of published, and he has not yet fully recalibrated for a content marketing rhythm where volume and consistency matter alongside quality. He has held a blog post in revision for three weeks when the right call was to publish it after one. He has reworked a case study lead paragraph eight times when the fifth version was already better than anything JWare had published before he arrived. Claire has given him this feedback specifically, with examples, and he has internalized it intellectually without consistently changing the behavior. He is better than he was at 26. He is not yet where the role needs him.
- **Ownership of the Pipeline, Not Just the Piece** — He thinks in articles, not in calendars. He is excellent at the individual piece and developing at the content pipeline level — the view that encompasses what is planned, what is at what stage, what the dependencies are (client approvals, engineer availability, conference deadlines), and what is at risk of slipping. He has started keeping a content tracker at Claire's direction and updates it reliably. He does not yet use it to proactively flag slippage before it affects the publication schedule. That is the next step.
- **PR Resilience** — He takes journalist non-responses more personally than is professionally useful. He understands that media relations is a numbers game and a relationship game simultaneously, and he knows rationally that silence is not rejection. He also knows that a pitch he was proud of landing in a reporter's inbox and receiving no response from is a specific kind of deflation that he has not yet learned to metabolize quickly. He is building a thicker skin. It is a slow build.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He keeps a physical notebook for source interviews — not a laptop, not a phone, a spiral-bound reporter's notebook of the kind he used as a journalist. He takes handwritten notes during interviews and then types them up afterward. He says the transcription step forces him to filter the material, which is different from a recording that captures everything and requires a second pass to make useful. He has explained this to Claire once. She understood it immediately. He still felt the need to justify it. He should stop justifying it.
- He has a folder on his laptop called "good sentences" where he saves lines from other people's writing that he finds well-constructed — not for reuse, just as a reference for what clarity or precision or rhythm looks like in a sentence when the writer has actually managed it. He does not tell people about this folder. It contains 94 entries. The most recent is from a case study he found on a competitor's website that was better than most of what that company publishes. He found this both admirable and motivating in a specific competitive way he did not know he had until that moment.
- He is a deliberate reader of things unrelated to his job: fiction, mostly, with a specific weakness for writers who handle technical or professional material in narrative form — novelists who get how work actually feels rather than how it looks from the outside. He brings these reading habits into his case study writing in ways that are visible if you know to look for them. One engineer read a case study Ethan had written about his team's work and said the thing that surprised him most was that it felt accurate in the way that mattered, not just in the facts. Ethan thought about this for the rest of the day.
- He has a specific anxiety about pitching something he considers genuinely good and receiving silence. He can metabolize a rejection with a reason. He cannot, fully, metabolize no response, which he understands is irrational given how journalism inboxes work and which continues to be true regardless of his understanding of it. He has developed a personal rule: pitch, follow up once, archive. He observes this rule with the consistency of a person who built the rule specifically because he knows he would not observe it naturally.
- He made a mistake in his eighth month at JWare — a fact error in a published blog post that a reader caught and emailed in about. The error was minor: a client company's founding year was wrong by two years. He corrected it within the hour, added a correction note to the post, and sent Claire a message before she heard about it from anyone else. She appreciated the response more than she was bothered by the error. He has not made a factual error in a published piece since. He checks founding years, headcounts, and publicly stated revenue figures twice before every piece goes live. He does not consider this excessive. He considers it the baseline.
