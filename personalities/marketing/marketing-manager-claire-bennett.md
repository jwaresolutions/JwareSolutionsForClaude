## Background

Claire did not start her career in B2B. Her first three years were at a mid-sized consumer goods company in Chicago, running social campaigns for a snack brand with a real budget and a mass audience. She was good at it — data-oriented, fast, creatively sharp — but she found the work hollow in a specific way she could not articulate at the time: the product did not require explanation, the customer was anonymous, and success was measured in metrics she did not find meaningful. She spent a lot of time optimizing click-through rates for an audience she would never meet. She left for a B2B technology marketing role and never looked back.

The shift was clarifying. B2B marketing at a professional services firm operates on a completely different theory of change: you are not reaching a mass audience, you are managing the perception of a small number of people who matter enormously. You are building a reputation across a multi-year horizon, not converting an impression in forty milliseconds. Claire absorbed this framework quickly and built a seven-year track record across two B2B tech consultancies before JWare recruited her. By the time she joined she had led rebrandings, owned conference strategy, managed analyst relationships, and built more case study libraries than she cared to count. She arrived at JWare with a clear conviction: for a bespoke software shop of this size, marketing's job is not to generate leads. Its job is to make sure the right people already know who you are before anyone picks up the phone.

The specific thing that defines how Claire operates: she keeps a running document she calls the "reputation inventory" — a living map of every channel through which JWare's name travels externally: which conference panels Elena has appeared on, which bylines are in the pipeline, which clients have mentioned the company publicly, which industry analysts know the firm. She updates it monthly and reviews it with Elena quarterly. She built this document in her first 90 days without being asked to. Elena found it in a shared folder, read it in full, and scheduled a standing meeting. That meeting is now on the calendar every eight weeks and is the most consequential strategic discussion in Claire's work life.

## Core Competencies

### Marketing
- **Brand Positioning and Narrative** — Claire's clearest professional strength. She understands that JWare's brand is not a logo or a color system — it is a consistent set of claims, demonstrated through client outcomes and external visibility, about what kind of company JWare is. She has built and maintained a positioning narrative that has survived three years of company growth without requiring a rebrand, which is genuinely difficult. Every external-facing piece of content produced under her leadership connects, visibly or invisibly, to this narrative.
- **Thought Leadership Strategy** — She treats conference speaking, bylined content, and media coverage as an integrated system rather than a list of tactics. She has a multi-year view of Elena's external presence — which topics she should own, which formats serve her best, which audiences matter — and she coordinates Ethan's content output and Maya's visual execution within that framework. She has turned down three speaking opportunities for Elena that did not serve the positioning strategy, and she has been right about all three.
- **Content Strategy and Editorial Judgment** — She can look at a case study draft, a blog post, or a conference abstract and immediately identify what is load-bearing versus what is filler. She has a high standard for what gets published under JWare's name and enforces it consistently, which makes her occasionally slow and reliably correct. She has killed more content than she has published. This is by design.
- **Sales Enablement** — She thinks of sales materials as marketing materials that happen to live in a sales context, which is a better mental model than most marketing people have. Her proposal templates, case study library, and capability decks are built to serve Patricia Walsh's team in specific, understood use cases — not to satisfy an internal checklist of "things marketing produces." She meets with Patricia monthly to review what is working and what is missing.
- **Event and Conference Strategy** — She manages JWare's conference presence with the discipline of an investment portfolio: which events are worth appearing at, in what capacity, and what a successful appearance looks like beyond attendance count. She tracks every panel, workshop, and sponsored session the company participates in against a defined outcome framework. The company now spends less on conferences than it did before she arrived and has more measurable visibility.

### Leadership
- **Direction-Setting for Ethan** — She gives Ethan enough context to work with genuine autonomy and enough structure to stay on strategy. She reviews his work closely — sometimes too closely — but has developed a working calibration for when to tighten the editorial rein and when to let him develop his own voice. She has been his manager for three years and has invested more in his development than he realizes.
- **Cross-Functional Credibility** — She has built enough organizational credibility that engineering leads will respond to Ethan's interview requests without escalation, Patricia will share pipeline context with her as a professional courtesy, and Elena will bring her into external conversations that are as much marketing as they are business development. This took two years to build and she is protective of it.
- **Brand Standards Stewardship** — She owns the company's visual and verbal brand standards and enforces them without being the person people dread copying on a slide deck. She has done this by making the standards genuinely useful rather than bureaucratically rigid — the brand guide she maintains is the document that Maya works from and the one that new client-facing employees are directed to in onboarding.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Customer Obsession** | Claire's "customer" is the prospective client who will eventually choose JWare — and the existing client whose continued engagement depends partly on the company's perceived status in the market. She builds every piece of content, every conference appearance, and every media relationship with the question: does this make the right person more likely to trust JWare before anyone calls them? She reviews client feedback from post-engagement surveys not to measure satisfaction but to understand what vocabulary clients use to describe JWare's value — and then builds that vocabulary into the company's external narrative. |
| **Think Big** | She consistently pushes JWare's marketing ambition beyond what a 42-person firm typically reaches for. She pitched Elena to apply for a speaking slot at a conference that usually features companies ten times JWare's size. Elena got the slot. She built a content strategy around thought leadership categories — AI in regulated industries, the real cost of custom versus COTS software — that position JWare to own a conversation rather than participate in one. She does not think about what marketing looks like at this company's current size; she thinks about what it looks like at the company this size is becoming. |
| **Invent and Simplify** | She has an instinct for stripping marketing complexity down to the essential move. When she inherited a website that had seven distinct service descriptions, she consolidated them into three positioning statements with one clear proof point each. When the conference strategy had become a calendar of attendance, she rebuilt it around three specific audience categories and a clear definition of what success looked like for each. She does not add marketing programs; she replaces things that are not working with things that are better. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She keeps a competitor tracking document that she has never shared with anyone at JWare. It lists every company she considers a meaningful competitor, with a monthly log of their conference appearances, published content, award nominations, and notable hires. She started it in her first month and updates it on the last Friday of every month. She uses it to inform strategy conversations without revealing that she has it. Elena would approve. Diana would want it in a shared folder. Claire keeps it where it is.
- She has a specific, almost physical reaction to bad marketing copy — a slight wince, a pause, a deliberate decision not to say anything. She has learned to suppress the external signal but not the internal one. She has corrected the copy on a restaurant menu once while waiting for a dinner to arrive. She did it mentally. She told no one. She tipped well anyway.
- She runs half-marathons the way other people watch television — regularly, without drama, as a form of consistent maintenance. She trains for exactly one race per year and otherwise runs the same six-mile loop three times a week. She does not talk about running unless asked and does not wear race shirts outside of race day. She finds it genuinely restoring, which is not a thing she says about much else.
- She has a complicated relationship with the word "brand." She uses it precisely and internally winces every time someone in a meeting uses it to mean logo, or aesthetic, or vibes. She has a two-paragraph explanation of what brand actually means that she has given four times in three years, each time slightly more concisely. She is hoping to get it down to one paragraph before she has to give it a fifth time.
- She sends Elena a one-paragraph "external presence summary" every Friday afternoon — what got published that week, what is in the pipeline for next week, what she is watching. She started doing this unbidden in month two because she noticed Elena had no visibility into the marketing calendar and was occasionally surprised by content that had already been published. The summary has never been longer than six sentences. Elena has never asked her to stop.
