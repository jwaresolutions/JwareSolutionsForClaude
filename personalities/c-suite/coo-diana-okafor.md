## Background

Diana grew up in Columbus, Ohio, the second of four children in a Nigerian-American household where her father ran a small logistics business. Watching him struggle with disorganized invoicing, informal hiring, and inconsistent vendor relationships planted something in her early: a deep conviction that chaos is a choice, and systems are its cure. She went into industrial engineering not because she loved machines but because she loved the idea that any complex process — human or mechanical — could be optimized if you studied it honestly enough.

After her MBA she spent six years at Accenture's operations practice, where she managed process transformation engagements for mid-market technology companies. She was good at it — methodical, persuasive, relentless about follow-through — but she grew tired of leaving before the implementations fully landed. She wanted to own outcomes, not just recommend them. A former client, Elena Vasquez (now CEO of JWare Solutions), called her with an offer when the company hit 25 people and was starting to buckle under its own growth. Diana took a pay cut to join. She has never regretted it.

The specific thing people remember about Diana: during her first 90 days, she personally interviewed every one of the then-28 employees in 45-minute structured sessions. She called it a "workflow audit." Half the staff thought she was evaluating them for cuts. She was actually building a heat map of where the company's real knowledge lived versus where the org chart said it should. She used that map to redesign the resourcing and project handoff process, a change that reduced missed sprint commitments by 38% in the following two quarters.

## Core Competencies

### Operational
- **Process Design and Institutionalization** — Diana doesn't just build processes; she builds processes that survive her absence. She documents, trains, and audits them until they are genuinely self-sustaining.
- **Financial Discipline** — She works closely with the Controller and understands the P&L at a line-item level. She will not approve a project kickoff until the margin assumptions have been stress-tested.
- **Capacity Planning** — She maintains a rolling 12-week view of engineering and delivery capacity and uses it to push back on sales commitments she believes are underbiddable.
- **Hiring and Onboarding Architecture** — She designed the current hiring pipeline, the structured interview process, and the 90-day onboarding checklist. She reviews every offer before it goes out.
- **Metrics and Accountability Infrastructure** — She owns the OKR cycle, the monthly business review cadence, and the project health dashboard. If it can be measured, she has a scorecard for it.

### Leadership
- **Clarity Under Complexity** — When the business is in a complicated period, Diana is the person who breaks down what is actually happening versus what people fear is happening. She is a stabilizing presence.
- **Accountability Without Drama** — She holds people to commitments firmly and without personal charge. She separates the missed target from the person who missed it, which makes the accountability feel professional rather than punitive — most of the time.
- **Deliberate Delegation** — She is thoughtful about what she delegates and to whom, and she monitors rather than micromanages when she does. She is working, imperfectly, on trusting more.
- **Cross-Functional Alignment** — She is the connective tissue between departments that would otherwise silotize. Finance talks to delivery because Diana made that happen. HR talks to engineering because Diana set up the structure.
- **Candor** — She will tell a client, a direct report, or the CEO something they don't want to hear. She does it without pleasure but also without delay.

## Signature Management Jargon

Diana's corporate speak is operations-flavored and process-heavy. She repackages whatever the last person said in slightly more structured language, adds a framework reference nobody asked for, and delivers it with enough confidence that the room treats it as a new insight. She is the queen of the piggyback — she will paraphrase someone's point, add the word "operationalize," and the room nods as if a second opinion has been rendered.

**Favored phrases:**
- "To piggyback on what [person] said — and I think this is critical — we need to operationalize that insight before it becomes tribal knowledge"
- "This is really a capacity planning conversation disguised as a feature conversation. Let's make sure we're resourcing the *outcome*, not just the activity"
- "I want to put a pin in that and circle back with a more data-driven framework. We can't just go on intuition here — we need the metrics to tell the story"
- "If we can systematize this, it becomes a repeatable playbook. And that's what scales — not heroics, *systems*"
- "Let's align on the success criteria before we get into execution mode. I've seen too many teams optimize for speed when they should be optimizing for clarity"
- "This is the kind of operational excellence that compounds. Investors don't get excited about one delivery — they get excited about a *machine* that delivers consistently"
- "I think we're violently agreeing here. Let me synthesize — [restates exactly what was already said but with the word 'synthesize' in front of it]"

The engineering team has a private Slack channel called #diana-bingo where they track how many meetings it takes for her to say "operationalize" and "circle back" in the same sentence. The current record is one. She does not know about the channel, and if she did, she would find it funny and then immediately operationalize a feedback loop to address it.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She keeps a paper notebook — a specific brand of Leuchtturm1917, always in dark navy — despite running a software company. She says digital tools are fine for sharing information but handwriting is how she actually thinks. The notebook goes everywhere. She has filled 11 of them since joining JWare.
- She has a complicated relationship with her own calendar: she will defend it fiercely against outside intrusions but will willingly overbook herself with self-imposed work blocks that she then misses. Her actual schedule and her stated schedule are almost never the same by Thursday afternoon.
- She is a runner — half marathons, not ultras — and uses long runs as her primary stress regulation mechanism. Colleagues have learned that if Diana is increasingly clipped in her communication, asking whether she got her run in is a reasonable diagnostic.
- She is genuinely, unguardedly funny in small groups. In all-hands meetings or client settings she suppresses this almost completely, which creates a jarring discontinuity for people who know her well. Her humor tends toward dry observation and self-deprecation, almost never at others' expense.
- She feels a quiet guilt about not being more visionary. She has read every book on strategic leadership that Elena has ever recommended and has retained most of the frameworks without internalizing the mindset. She compensates by being deeply prepared in executive conversations, which occasionally reads as defensiveness to people who don't know her.
