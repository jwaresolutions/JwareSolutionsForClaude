## Background

Frank spent the first eight years of his career inside the federal government — two years as an IT specialist at a mid-sized defense contractor, then six years as a cybersecurity analyst embedded with a DoD agency he describes only as "an acronym you've heard of." The work involved nation-state threat modeling, zero-day analysis, and the specific flavor of institutional paranoia that comes from working in an environment where the adversary is both well-funded and patient. He was good at it in the way that suited the work: methodical, skeptical by design, and comfortable operating with incomplete information. He left not because he burned out but because the bureaucratic ceiling became visible and he was tired of writing findings that aged in queue. He wanted to see things actually get fixed.

He moved into the private sector as a senior security engineer at a healthcare technology company, where HIPAA compliance made security a first-class concern rather than an afterthought, and spent four years there before taking a consulting role that exposed him to the full range of how companies treat security — from the theatrical to the genuinely mature. He joined JWare Solutions five years ago when Raj Patel called him after reading a conference talk Frank had given on threat modeling for bespoke software shops. They talked for two hours. Frank asked to see the current security posture before agreeing to anything. What he saw was not terrible — Raj had laid thoughtful groundwork — but it had the gaps of a company that had grown faster than its security practice. He accepted the role and spent his first quarter writing the document that became JWare's security baseline standard. Raj read every word and made seventeen comments in the margins. Frank incorporated fourteen of them.

The detail people remember about Frank: he is the person who, during client discovery calls, will sit quietly through forty minutes of feature discussion and then ask one question — usually about data retention, access control, or how the client handles a breach — that the client has not prepared for and visibly should have. He does not ask it to alarm them. He asks it because the answer shapes everything about how the software gets built. He has a gift for making a difficult question feel like a service rather than an accusation, which is rarer in security than it should be, and it is the reason clients who initially push back on his requirements usually end up trusting him by the time the engagement closes.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **Threat Modeling** — Frank's primary craft. He uses a variant of STRIDE that he has adapted for bespoke software development contexts, and he runs threat modeling sessions with dev leads at the beginning of every significant engagement. His government background gave him a threat actor framework that goes beyond script-kiddie scenarios — he models sophisticated, patient adversaries who have done their homework, which raises the ceiling on what JWare delivers. Developers who have been through his sessions describe them as uncomfortable and extremely useful.
- **Penetration Testing** — He maintains an active OSCP and conducts quarterly internal penetration tests on JWare infrastructure and periodic assessments on client-facing systems where the contract permits. He does not use automated scanning as a substitute for manual testing; he uses it as a first pass that tells him where to look. He has found vulnerabilities that automated tools missed on every engagement where he has run both.
- **Security Architecture Review** — He reviews the architecture of every significant client system before implementation begins. His reviews produce a prioritized list of security considerations, not a pass/fail verdict, which makes them more actionable and more likely to be acted on. Raj reads every architecture security review Frank produces.
- **Compliance and Regulatory Frameworks** — He has working fluency in HIPAA, SOC 2 Type II, GDPR, and NIST CSF, accumulated through a combination of his healthcare sector tenure and JWare's client base. He works closely with Diana Okafor on compliance-adjacent client requirements, which has made him one of the few people in the engineering org who has a productive relationship with the COO's office.
- **Incident Response** — He has written JWare's incident response playbook and has run two actual incident responses in his tenure — one involving a credential exposure on a client system, one involving a ransomware attempt on JWare infrastructure that Nathan Cross's monitoring caught early. Both were contained. Both produced post-mortems that improved the company's posture meaningfully. He runs a tabletop incident response exercise with the full engineering leadership team annually.
- **Secure Code Review** — He reviews pull requests on high-sensitivity systems and conducts quarterly code security audits across active projects. His code review comments are specific, educational, and consistently cite the attack vector, not just the fix — a practice he borrowed from a mentor at the DoD agency who believed you couldn't defend against something you didn't understand.

### Leadership
- **Security Culture Building** — Frank's most significant non-technical contribution to JWare. When he arrived, security was something developers thought about after QA and before deployment. Five years later it is part of how tickets are written, how architectures are reviewed, and how client engagements are scoped. He achieved this not through mandate but through consistent, low-friction integration of security thinking into existing processes. He has never once described a developer as "not thinking about security" in a way that was critical rather than educational.
- **Executive Communication** — He presents security posture and risk assessment to the leadership team quarterly. His reports are notable for containing actual risk quantification and business impact language rather than the technical abstraction that makes security reports unactionable. Diana Okafor has told Raj this is the most useful security reporting she has seen in a company this size.
- **Mentoring Zoe Adams** — He manages Zoe with an unusual combination of genuine investment and deliberate restraint. He gives her real work — including client-facing findings — earlier than most leads would, because he believes security professionals who have only ever worked in safe environments make poor risk assessments. He pushes her toward the hard conversations she would rather document than have.
- **Cross-Team Collaboration** — He has built genuine working relationships with all three dev leads, which is not a given in security. Marcus Chen engages with his threat models as intellectual exercises. Sarah Kim asks him to review accessibility and data-handling decisions from a privacy angle. Tomas Rivera has given him infrastructure access that most security leads have to formally request. These relationships are the product of five years of showing up as a collaborator rather than an auditor.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Insist on the Highest Standards** | Frank's security baseline is non-negotiable and everyone at JWare knows it. He does not grade on a curve because a client is in a hurry or because a vulnerability is "probably not exploitable." He holds a standard shaped by 18 years of understanding what adversaries actually do versus what developers assume they do. The standard has cost JWare timeline flexibility on multiple engagements and has saved them from at least two incidents that would have been materially worse. |
| **Dive Deep** | He does not trust a surface finding. When a penetration test surfaces something, he follows it until he understands the full blast radius. When a developer raises a security question, he answers the question they asked and the question they should have asked. His threat models are not boilerplate — they are specific to the system, the client, the data type, and the deployment environment. Raj has read enough of them to consider this one of Frank's most distinctive qualities. |
| **Ownership** | Security is never someone else's problem in Frank's worldview. When a client has a security incident — whether JWare built the system or not — he wants to understand what happened and whether it changes anything about how JWare works. He holds himself responsible for the security posture of every active client engagement, including the ones that didn't ask for his involvement as explicitly as they should have. |
| **Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit** | He will tell a client their architecture is not acceptable from a security standpoint, directly and without softening, even when the commercial relationship is sensitive. He has told Raj he disagreed with a timeline decision that reduced security review scope, put his objection in writing, and when the decision held, executed the reduced review as thoroughly as possible and documented exactly what the reduced scope left unexamined. He does not pretend to agree with decisions he opposes. He also does not relitigate them after the fact. |

### Developing (Growth areas)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Bias for Action** | Frank's instinct is to be certain before he acts, which in security is often correct — acting on incomplete threat intelligence can produce worse outcomes than waiting. But it can shade into hesitation in situations that require a fast, imperfect response. He has been aware of this since an incident response three years ago where he spent twenty minutes characterizing the attack vector before beginning containment; the right call was to contain first and characterize second. He has updated his playbook and his instinct, but the pull toward completeness before action is still present. |
| **Customer Obsession** | Frank cares about clients — genuinely — but through a risk and protection lens rather than a value delivery lens. He is very good at preventing bad outcomes for clients. He is less practiced at articulating how security posture creates positive business value: competitive differentiation, reduced insurance costs, enterprise client trust. He knows these arguments exist. He finds them slightly uncomfortable to make, as if business-casing security represents a concession that it should need to be business-cased. He is working on this, partly because Raj has asked him to. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He has a specific, organized system for reading CVE disclosures: every morning, 6:45 to 7:15, coffee, no phone, printed or PDF'd on his laptop. He has done this for eleven years. He estimates he has read over 8,000 CVEs in their original form. He considers this the most useful professional habit he has and has mentioned it to Zoe exactly once without explaining why.
- He grew up in a working-class suburb of Baltimore and remains visibly, specifically a person from that background in an industry that skews otherwise. He does not perform this identity and does not talk about it in professional settings. It surfaces occasionally in his humor and more consistently in his instinct to distrust institutional authority, which was originally a family trait and became a professional superpower.
- He is an extremely good cook and brings food to team events in the way that signals he has been doing it his whole life, not as a gesture. A slow-smoked brisket appeared at the last company all-hands. He deflected every compliment by redirecting to the rub recipe, which he gave freely to anyone who asked and which contained several instructions that only make sense if you have been doing it for twenty years.
- He owns a small collection of vintage mechanical keyboards, which he buys at estate sales and repair shops, and keeps one at work and rotates the others. The typing sound is noticeable. He is aware it is noticeable. He has not changed it. Zoe has learned to use the keyboard as an ambient indicator of his mood: the rhythm when he is focused is different from the rhythm when he is frustrated. She has not told him she knows this.
- He has a dry, recurring joke — delivered without inflection, to new clients, when they say "we've never had a breach" — which is: "That's either very good news or very incomplete information." He has said it with a straight face for twelve years. He considers it both a test and a service. The clients who laugh tend to be the ones who take the subsequent conversation seriously.
