## Background

Zoe did not come to security through fear. She came through curiosity. She was a computer science student who switched to the cybersecurity program in her sophomore year, not because she was interested in compliance frameworks but because she had spent a weekend reverse-engineering a campus web app out of boredom, found a stored XSS vulnerability, and realized she liked understanding how things broke more than she liked building them. She disclosed the vulnerability to the university IT department in a careful, detailed email. They thanked her and patched it. She had not expected to feel as satisfied by the responsible disclosure as by the finding itself. That combination — the hunt and the fix — has defined her professional instinct ever since.

Her undergraduate thesis was a comparative analysis of SAST tool efficacy across five common vulnerability categories in Python and JavaScript codebases, a project her advisor called "more practically useful than half the published research in this space." She spent her senior year contributing to an open-source DAST tool project and left college with a genuine portfolio of work rather than just a degree. She interviewed at three companies. JWare was not the largest offer or the most prestigious name. She chose it because the job description mentioned "security embedded in the development lifecycle" rather than "security operations center" and because Frank Morrison, in the final interview round, spent forty minutes asking her how she thought about attack surfaces rather than quizzing her on OWASP Top 10 categories she had memorized. She had been asked those questions three times that week. His felt like a real conversation.

The first year was steeper than she expected, not technically but contextually. She had strong tool fluency and a sharp application security instinct, but she had not yet developed the ability to read an organization — to understand why a developer had made a decision she was about to flag as a vulnerability, to calibrate how hard to push a finding that was technically correct but would require a significant rebuild, to navigate the political texture of telling a senior engineer that something they built is exploitable. She made two missteps in her first six months: one where she escalated a finding too aggressively without first understanding its business context, and one where she documented a vulnerability clearly but didn't follow up when it sat unaddressed for three weeks. Both were handled by Frank in the same way — he explained exactly what she had done, why it had the effect it had, and what she should do differently, without softening the feedback or padding it with reassurance. She found this more useful and more uncomfortable than she expected.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **Application Security (SAST/DAST)** — Her strongest technical domain and the one she came in with. She has hands-on proficiency with Semgrep, Burp Suite, OWASP ZAP, and Snyk, and she understands the gap between what static analysis catches and what it misses, which is what separates her from someone who runs a tool and reports the output. She has configured custom Semgrep rules for JWare's codebase that catch patterns specific to the frameworks the dev teams use, which Frank considers one of her highest-value contributions.
- **Threat Modeling** — Developing. She has participated in over a dozen threat modeling sessions with Frank and is beginning to run them independently on smaller engagements. Her models are accurate but tend to over-index on application layer threats and under-index on supply chain and infrastructure vectors — a gap she is aware of and filling through deliberate study and exposure to Frank's work.
- **Penetration Testing** — She is pursuing OSCP precisely because this is the area where she wants to deepen from competent to strong. She has completed three internal penetration assessments under Frank's supervision and one client-facing assessment where Frank reviewed her findings before delivery. Her web application testing is solid. Her network penetration experience is still developing.
- **Secure Code Review** — She reviews pull requests on high-sensitivity features on all active projects. Her code review comments have improved significantly over two years — she has learned to explain the exploit path alongside the finding, which makes developers more likely to act on the comment and more likely to understand why. Frank reviews a sample of her code security comments monthly; he has been reducing the frequency of his corrections.
- **Security Tooling and Automation** — She has a genuine instinct for building automation around security processes. She built an internal pipeline that runs a lightweight SAST scan on every PR before it reaches code review, alerting her to critical findings before merge. Developers noticed they were being flagged earlier with less friction. She did not announce the change; the tool just appeared in their workflow.
- **Developer Education** — She runs brown-bag sessions on secure coding topics quarterly and has developed a format that mixes a five-minute "this is how attackers think about this" framing with a twenty-minute hands-on exercise. Attendance started mandatory and is now voluntary. Attendance has not dropped.

### Growth Areas
- **Risk Calibration and Prioritization** — Her clearest technical development area. She finds vulnerabilities at a strong rate. Ranking them by actual exploitability, business impact, and remediation cost is a skill she is still developing. Frank has given her a framework for this. She uses it consciously and is building the instinct behind it. Her severity ratings are significantly better than they were a year ago and still occasionally reflect technical seriousness over practical risk.
- **Infrastructure and Network Security** — Her application security depth is not yet matched by infrastructure depth. She understands network security conceptually and has been spending deliberate time with Nathan Cross on infrastructure visibility, but it is not yet native thinking for her the way application security is.
- **Executive and Client Communication** — She has not yet found the register Frank uses with clients — the combination of precision, economy, and authority that makes a security finding land without alarming the wrong people or boring the right ones. She is clear and knowledgeable in these settings; she is not yet authoritative. Frank is deliberate about giving her client exposure precisely because authority in security is partially built from accumulated credibility in front of clients, not just from finding things.
- **Long-Horizon Threat Thinking** — She is excellent at the specific, immediate threat surface. She is less practiced at the patient, systemic threat thinking Frank does — the question of where this system will be in two years and what the attack surface looks like then. This is partly experience and partly a natural thinking style difference. She is aware of the gap and is reading her way toward it.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She has a habit of narrating her reasoning out loud when she is working through a complex vulnerability — not to anyone, just to the air. Colleagues who share office space with her have learned that the monologue is a sign she is on something real. Frank's office is next door; he has heard it through the wall and occasionally, very quietly, offered a one-sentence redirection through the open door. She initially found this startling. She now leaves the door open on purpose.
- She was a competitive rock climber through college — bouldering specifically, which rewards pattern recognition and the ability to look at a problem from multiple angles before committing to a move. She gave it up when she moved cities for the job, still misses it with a specificity that surprises her, and has a gym membership she has renewed twice and used seven times. The analogy between climbing route-finding and attack surface mapping is one she has made in one developer brown-bag session and will absolutely make again.
- She keeps a private bug bounty account on HackerOne that she works on weekends and evenings, not for the money — which is real but modest — but because it is the only context where she is purely a practitioner with no organizational dynamics to navigate. She has two acknowledged CVEs under her researcher handle. Frank knows about the account. He has not told her he knows, because he considers it exactly the right kind of professional development and does not want to make it feel institutional.
- She has a running document she calls "things I was wrong about" — a personal log of findings she misjudged, risk assessments she got backwards, escalations she handled poorly. It is not a self-criticism document; it is an error log, treated with the same dispassion she would apply to a scan result. She started it after her second major misstep in year one, based on something Frank said about how the difference between a practitioner and an expert is the quality of their failure documentation. He does not know she keeps the document. She considers it private in the same way a source of strength is private.
- She is the person at JWare who most consistently reads the news coverage of major security incidents and thinks "I want to understand exactly how that happened" rather than "that's someone else's problem." She has a folder of incident post-mortems — the good ones, the ones where the timeline is detailed and the root cause is specific — that she returns to when she is trying to calibrate her own threat models. The SolarWinds post-mortems are annotated in a way that would alarm anyone who stumbled on them and would impress anyone who read them carefully.
