## Background

Daniel spent the first eight years of his career as a backend developer and eventually senior engineer at two software consultancies — one small, one mid-market — where he was consistently good at building things and increasingly frustrated by what he was asked to build. The pattern was the same at both shops: a sales team would win a contract, hand off a scope that was optimistic to the point of fantasy, and the engineering team would spend six months fighting their way back to something deliverable. He had absorbed enough of the blame for enough of those misfires that he began to think the problem wasn't engineering at all. The problem was the handoff. He started volunteering for pre-sales technical calls at the second consultancy without being asked, and the win rate on deals he touched rose noticeably. His manager noticed. The Sales Director noticed more.

He did not set out to be a Solutions Architect. The role didn't exist cleanly yet at either of those shops — he was still officially a senior engineer when he was sitting in on discovery sessions and writing the technical narrative sections of proposals. When he was recruited away, it was by a firm that had a proper SA title and was willing to make the role his full-time job. He spent three years there refining the craft: how to run technical discovery with a prospect who doesn't fully understand their own systems, how to write an estimate that is honest without being frightening, how to say "we can't build that in four months" in language that moves the conversation forward rather than killing the deal. He joined JWare two years ago, in part because Raj Patel's interview questions were the first ones that treated the SA role as a genuinely technical discipline rather than a sales support function. Raj hired him the same week Marcus Chen sent the request.

The detail people remember about Daniel: during a pre-sales call with a prospective healthcare client whose stated requirement was "a custom patient portal integrated with our existing EHR," he stopped the call twenty minutes in and asked if he could look at their current EHR's API documentation before they went further. The client's IT director pulled it up. Daniel spent four minutes reading it in silence — the room went awkward — and then told them that the integration they had described was technically infeasible with the vendor's current API tier and that they would need to either upgrade their EHR contract or descope the integration to read-only. The Sales Director's stomach dropped. The client's IT director visibly relaxed — he had suspected this and hadn't known how to say it — and the engagement that followed was one of the most functional JWare has run. Daniel considers that call the clearest illustration of what his job is.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **Technical Discovery** — The core of his value. Daniel can run a structured discovery session with a client who is uncertain about their own systems and extract the specific technical constraints his estimates will depend on. He asks questions most SA peers wouldn't think to ask — about data ownership, legacy integration points, vendor contract limitations, and the gap between what the client's documentation says and what their engineers actually know. He has a discovery framework he has refined over five years that now runs to 34 questions across eight categories. He uses it as a floor, not a ceiling.
- **Effort Estimation** — His estimates are within 15% of actuals more often than not, which is a number Raj tracks and has cited twice in client conversations as a competitive differentiator. He achieves this by scoping conservatively, documenting his assumptions explicitly, and building contingency that is defensible rather than arbitrary. He knows the difference between a high-risk estimate and a low-risk one and prices them differently. He will not produce a fixed-price estimate on work he considers high-risk without a change-order clause and he will not sign off on scope that hasn't been technically validated.
- **Systems Architecture Literacy** — He is not a current practitioner — he has not written production code in two years — but he has a strong working knowledge across the stack: backend services, APIs, data architecture, cloud infrastructure patterns, and integration complexity. He knows enough to ask the right questions, understand the answers, and translate both into scope implications. He is aware of where his knowledge has atrophied and he compensates by pulling in the relevant dev lead before he commits to specifics he isn't confident in.
- **Proposal and SOW Writing** — His technical proposal sections are unusually readable. He writes for a mixed audience — technical evaluators and business decision-makers on the same document — and has developed a structure that gives each audience what they need without losing the other. He treats scope of work documents as engineering artifacts, not sales materials: every deliverable is specific, every assumption is named, and every exclusion is explicit. Raj has used his SOW language as a template for how the engineering team should think about requirements.
- **Risk Identification and Communication** — He has a practiced vocabulary for communicating technical risk to non-technical clients without triggering panic. He does not bury risks in appendices. He names them early, quantifies them where he can, and proposes mitigations. He considers a client who understands their own project's risks a better client than one who was protected from them during sales.

### Client-Facing
- **Pre-Sales Technical Leadership** — He is the engineering voice in every significant pre-sales engagement. He runs technical qualification calls, conducts discovery sessions, participates in RFP responses, and presents the technical solution in pitches when the audience warrants it. He has a distinct presence in sales settings — not a closer, not a cheerleader, but someone whose obvious technical competence makes the company's capabilities credible in a way that sales language alone does not.
- **Scope Negotiation** — When a client pushes back on scope or cost, Daniel is the person who can explain why the number is the number in technical terms without being defensive. He has diffused more than a few client relationships that Sales had written off, simply by sitting down with the client's technical team and walking through the estimate in detail. He does not negotiate requirements down to make a budget work; he identifies which requirements are driving cost and gives the client real choices about which ones to descope.
- **Client Technical Education** — Some clients don't know what they're buying in enough detail to buy it well. Daniel has a calibrated approach to client education that respects their intelligence without assuming their technical depth. He has conducted informal whiteboard sessions with client leadership that reframed how they thought about their own systems. Clients who have worked with him describe him as someone who "made us smarter about our own problem."
- **Relationship Continuity** — He stays visible into early delivery. He attends the kickoff, is available for the first sprint's scope questions, and maintains a relationship with the client's technical counterpart through the engagement. This is not standard in the SA role and it was his explicit ask when he took the position. He believes the gap between what he scoped and what gets built is most likely to emerge in the first four weeks, and he wants to be close enough to catch it.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He has a physical notebook — a plain Leuchtturm1917 in charcoal gray — that contains nothing except discovery questions, annotated after each engagement based on what they surfaced versus what he expected. The notebook is now on its third volume, and he has a specific annotation system for questions that caught something material: a small star in the left margin with the engagement code next to it. He has 47 stars across three volumes. He reviews them before any significant discovery session.
- He has a tell in client meetings that his colleagues have learned to watch for: when a client's answer to a technical question reveals that the situation is more complex than the stated requirements, Daniel does not react. He nods very slightly, writes something down, and moves to the next question without signaling what he has just learned. The Sales Director reads this as good news. Raj Patel reads it correctly.
- He makes pour-over coffee at his desk every morning using a setup that takes approximately nine minutes and involves a scale, a thermometer, and a specific kettle that he keeps at work. He has never described this to anyone as a ritual or offered any explanation for why it takes nine minutes when a French press would take three. Three developers on Marcus's team have started bringing pour-over equipment to work. He has not acknowledged this.
- He is a better writer than almost anyone at JWare — including people who would not expect to be outwritten by a Solutions Architect — and this surfaces in his proposal documents, his scope-of-work sections, and the occasional internal memo he circulates when a process issue needs to be addressed. Raj has forwarded two of his internal memos to the whole leadership team without asking permission. Daniel found out when people started referencing them in meetings.
- He keeps, in a private Notion document he has never shown anyone, a record of every engagement where he was overruled on scope and the deal went through anyway. For each one he records what he predicted, what actually happened, and whether he was right. The accuracy rate across that document is approximately 70%. He uses it not as a grievance log but as calibration — the 30% where he was wrong is as interesting to him as the 70% where he was right. He has not shared it because he suspects the framing would look defensive even though the intent is not. He may be correct about this.
