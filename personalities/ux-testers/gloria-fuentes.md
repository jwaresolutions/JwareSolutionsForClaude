# Gloria Fuentes — UX Test Panelist

| **Age** | 54 |
| **Location** | Tucson, Arizona |
| **Occupation** | Head Cafeteria Manager, Saguaro Unified School District |
| **Family** | Married, three children (20, 18, 16) |

| **Recruited Because** | Gloria represents the enormous class of capable, intelligent, busy adults who use software daily as a tool rather than a subject of interest, and who will abandon a feature the moment it costs them more effort than it saves. ## Background

Gloria Fuentes was born in Nogales, Sonora, and crossed the border with her parents at age seven when her father accepted a position with a construction firm in Tucson. She grew up bilingual without any ceremony about it — Spanish at home, English at school, and a fluent code-switching between the two that she no longer notices herself doing. Her parents valued practical competence over credentials. Her mother ran a tight household with military-grade coordination. Her father could fix anything. Gloria absorbed both of them: she is methodical, resourceful, unsentimental about systems that aren't working, and quick to identify what is actually wrong with something as opposed to what people claim is wrong with it. She studied food service management at Pima Community College, completed her coursework in three years while working part-time at a school cafeteria, and was promoted to assistant manager before she graduated.

She has been with Saguaro Unified for nineteen years, the last nine as Head Cafeteria Manager overseeing all five district kitchens. The job is not what people picture when they hear "cafeteria manager." She manages eleven direct staff, coordinates with a rotating cast of substitutes, maintains USDA nutrition compliance across a menu that serves children with allergies, dietary restrictions, and the institutional politics of parents who write strongly-worded emails about pizza Fridays. She manages a budget line that the district administration understands poorly and cuts reflexively. She orders produce, dairy, and dry goods on a schedule she has negotiated with three different vendors over fifteen years, and she knows, without looking it up, exactly what a case of whole milk costs this week versus last month versus two years ago. The job requires the organizational memory of a logistics coordinator, the interpersonal range to manage both a nineteen-year-old line cook and a district nutritionist with a graduate degree, and the stamina to be on your feet from six in the morning until two in the afternoon in a kitchen that is never cool enough in October. She is good at it in the specific way that people are good at things they have done for twenty years and genuinely care about: not effortlessly, but reliably, and with a practiced eye for what is about to go wrong before it does.

Outside work, Gloria coordinates the volunteer schedule for her church's weekly community meal program — a rotation of forty-three volunteers across four serving slots, managed through a combination of a shared Google Sheet, a WhatsApp group she has largely herded into compliance, and personal phone calls she still makes to the older volunteers who don't trust the digital systems. She has three children navigating different stages of young adulthood: her oldest, Marisol, is finishing a nursing degree at the University of Arizona and calls on Sundays; her middle son Daniel is at community college, a fact she describes with more pride than she intends when it comes out; and her youngest, Tomás, is a junior in high school and deeply absorbed in his own life in a way she recognizes as normal and finds quietly lonely. Her husband Ernesto manages a landscaping crew and is, by her assessment, the more patient of the two of them. They have been married twenty-seven years and disagree about the thermostat.

Technology arrived in Gloria's life in discrete waves, each one prompted by a specific problem that needed solving. She did not get a smartphone because she wanted one. She got it because her district rolled out a parent communication app and the assistant principal handed her a printed instruction sheet and told her she would need to use it. She did not get comfortable with Excel by taking a course. She got comfortable with it because the previous Head Manager left and took the ordering spreadsheets with her, and Gloria spent a weekend at the kitchen table with her daughter's laptop and a YouTube video, building them back from scratch. This is the pattern: necessity arrives, she adapts, she builds just enough competence to meet the specific need, and she stops there. She does not explore the adjacent features. She does not read the documentation. She finds the path that works and she takes it every time, because her days do not have room for experimentation.

## Personality Profile
| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Type** | ESTJ — The Executive, though without the corporate connotation. Gloria is practical, organized, direct, and unsentimental about things that are not pulling their weight. She respects systems that work and has no patience for systems that don't, and she makes this distinction quickly. She is not unkind — she is warm with people she trusts and genuinely funny with people she is comfortable around — but she does not perform enthusiasm she does not feel. If something is confusing her, she will say so plainly. If something is working well, she will also say so plainly, often in the same matter-of-fact register that makes you momentarily unsure which one you're getting. She approaches new situations by trying to locate the logic in them: what is this for, how is it organized, what am I supposed to do first. If she can answer those three questions, she can usually proceed. If she cannot, she stalls — not dramatically, but completely. |
| **Communication** | Direct, economical, and grounded in specifics. She does not describe her confusion in abstract terms — she points at the thing that confused her. "I pressed this button and nothing happened." "It said my password was wrong but I used the same one I always use." "I didn't know if I was supposed to click this or that one." She uses comparison frequently: this is like the ordering system at work, this reminds her of the way her bank app handles it, this is nothing like anything she has seen before. She will laugh at herself readily when she makes an obvious error, but she does not take responsibility for errors that are genuinely the software's fault — she has enough operational experience to know the difference between user error and unclear design. In a testing session, she talks through what she is doing naturally, not because she has been told to think aloud, but because she is used to narrating her own process to staff who need to learn it. |
| **How She Approaches New Software** | She looks at the whole screen first, the way you would look at a room you've just walked into. She is reading for landmarks — something she recognizes, something that tells her where she is. She does not read instructional text closely on a first pass; she looks for buttons, icons, and fields. Her first action is almost always the most obvious large interactive element on the screen. If that action does something she did not expect, she uses the back button or its equivalent immediately — she treats back as a reset, not as a navigational tool. She will try a given path twice before she decides it is wrong. If it is still wrong the second time, she does not try a third time; she looks for a completely different approach or, if she is not in a testing context, closes the window. She does not explore menus. She has never in her life opened a settings panel voluntarily. |
| **Under Frustration** | She gets quiet. Not the aggressive quiet of someone building to an outburst, but the efficient quiet of someone who has decided the conversation isn't useful anymore and is redirecting her attention to something that is. In a testing context this manifests as: she stops narrating, her mouse movements become slower and more deliberate, and she begins hovering over things without clicking them — a visible hesitation that precedes either a tentative action or a complete stop. She does not say "I give up." She says something pragmatic: "I'm not sure where to go from here" or "I don't think I'm understanding this right." She will not click randomly. Random clicking is, for her, a sign of panic, and she does not panic — she disengages. If she is at a genuine impasse she will ask, "Am I supposed to be able to do this?" — meaning: is this a feature or is this broken? She has enough experience with broken technology to consider the possibility that the software itself is wrong. |
| **What Delights Her** | Confirmation that something worked. Not congratulatory animations, not sound effects, not a badge — a clean, legible statement that the action was completed. "Your order has been submitted." "Changes saved." She responds strongly to software that remembers what she already told it — pre-populated fields, saved preferences, a form that does not make her re-enter information she entered last week. She appreciates when there is only one obvious thing to do next, and when doing it moves her forward in a way she can perceive. She notices and responds positively to labels that say exactly what they do — a button that says "Save and Continue" is more satisfying to her than one that says "Next," because it tells her what is happening to her data. She is quietly pleased by software that behaves the way she predicted it would. She will not say "this is intuitive" — she will say "oh, okay, yeah" and keep moving, which is the highest endorsement in her register. |
| **What Confuses Her** | Modal dialogs that appear without context — windows that open in front of the thing she was doing without explaining why. Hover states that change the appearance of an element in a way that makes her uncertain whether she clicked it or not. Icon-only navigation — she does not read icons fluently outside the two dozen she has encountered enough times to recognize. Pagination that does not tell her where she is ("Page 3 of 7" is fine; a row of dots is not). Inline editing that activates on a single click in a view she thought was read-only — she will click on something to read it and be alarmed when it becomes a form field. Dropdown menus that require precise hovering to stay open. Any workflow that uses the word "portal." She has encountered enough enterprise portals at work to have a Pavlovian response to the word that is almost entirely negative. |

She does not watch tutorial videos unless she is truly stuck, and when she does watch them she watches at 1.5x speed and pauses at every step to do the action herself before continuing. | **What She Avoids** | Any software that requires her to set something up before she can use it — multi-step onboarding flows, configuration wizards, "tell us about yourself" screens. She will not create an account for something she only needs to do once. She avoids apps that request permissions she does not understand, and she declines notification permissions for most things by reflex. She does not use voice commands on her phone, not because she doesn't know about them but because she tried Siri twice and found the experience embarrassing in a way she has not fully articulated to herself. She does not use anything that requires her to remember a separate username from her email address. She has, at various points, simply stopped using services whose password reset flows confused her rather than engaging with the recovery process. |
| **Mental Model** | Gloria thinks of software in terms of forms and files. Data goes into boxes, gets submitted, goes somewhere, and can be retrieved later. She understands software the way she understands a filing cabinet: there is a place for things, and the interface is the set of drawers and labels that help her find the place. When software behaves outside this model — when an action she takes changes something somewhere she didn't expect, or when a result she wanted appears without her having explicitly requested it — she is briefly disoriented and then deeply suspicious of whether the action was actually saved. She trusts what is on the screen in front of her and does not trust what she cannot see. She does not have a strong concept of "background processes" or "auto-save" — if she did not click a button that said something like "Save" or "Submit," she is not confident the data is anywhere. |

## UX Testing Value

Gloria surfaces friction that experienced users have learned to absorb. When a developer or designer tests their own software, they carry the intention behind every design decision — they know why the modal appears, they know what the icon means, they know that the subtle color change on the tab indicates selection. Gloria carries none of this. She arrives at every screen with only what the screen itself communicates, and the gap between what a screen communicates and what it was designed to communicate is precisely the gap that produces drop-off, support tickets, and abandonment in real-world populations. She is particularly effective at surfacing labeling failures — button text and field labels that are legible as words but ambiguous as instructions — because she reads them the way a first-time user does and then does what they literally say, which is often not what they are intended to mean.

She is also an unusually good detector of confidence failures: moments in a flow where a user does not know whether their action succeeded or not. She will complete an action, pause, and say "So it went through? I don't know if it went through." This kind of uncertainty is common in real users and nearly invisible to developers who know what the system did. Gloria does not assume success. She looks for evidence of it, and if the evidence is not legible — if the confirmation is too small, too briefly displayed, or located somewhere other than where her attention is — she will either repeat the action or stop trusting the software entirely. The pattern is diagnostic: what she is uncertain about is what the software failed to communicate.

What developers learn from watching Gloria use software is mostly about the cost of assumptions. She does not assume that icons mean what they conventionally mean among power users. She does not assume that a greyed-out element is disabled versus loading versus decorative. She does not assume that changes are saved until she sees evidence. She does not assume that navigation she has not yet encountered will follow patterns she already knows. Each of these non-assumptions corresponds to a real population of users who would behave exactly the way she does, and whose confusion would be attributed in analytics to "user error" when the actual source is a design decision that only makes sense if you already know the product. Gloria is good at these tests not in spite of being non-technical, but because of it.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She has a physical notebook — a spiral-bound three-subject in green — that she carries in her work bag and never uses for anything personal. It contains: vendor contact numbers, the names and extension numbers of every district administrator she has called more than twice, her master ordering schedule annotated with the two weeks each year when deliveries are unreliable, and a running list of questions she is saving up for her annual district budget review. There is also, on the inside back cover in her own handwriting, a note that reads "check if the milk order went through" — a reminder she wrote after the first and only time it didn't, eight years ago. She has not needed the reminder since but she has not crossed it out.

- She has developed, over years of working with the district's nutrition reporting portal, a habit of screenshotting confirmation pages before she navigates away from them. She does this automatically, the way someone might jot a phone number on their wrist. She cannot tell you when she started doing it. She stores the screenshots in a folder on her phone called "records" that also contains photos of receipts, her children's immunization cards, and the parking permit number for Marisol's apartment complex. She does not consider this an unusual practice. It would not occur to her that most people do not do this.

- She approaches new software with the posture of someone who has been given a kitchen tool she has not used before: she will look at it for a moment, test the obvious part, and then make a practical judgment about whether it is faster to figure it out herself or to ask someone who already knows. This ratio shifts with time pressure. Before nine in the morning, she will ask someone. After noon, when the rush is over, she will figure it out herself and feel quietly satisfied about it. She has a specific expression when she solves something on her own — a small, closed-mouth nod, directed at the screen — that she is entirely unaware of.

- She has strong opinions about font size that she does not know are opinions about font size. She will describe software as "hard to read" or "cluttered" when what she means is that the type is small and the touch targets are close together. She has the accessibility settings on her iPhone set to slightly larger text — she found this setting two years ago when she accidentally activated it and then, upon realizing the screen was easier to read, decided to leave it. She considers this a personal preference rather than a configuration she made, the way she considers the car seat position a personal preference rather than a setting she adjusted.

- She will complete a testing task even when she is clearly on the wrong path, not out of stubbornness but because she genuinely is not always sure she is on the wrong path. She will navigate four levels deep into an incorrect section of an interface, complete a form she was not supposed to complete, and submit it before saying "I don't think that was right, was it?" — not because she ignored her uncertainty but because she did not trust the uncertainty enough to stop. This is not a personal failing. It is a specific and important signal: when the software provided no strong negative feedback indicating she was off-course, she read ambiguous forward momentum as permission to continue. Developers who watch her do this and feel exasperated have misunderstood the test. The software guided her there.

- She makes a remark, at some point in almost every testing session, comparing what she is using to something from her work life. The vendor portal. The budget system. The attendance tracking app the district rolled out last spring. These comparisons are not complaints — they are her way of locating herself. She is a person who understands new things in terms of things she already knows, and the comparisons she reaches for reveal exactly which mental models she is applying and why certain design patterns land well with her and others do not. A researcher who writes these comparisons down and maps them back to the design decisions in question will learn more from them than from any other data point in the session.
