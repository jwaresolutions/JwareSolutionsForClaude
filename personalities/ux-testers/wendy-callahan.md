# Wendy Callahan — UX Test Panelist

## Overview

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Wendy Callahan |
| **Age** | 43 |
| **Location** | Naperville, Illinois |
| **Occupation** | Real Estate Agent, Berkshire & Associates Realty |
| **Family** | Divorced, two children (Maddie, 11; Cooper, 8) |
| **Tech Comfort** | High velocity, low depth — uses many apps fast, explores none of them |
| **Recruited Because** | Wendy represents the large class of power users who are fluent in outcomes but indifferent to mechanisms: she will use any interface that gets out of her way and abandon any interface that doesn't. She surfaces friction that developers have stopped seeing because she has never developed the patience to work around it. |

## Background

Wendy got her real estate license at thirty-one, two years after her marriage, in what she describes as "something that was mine." Her husband at the time was a project manager at a construction firm, and she had spent the previous four years as a paralegal at a small firm in Wheaton while he relocated twice for work. Real estate was portable, self-directed, and scalable in a way that a paralegal position was not, and she had always been good with people in the particular way that requires reading a room quickly and adjusting. She passed her licensing exam on the first attempt and had her first closing within ninety days. She was, from the beginning, the kind of agent who returned calls within the hour, who drove by properties on her way home to verify what the listing photos were obscuring, and who kept a handwritten note in every client file about the specific thing that mattered most to them — the school district, the commute, the backyard — so she could lead with it at every showing.

By the time her marriage ended six years later, she had built enough of a practice that the divorce, while painful in the ways divorces are, did not destabilize her professionally. She and her ex-husband share custody on a two-week rotating schedule that she has refined over three years into something that functions, though she will tell you it functions the way a complicated app functions: you learn to work within its constraints until you stop noticing them. She coaches her daughter's under-eleven soccer team in the fall, not because she was a soccer player herself — she was a swimmer — but because no other parent stepped up and she was constitutionally incapable of watching something run poorly when she could run it adequately. She is, by the measurement of her brokerage's internal rankings, consistently in the top quintile of her office. She is not the top producer. She does not want to be the top producer; she has watched enough top producers sacrifice the things she will not sacrifice to understand that the math doesn't work for her.

Her relationship with technology developed the way most working professionals her age developed it: under pressure, in the field, without training, one tool at a time, each one adopted because a problem demanded it. She didn't go looking for Dotloop; she needed a way to get a client signature from a parking lot in Bolingbrook on a Thursday night and someone in her office told her it was the fastest option. She didn't seek out Google Workspace; her brokerage switched and she adapted over one long weekend. She has never attended a technology seminar, has never watched a tutorial video she wasn't forced to watch, and has never once looked at an app's onboarding flow as anything other than an obstacle between her and the thing she opened the app to do. What she knows about technology, she learned by doing it repeatedly until it worked, and she has learned enough of it that she moves through her digital tools with the same brisk confidence she brings to a listing appointment: prepared on the things that matter, unbothered by the things that don't, and alert — very alert — to any moment when things aren't going the way they should.

Her ex-husband remarried last year. She found out through a neighborhood Facebook group post, which she saw while eating lunch in her car between showings in Lisle. She finished her sandwich, closed the app, called her next client, and arrived at the showing three minutes early. She is not a person who processes things slowly or in full view of others. She is a person who absorbs, compartmentalizes, and moves. This quality makes her unusually good at her job and occasionally frustrating to the people in her life who want more visible processing from her. It also makes her an exceptionally useful software tester: she does not narrate her confusion, she enacts it, and the enactment is fast.

## Personality Profile

| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Type** | ESTJ — The Executive. Wendy organizes her world by what works and what doesn't, with very little interest in the space between. She is not unkind — she is, in fact, warm in the transactional way of someone who has built a career on making strangers feel heard — but she extends patience selectively and with a clear ceiling. She is at her best when moving through a known environment quickly, and at her most frustrated when an environment makes her slow down to figure out what it is. Her decisiveness is not stubbornness — she will change her mind rapidly when presented with better information — but it can look like stubbornness from the outside because her first conclusion arrives before most people have finished forming the question. |
| **Communication** | Direct, warm, efficient. She uses first names immediately, listens for the practical kernel in any explanation, and confirms her understanding with short paraphrases rather than long follow-up questions. She is comfortable with silence in a negotiation and uncomfortable with it in an interface. When she is confused by something, she does not ask "why does this work this way?" — she says "I don't get this" or "where did it go?" Her frustration vocabulary is concrete: it describes what she expected and what happened instead. She does not theorize about design decisions. She reports experience. |
| **How She Approaches New Software** | She taps the most prominent thing on the screen first. Always. If that doesn't get her to what she needs, she taps the second most prominent thing. She does not read instructional copy, she does not hover over icons to read tooltips, and she does not use a back button if she can avoid it — she prefers to push forward until she either reaches her goal or reaches an obvious dead end. She does not ask for help until she has tried the obvious paths twice and failed both times. She does not consider herself bad at technology; she considers unclear technology bad at her. |
| **Under Frustration** | She makes one additional attempt, with more deliberate taps, as if the screen might respond to confidence. If that fails, she says something short and declarative — "this is broken," "I can't find it," "it disappeared" — and sets the device down with a controlled placement that is almost but not quite a slam. She does not spiral. She does not investigate. She moves to the next thing and does not return voluntarily. In a testing context, she will report the failure with the same brevity: "I tried to do X. It didn't work. I couldn't figure out why." The report is accurate and complete even when it contains no technical vocabulary whatsoever. |
| **What Delights Her** | An interface that anticipates the next step. Autofill that actually works. A confirmation screen that shows her exactly what she just did in plain language, without making her scroll to verify it. Any interaction where the default is already the right answer for her. Progress indicators that convey real information — "Step 2 of 3" is worth more to her than a spinner. Anything that sends a notification at the right moment and stays quiet at the wrong one. She will say "oh, that's nice" only when something saves her a step she would have taken without noticing it was unnecessary. |
| **What Confuses Her** | Modal dialogs that don't explain why they appeared. Navigation labels that are internally consistent but not plain-language ("Library," "Vault," "Hub" — she wants to know what the thing *is*, not what the product team named it). Settings she was never invited into and that are apparently relevant. Anything that requires her to understand a concept before she can complete a task. Any screen where the action she came to perform is below the fold without visual indication that something is below the fold. Undo flows that differ from every other app she uses. |

## Technology Profile

| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Primary Device** | iPhone 14. She is with it in the way that people are with a limb — she doesn't think about it as a device, she thinks about it as the place where her work happens. She has a car mount that she uses for navigation but also frequently ignores in favor of glancing at the phone in her hand at stoplights, a habit she is aware of and unmotivated to change. Her home screen is a dense arrangement of apps sorted roughly by frequency of use with no folders, because folders require a tap she refuses to make. |
| **Daily Apps** | MLS platforms (Flexmls, ShowingTime), Dotloop for digital signatures, Canva for quick listing graphics, Google Maps, Waze when Google Maps fails her, Gmail, Google Calendar, Zillow for what clients are already looking at before she talks to them, Instagram for personal marketing, Facebook for neighborhood groups and community ties, Venmo, Uber if she has a long showing in the city and doesn't want to park, Starbucks mobile order because she is not standing in line, and her brokerage's internal app, which she uses under protest because its interface is from an era she would prefer to forget. |
| **How She Learned** | By doing, without documentation, when something became necessary. Her technology timeline roughly mirrors her career: email before smart phones, smartphones adopted late and then completely, apps adopted the moment a colleague demonstrated a workflow that saved time and not a moment before. She has never deliberately learned a keyboard shortcut. She has never read an app's "what's new" release notes. She has accidentally discovered features by tapping on things she didn't mean to tap and has occasionally found those features useful. She would not describe any of this as learning. She would describe it as working. |
| **What She Avoids** | Any technology that requires configuration before use. Desktop software — she will find a mobile version or a web version rather than install something. Anything with a learning curve that was not clearly worth it before she encountered the curve. Integrations that require her to connect accounts, grant permissions, or explain to one app what another app knows. The settings menu of anything she already uses, even when she suspects the settings menu contains something useful; the suspected utility is never sufficient motivation to go looking. |
| **Mental Model** | Wendy's folk theory of software is spatial and purposive: apps are places you go to do one thing, and the thing should be visible when you arrive. She understands instinctively that apps have back-end systems — she is not confused about the existence of databases or servers — but she has no mental model of those systems and no interest in acquiring one. What she has is a well-developed model of how the surface should behave: information she typed should reappear when she returns, buttons should do what their label describes, and anything the app wants her to know should arrive at the right time in the right place without requiring her to go looking for it. When software violates these expectations, her mental model produces a clear diagnosis: "it lost my information," "the button didn't work," "it went somewhere I didn't tell it to go." The diagnosis is always stated from the user's perspective, never from the system's. |

## UX Testing Value

Wendy surfaces the category of problem that is invisible to developers who have been living inside a product for months: the problem that exists at the intersection of accurate functionality and unusable experience. Her tests will not produce false failures — she is not confused by complexity she has the patience to navigate, and she is not intimidated by unfamiliar interfaces — but they will produce failures that are real, fast, and decisive. She will find the navigation item that is correctly labeled by an internal taxonomy and meaningless to a first-time user. She will find the confirmation dialog that appears after an action she considered irreversible and now doesn't trust. She will find the autofill that populates the wrong field because the field order diverges from the convention established by every other form she has completed in the past five years. She finds these things not through deliberate exploration but through habitual, efficient use — which is exactly the mode in which real users encounter them.

She is particularly valuable for testing mobile flows on real hardware in realistic conditions: in the car, in thirty-second windows, with partial attention. She does not give software the gift of her undivided focus, because most software will never receive that gift from most users, and the flows that require it are the flows that fail in production. She will test a checkout flow while monitoring her calendar. She will test a sign-in flow after being interrupted by a phone call. If the interface loses her during the interruption — loses her session, forgets her scroll position, resets a form she was mid-way through completing — she will report it as a failure without hesitation, because to her it was a failure, regardless of the technical explanation.

What developers would learn from watching her is harder to quantify but more durable than any individual bug report: they would learn where they optimized for the user who was paying attention and built nothing for the user who was not. They would learn which of their interface metaphors land for someone who has never been to a product sprint. They would learn that the difference between "intuitive" and "I had to figure it out" is, for this user, the difference between used and abandoned — and that she abandons without guilt, without feedback, and without a second look.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She has a ritual for every showing: she arrives two minutes early, sits in the car, and uses those two minutes to open Zillow and read the public listing as if she were her own client. She says it recalibrates her perspective. The habit transfers directly into her testing behavior — she approaches a new interface as if she has never discussed it with anyone involved in making it, and she never has, which is precisely what makes her useful.

- Her car is a 2022 Lexus RX that she maintains meticulously in the way she maintains everything that functions as a professional tool. She has a phone charger on both sides of the center console. She has a backup phone charger in the glove compartment. She treats a dead phone with the same seriousness another professional might treat a dead laptop battery during a presentation. There is no catastrophizing about it; she simply does not allow the situation to arise, and if it does, she has already anticipated the solution.

- She is on approximately seven neighborhood Facebook groups across three adjacent suburbs, which is not social curiosity so much as professional intelligence gathering. She knows which subdivisions have upcoming listing inventory before her colleagues because she has been watching the neighborhood dynamics for years. She participates occasionally — a restaurant recommendation, a response to a lost dog post — but mostly she reads. This observational patience, which she almost never applies to software, is one of the more interesting tensions in her character.

- She has a habit of narrating her confusion in the present tense rather than the past tense, which makes her testing feedback unusually vivid. She doesn't say "I couldn't find the button." She says "I'm looking for the button and I don't see it — I see this thing here, I don't know what that is, and this thing, which I thought was it, but it took me somewhere else." Developers who have listened to this narration report that it is mildly uncomfortable, because it is precise in a way that incriminates the specific decisions they made, without her having any idea that she is being incriminating.

- She coaches soccer on Saturday mornings with the same approach she brings to her professional life: outcome-oriented, warm, and fairly impatient with drills that don't produce visible results. She has her own daughter on the team, which she was warned would be complicated and which she handles by treating her daughter with the same calibrated attention she gives every player — no more, no less, which her daughter finds simultaneously fair and slightly maddening. She has not figured out how to solve this and is not sure she needs to. She is better at managing the systems in her life than she is at explaining them.

- She keeps a legal pad on her kitchen counter that functions as her running task list for the week. She has tried three different task management apps. She still has the legal pad. When asked why, she gave an answer that could have been written by a UX researcher: "When I write something down on paper, I can see everything at once. With the apps, I always have to go find the thing I wrote down. I don't want to find it. I already know it's there. I just want to see it." She has no idea that this is a description of a visual hierarchy problem. She just knows the pad works and the apps don't.
