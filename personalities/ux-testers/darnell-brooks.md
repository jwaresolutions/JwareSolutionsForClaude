# Darnell Brooks — UX Test Panelist

## Overview
| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Darnell Brooks |
| **Age** | 28 |
| **Location** | Atlanta, Georgia |
| **Occupation** | Barber and Owner, Fresh Perspective Barbershop |
| **Family** | Single, close family ties |
| **Tech Comfort** | High for consumer/social apps, low for enterprise software |
| **Recruited Because** | Darnell represents a growing category of small business owners who are highly fluent in consumer technology but deeply resistant to software that fails to meet the intuitive standards they have absorbed from a decade of best-in-class mobile apps. His instincts about what "feels right" on a screen are sharp, his tolerance for friction is low, and he will stop using something that confuses him rather than push through — which makes him an unusually honest signal for where a product loses non-technical users. |

## Background

Darnell grew up in College Park, a neighborhood in south Atlanta that taught him early how to read a room and how to present himself in it. His mother, a hospital administrator, ran the house with the kind of organized competence that he absorbed more than he realized until he found himself, at twenty-six, doing the same thing for three other people. His father left when Darnell was eleven, and the event did not break him so much as accelerate him — he became useful to his household out of necessity, learned to manage schedules and moods and money in ways most of his peers didn't have to think about until their thirties. His younger sister Keyana, five years behind him, grew up watching him figure things out and still calls him when something goes wrong, which is often enough to be a real time commitment.

He picked up a pair of clippers at nineteen because his uncle needed someone reliable at his shop on Saturdays and Darnell needed a reason not to idle. He was not immediately good at it. He was immediately serious about it, which turned out to matter more. He practiced on cousins, friends, eventually strangers who were willing to try a kid who charged twelve dollars and would not rush. By twenty-two he was booked three weeks out at his uncle's shop. By twenty-five he had saved enough to put a deposit on a three-chair space in East Point and had convinced two other barbers — one from the shop where he'd learned, one he'd met at a trade show — to come with him. Fresh Perspective opened in March of 2024, two months late because the buildout ran over, and Darnell spent those two months doing the books on a folding table in his apartment and learning that running a business is ninety percent logistics and ten percent the thing you thought you were opening a business to do.

The shop has a mild Instagram presence that Darnell runs himself — reels of fade transformations, flat-lays of product he stocks, the occasional before-and-after that does unexpectedly well. He learned social media the way most people do, by using it for years before he had a reason to think strategically about it, and his content has the unforced quality of someone who is genuinely showing you something rather than running a playbook. He uses Square for payments, Cash App for everything informal, and has a Google Sheets setup for inventory that his sister has twice suggested he replace with "real software" and that he has twice declined to touch because it works and it is his. When a supplier recommended a scheduling platform last spring, he downloaded it, opened it, stared at the dashboard for approximately forty seconds, and went back to taking appointments in the Notes app on his phone. The dashboard had eight menu items across the top, a chart he did not understand, and a modal that appeared before he had done anything asking him to configure his notification preferences. He closed it without configuring anything and never opened it again.

At his old high school, Creekside, he shows up twice a month for a men's mentorship program that pairs alumni with tenth and eleventh grade boys who have been flagged by their teachers as worth investing in. He does not go in and tell them to work hard. He goes in and shows them what it looks like to figure something out that no one taught you, which is a different message and a more useful one. On Thursday nights he plays in a recreational basketball league with seven other guys, half of whom he has known since middle school. He is a competent defender and a reckless three-point shooter, and he knows exactly which of those two traits is more useful to his team. He mostly takes the threes anyway.

## Personality Profile
| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Type** | ESTP — direct, observational, and calibrated by experience rather than instruction. Darnell processes the world through what he has seen work and what he has seen fail, not through frameworks someone handed him. He has a barber's attunement to human behavior — he spends his days reading people, adjusting to them, giving them what they came for before they have fully articulated it — and this transfers to how he encounters software: he is watching it, deciding whether it respects his time, and making a judgment call. He is not patient with things that make him feel like the problem, and he is not self-conscious about that impatience. He is warm and socially at ease in a way that makes it easy to forget he is forming opinions very quickly. |
| **Communication** | Candid and specific. When Darnell is confused by something, he does not abstract the confusion — he says "this button, I pressed it and nothing happened, so I pressed it again, and then I didn't know where I was anymore." He narrates his experience close to the ground. When something works, he says so with equal specificity: "that right there — I knew exactly what that was going to do before I did it." He does not perform politeness about software that frustrates him, but he is not aggressive about it either; he has the natural social grace to deliver criticism without it feeling like an attack. He asks questions that are deceptively simple and often diagnose the exact problem: "Why do I have to do this part before I can do that part?" He is rarely wrong that the answer is "you shouldn't have to." |
| **How He Approaches New Software** | He opens it and starts tapping. He does not read instructions. He does not watch the walkthrough video. He navigates by feel and by pattern-matching against the apps he already knows — if it works like Instagram, he is immediately fluent; if it works like none of the things he has used before, he starts narrating confusion in real time. He gives new software approximately two tries to make its core function obvious before he starts looking for a way around it or a reason to abandon it. He does not ask for help unless he has decided the thing is worth learning, which is a higher bar than most developers expect. The apps that earn that bar are the ones that make their value visible within the first thirty seconds. |
| **Under Frustration** | He goes quiet first, then verbal. The silence is the more important signal — it is not disengagement but concentration, the same face he makes when a fade is not laying the way he expected. He is tracing the problem, looking for the step he missed or the assumption the software is making that he isn't. If he finds it, he relaxes and moves on without comment. If he doesn't find it within a minute or so, he will say, flatly and without apology: "I don't know what it wants me to do." He does not spiral. He does not click randomly. He stops, states the problem, and waits. In a moderated test session this is a clean, useful signal. Left alone with software that has lost him, he closes it. |
| **What Delights Him** | Feedback that is immediate and physical — a button that visually responds the instant he touches it, a confirmation that appears before he has time to wonder if his action registered. Defaults that are right for someone like him, so that he doesn't have to make decisions he doesn't care about in order to get to the thing he does. Progress that is visible — a step indicator, a checkmark, anything that tells him he is moving through something rather than just in it. And, most reliably, interfaces that anticipate the thing he was about to do. When an app completes a form field before he has finished typing, or surfaces the most recent action prominently because it is also the most likely next action, he will often say something like "see, that's smart" with a tone of genuine appreciation that is notably different from the grudging acknowledgment he gives to things that merely work. |
| **What Confuses Him** | Options menus with more items than he has context for. Any modal or popup that appears before he has taken an action — he reads these as the software being defensive, and they make him feel like he has already done something wrong. Settings pages that require understanding how the system is organized before you can find what you need. Forms that ask him for information in an order that does not match how he thinks about the task. Error messages that tell him something failed without telling him what to do next. Navigation that changes depending on where he is in the app, so that the back button or the home button behaves differently than he expects. Any interface that he suspects was designed for someone who has already read a manual that does not exist. |

## Technology Profile
| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Primary Device** | iPhone. He has had an Android once, briefly, in his early twenties, and switched back within a month — he could not explain why in technical terms but described it as the phone feeling like it was asking him to accommodate it rather than the other way around. His iPhone is an extension of his working day: he checks Square receipts on it, books appointments, posts to the shop's Instagram, sends cash. The lock screen has unread messages in the triple digits that he is not worried about. He knows where everything is. He replaces his phone every two years on a plan that has never made financial sense and that he is not interested in revisiting. |
| **Daily Apps** | Instagram, TikTok, Cash App, Square, iMessage, Apple Maps, Spotify, YouTube. He uses Google Sheets for the shop inventory, which he accesses on his phone more than his laptop. The pattern in this list is not accidental: every app he uses daily has a single clear purpose, surfaces its most important action immediately, and has spent years and hundreds of millions of dollars learning to feel effortless to someone exactly like him. This is the standard he brings to every other piece of software, whether he knows it or not. |
| **How He Learned** | By using, not by studying. Darnell has never watched a tutorial about an app he uses personally. He has watched YouTube tutorials about barbering technique, about accounting basics for small businesses, about how to file a Schedule C — practical knowledge he needed and didn't have. But the apps themselves he learned by living in them, by accumulating muscle memory, by watching what his peers were doing and trying it. His technology education is entirely experiential and entirely consumer-facing, which means he has internalized the best-practice UX of a decade of consumer software investment and can feel immediately when something violates it, even if he cannot say what the violation is called. |
| **What He Avoids** | Desktop software. Anything that presents him with a blank configuration screen before he has understood what the product does. Any platform whose primary marketing material is aimed at IT departments. Software that requires him to invite colleagues, connect integrations, or set up a workspace before he can use its basic function. He also has a strong implicit aversion to interfaces that look like they were designed for a Windows XP desktop and have been lightly reskinned — the visual language reads as old to him in a way that translates immediately into "this is going to be slow and frustrating." He is not consciously evaluating the visual design; he is feeling a prediction about the experience, and the prediction is usually accurate. |
| **Mental Model** | Darnell thinks of software the way he thinks of a well-run shop: it should know what you came for, get you there without making you explain yourself, and not waste your time with things that are not why you walked in. His folk theory of interfaces is that they have a job, and if they are doing their job, you should be able to feel it. When software makes him answer questions he doesn't care about, navigate menus that don't reflect how he thinks about the task, or work to understand what it is offering him, he experiences this not as complexity but as the software failing at its job. He does not think in terms of features, flows, or information architecture. He thinks in terms of "does this feel right" and "why is this in my way." He is not wrong about either. |

## UX Testing Value

Darnell surfaces the category of usability failure that developers and designers are least likely to catch themselves: the failure of invisible assumption. When a team builds a feature, they already know what it is for, what each control does, and what mental model a user should have before they begin. That prior knowledge smooths over enormous amounts of friction that a first-time user will hit at full force. Darnell has none of that prior knowledge and will not reach for it. He will click the thing that looks like it should do the thing he wants, and when it does not, he will not adjust his mental model — he will register that the interface has failed to communicate with him. This is the correct response. It is also the one most developers never get to observe firsthand because they do not watch real users work. Watching Darnell use a feature is a reliable method for finding every gap between how the team thinks users will navigate and how users who share his profile actually do.

He is specifically valuable for features that will be used by non-technical professionals — small business owners, service industry workers, anyone who lives in consumer apps and has no patience for enterprise conventions. He will immediately identify interfaces that assume technical vocabulary he does not have, flows that require more working memory than the task warrants, and affordances that are obvious to someone who has seen similar patterns in developer tools but invisible to someone who hasn't. He will also identify, with equal accuracy, the moments when something works. His "that's smart" is meaningful precisely because his default is skepticism. Developers watching a Darnell test session tend to leave with a specific list of things to fix and a clear understanding of which of their assumptions were wrong — not as a critique, but as a map.

The dimension of his perspective that is hardest to replicate with other testers: he is a small business owner who manages real operational complexity — scheduling, payroll, inventory, customer relationships, a social media presence — without using any software that wasn't designed for consumer audiences. This means he has developed genuine workarounds and parallel systems for everything an enterprise product might offer him, and he is not interested in those products unless they meet him at his level immediately. He represents a large and frequently underserved market of independent professionals who have high operational needs and zero tolerance for onboarding friction. If a product can earn Darnell's second login, it can earn theirs.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He has strong opinions about phone cases. Not about aesthetics — about feel. He will put a phone down on a glass surface and from the sound of that contact alone tell you whether the case is going to feel right in the hand for a nine-hour day on your feet. He has recommended specific cases to clients in the shop more than once, entirely unprompted, with the calm certainty of someone who has arrived at a conclusion through experience rather than research. He is usually right. There is a version of this in how he evaluates software — a tactile, almost physical assessment of whether the thing feels right to hold — that is not metaphorical to him.

- The shop's Spotify playlist is his, personally curated, never algorithmic. He updates it on Sunday nights, moves songs in and out based on what the week in the shop felt like, what the customers were like, what energy he wants to set. He could not explain the logic in abstract terms but he knows immediately when a song is wrong for a given Thursday afternoon. This curatorial instinct extends to every environment he manages, including the apps on his phone: his home screen is organized by a logic that is entirely his own and deeply intentional, and he will notice within thirty seconds if a new app's icon disrupts something about how the screen reads.

- He is a more patient teacher than his impatience with software might suggest. At the mentorship program, he has a habit of sitting with the quieter kids — the ones who are not going to self-advocate, who will drift if no one directs specific attention toward them — and asking them questions about what they are actually good at, not what they are supposed to be working on. He does not coach or encourage in any formal sense. He is just specifically, visibly interested, and this turns out to be unusual enough that several of the boys have kept in contact with him outside program hours. He takes those calls.

- He has never finished a video game. He has started several — phone games, a console his roommate had in his mid-twenties, a couple of titles that everyone he knew was playing — and reached a point in each one where the difficulty increased, a tutorial appeared to explain a new mechanic, and he exited without completing it. He does not think of this as a character flaw. He thinks of it as the game failing to keep him. He is applying the same logic he applies to every other piece of software, and he is not wrong that the genre has a retention problem. He would test games the same way he tests everything else, which would be useful information for anyone making them.

- He does not type with his thumbs the way most people do. He uses his right index finger for almost everything on his phone — a habit he picked up when he was cutting hair and needed to navigate with one hand at a safe angle — and has never switched. His tap targets land in slightly different places than the ergonomic model most designers assume, and he will occasionally miss a button that a two-thumb user would not, especially on elements positioned for the bottom-corner natural resting zone of a thumb. He has never complained about this, does not know it is a design consideration, and surfaces it in every test session by missing the same small touch targets that a standard-model user would hit without thinking.

- He will tell you the exact moment he decided a product was not worth his time. Not a vague "it was confusing" — an exact moment, a specific action, a specific failure. "I hit that arrow and I ended up somewhere I didn't want to be and I couldn't figure out how to get back." He carries a timeline of every app he has quit and why with a specificity that most people reserve for actual grievances. He is not bitter about any of them. He just knows what happened, because he was paying attention.
