# Tomás Herrera — UX Test Panelist

## Overview
| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Tomás Herrera |
| **Age** | 36 |
| **Location** | Albuquerque, New Mexico |
| **Occupation** | Owner-Operator, Long-Haul Truck Driver |
| **Family** | Married; one daughter, age 4; wife is a dental hygienist |
| **Tech Comfort** | Practical and context-bound — strong with mobile and tablet apps that serve an immediate, specific purpose; desktop software is largely foreign territory; all technology interactions happen in constrained physical environments |
| **Recruited Because** | Tomás represents a class of professional user who depends on software under conditions developers rarely design for: moving vehicles, direct sunlight, one free hand, decision urgency. He surfaces the gap between interfaces that work in a testing lab and interfaces that work in the world. |

## Background

Tomás grew up in Las Cruces watching his father back a Peterbilt into tight loading docks with a calm that looked, to a seven-year-old, like a form of magic. His father, Ernesto, drove for a regional produce carrier for thirty years, came home smelling like diesel and road grime, and was, by Tomás's accounting, one of the most competent people he ever knew. Tomás did not romanticize the work — he watched his father miss school plays and birthday dinners and come home exhausted in a way that was not the tiredness of a man who had been sitting — but he understood from an early age that trucking was a skilled trade with real consequences, and that the men who did it well had a kind of professional authority that did not require anyone's permission. He got his CDL at twenty-two, the week after his father retired, and felt this timing as something he couldn't quite name.

He drove for a carrier out of El Paso for nine years, running general freight on the I-25/I-10 corridor, then longer hauls up through Arizona and Nevada when he transferred to a larger outfit based in Albuquerque. He married Gabriela in his late twenties. She was finishing her hygiene program at UNM; he was saving aggressively and working out the math on what it would cost to buy his own rig. The calculation took two years of spreadsheets built on his phone, of rate comparisons on load boards, of conversations with owner-operators at truck stops who had done it and with ones who hadn't made it. He bought a 2017 Kenworth T680 five years ago — used, with 280,000 miles on it, a financing arrangement he understood in detail, and a maintenance reserve he funded before he spent a dollar on anything else. He runs Southwest to Pacific Northwest routes primarily: Albuquerque to Phoenix to Portland, variations on that corridor. He does not work for anyone. He pays himself, his fuel, his insurance, his truck payments, and the quarterly taxes he files himself using an app and, when the quarter is complicated, a tax preparer in Albuquerque he has used for four years.

The business of running a truck is more administrative than most people outside the industry understand. Tomás manages his own dispatch — deciding which loads to take off the board, negotiating rates when the posted rate is wrong, building routes that account for fuel price differentials across states, hours-of-service regulations, weather, and the specific quirks of the loading docks at his regular receivers. His ELD device records his hours automatically, but the judgment calls — when to push to make a delivery window, when to stop short and rest, when to deadhead a stretch to get into a better load market — are his. He thinks in numbers and logistics the way a project manager thinks in timelines and dependencies, though he would not use either of those terms. He knows where the Pilot Flying J stations are on his routes and which ones have the best diesel prices on Tuesday afternoons. He knows which shippers run late and build that into his schedule. He has a mental model of his business that is accurate, granular, and entirely informal.

Gabriela is home with Marisol during the weeks, manages pickup and dropoff from daycare, and does a job that Tomás is acutely aware is harder in some ways than his own. He coaches Little League in the spring when his schedule permits — he has missed games, felt the specific weight of that, and made peace with it in a way that is not quite acceptance but is functional. He grew up Catholic, coaches with the same matter-of-fact seriousness his father brought to everything, and has a way with eight-year-olds that is direct enough to be heard and warm enough to be trusted. He is not a man who performs emotion easily or often, but watch him at the field and the warmth is not hidden.

## Personality Profile
| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Type** | ISTP — The same type as someone who became a systems engineer through years of fixing things that other people broke, except Tomás's systems are physical, time-critical, and governed by federal regulation rather than software architecture. He is oriented toward immediate, concrete problems. He does not theorize about why something doesn't work; he tries the thing that seems most likely to work and adjusts from there. He is not incurious — he will spend an hour at a truck stop researching why a fuel surcharge formula changed — but his curiosity is always anchored to a practical question. Abstract interface philosophy would bore him to paralysis; show him a task and he'll show you whether the interface survives contact with a real human who has other things to think about. |
| **Communication** | Direct and efficient. He doesn't soften frustration with qualifications — when something doesn't work, he says it doesn't work, and he usually says it plainly enough that you know exactly what doesn't work and where he gave up. He is not a complainer by temperament; his feedback is observational rather than aggrieved. "I couldn't find it" is his most common expression of a navigation failure. "I don't know what this is asking me" is how he describes ambiguous form fields. He does not use UX vocabulary — he will not say "affordance" or "information hierarchy" — but his descriptions of what went wrong are often more useful than those terms, because they are grounded in what he was actually trying to do and where the task broke down. He says "yeah, that makes sense" when something works and means it. |
| **How He Approaches New Software** | He starts by trying to do the thing he was told the software does. He does not read instructions, onboarding screens, or tooltips before attempting the primary action. If he can find it fast, he proceeds. If he cannot find it within about thirty seconds of looking, he taps around the screen looking for something that looks interactive and relevant. If that fails, he stops and looks at the screen more carefully, which is not reading so much as scanning for something that could be a button or a navigation element. He will not explore the app's settings or secondary menus to understand the app's structure — he is not trying to understand the app, he is trying to complete a task. If a second attempt also fails, he sets the device down and does not pick it back up unless someone prompts him. He does not ask for help as a first move; he exhausts his own attempts first. This is not pride exactly — it is the same instinct that makes him try to diagnose a mechanical sound in the cab before he calls his mechanic. He wants to know what he thinks before he hears someone else's explanation. |
| **Under Frustration** | He goes quiet and still in a way that is distinct from concentration. When he is concentrating, his gestures are deliberate; when he is frustrated, they stop. He will set the tablet flat on the table, sit back, and look at it with an expression that is not quite a frown but is not neutral. He does not vocalize frustration as dramatic commentary — he does not say "this is ridiculous" or "why is this so hard" — but he will say, quietly and usually to no one in particular, "I don't see where to go from here." In a testing session, this is the moment to watch: it is the clearest signal that the interface has broken the task's navigational contract and left him without a next move. If the session facilitator says nothing, he will try one more thing and then stop. He does not experience the failure as personal inadequacy — he experiences it as the app's failure to communicate — but he does not belabor the analysis. He moves on with a patience that reads as either equanimity or exhaustion depending on the hour. |
| **What Delights Him** | Functionality that anticipates the physical context he's using it in. Large tap targets. Actions that complete in one or two steps from the main screen. Confirmation states that are visible and obvious — a button that changes color, a checkmark, something that clearly says it worked without him having to look for evidence that it did. He responds well to apps that remember what he did last time and put it front and center, because his usage patterns are consistent and he finds it genuinely satisfying when software behaves as though it knows that. He appreciates when an app tells him plainly what it needs from him — a specific format, a required field — before he submits rather than after. He has a specific and affectionate response to apps that work the same way on every screen, where the nav is always in the same place and the primary action is always the most visually prominent thing on the page. He does not call this good design. He says it "makes sense." |
| **What Confuses Him** | Modals and interstitials that interrupt a task he was mid-way through. Menus that are organized by the software's internal logic rather than by what he's trying to accomplish — he navigates by goal, not by feature category. Buttons that look like text and text that looks like it might be a button. Forms that validate on blur rather than on submit, which he encounters as mysterious red borders appearing before he understands what he did wrong. Progress indicators that show activity but give no signal of completion — spinners with no end state leave him uncertain whether to wait or try again, and in the cab, waiting is a variable he does not always have. Swipe gestures with no visible affordance, features that require two hands, anything that demands precise tap accuracy on a target smaller than roughly a thumb's width. He will miss small interface elements entirely and not realize he has missed them, which is a data point in itself. |

## Technology Profile
| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Primary Device** | A Samsung Galaxy Tab A mounted in a RAM mount to the right of his steering column. This is his primary computing environment for everything that happens while he is working. At home he uses his phone — a mid-range Android — for texting, looking things up, and managing household logistics. He does not own a laptop. The family has a Chromebook that Gabriela uses for work and that Tomás has used roughly three times, each time with the slightly uncertain posture of someone operating equipment they haven't been trained on. His tablet UI literacy is genuine; his desktop UI literacy is essentially zero. |
| **Daily Apps** | The ELD app from his mandate-compliant logging device, which he interacts with constantly and has firm opinions about. A load board app — he has used two different ones over the years and switched because the second one showed him current rates on the main screen without requiring a search. GasBuddy or a trucking-specific fuel price app updated for his current corridor. Google Maps as a fallback, though he prefers a trucking-specific GPS app that knows bridge heights and weight restrictions. The Notes app on his phone for rates, contact numbers, and delivery instructions he copies from broker emails. QuickBooks Self-Employed for invoicing and expense tracking, which he uses correctly and without enthusiasm — it works, it files the numbers, he does not want to think about it more than he has to. He uses weather radar apps with a specific fluency because weather has direct consequences for his schedule and his safety, and he can read a radar loop faster than most people. |
| **How He Learned** | He learned technology the way he learned everything: by needing to do something specific and figuring out the tool that did it. His ELD device was mandated by federal regulation in 2017, so he learned it because he had no choice, and he learned it by driving with it until he understood it, not by reading the manual. He learned the load board apps by watching an owner-operator he knew from the Las Cruces days use one on a phone at a scale house, then downloading the same app and using it himself. He learned QuickBooks because his previous system — a spreadsheet on his phone and a shoebox of receipts for his tax preparer — stopped scaling when his invoicing volume increased. His tax preparer recommended it. He watched a YouTube tutorial once, found it 40% applicable to his actual situation, and figured out the rest by clicking through the interface until it made sense. This is his standard technology learning method. It works well for apps with clear primary actions and breaks down entirely on apps where the learning must precede the doing. |
| **What He Avoids** | Desktop software, full stop — not out of bias but out of genuine unfamiliarity, as if someone handed him an aircraft instrument panel and asked him to file taxes on it. Multi-step account creation flows that require email verification before he can try the product, because he often encounters new tools at a truck stop where he has forty-five minutes and wants to know immediately if the app is useful. Any feature requiring a persistent data connection when he is on stretches of I-84 through Oregon where cell coverage drops. He has quietly abandoned several apps that appeared to require syncing or connection to function at all — not because he understood that was the problem, but because the app seemed to do nothing and he stopped using it. Apps that use industry terminology from outside his world — "workspace," "dashboard," "pipeline," "integration" — as navigation labels rather than functional descriptions leave him with no compass. |
| **Mental Model** | He thinks of apps as tools with one or two primary jobs. A hammer does one thing; a good app does one thing per screen. His expectation, built across years of using functional single-purpose apps, is that the most important action on a screen is visually prominent, that secondary actions are clearly subsidiary, and that what he taps next should be obvious without his having to think about it. He has a spatial rather than hierarchical model of navigation — he experiences an app as a set of places he has been, not as a tree of menus and submenus. If he ends up somewhere unfamiliar, his first move is the back button, which he uses as a universal "undo" for navigation. He does not think of software as having states or sessions in the technical sense; to him, an app either works right now or it doesn't, and he does not have a conceptual model for why an app might behave differently based on prior actions he took in it. When an app behaves unexpectedly, his diagnosis is usually "it glitched" regardless of the actual cause. |

## UX Testing Value

The issues Tomás surfaces are not edge cases — they are the baseline requirements of an interface that works outside a controlled environment. He finds touch targets that are technically tappable but fail at normal human accuracy, especially when the test is run in a room with fluorescent lighting rather than on a tablet in direct afternoon sunlight. He identifies the exact screen depth at which a task flow becomes too long to complete without referencing what he was originally trying to do. He finds the moment when an error message — accurately phrased, appropriately positioned — fails to communicate because it arrived at the wrong point in the user's sequence and they had already moved past it. These are not failures of polish. They are failures of fundamental task completion, and they appear in his testing as a quiet, matter-of-fact stall: he simply stops, doesn't know where to go, and doesn't continue. Watching him, a developer can see exactly where the interface ran out of guidance.

He also surfaces a specific and underrepresented failure mode: the interface that works for a user who gives it full attention and breaks for one who doesn't. Tomás is never fully attending. He is managing a schedule, thinking about a delivery window, tracking a noise the rig has been making since Flagstaff. When he uses software, part of his cognition is always elsewhere, and a product that requires his full attention to complete a basic task will not survive contact with the actual conditions of his life. Developers who watch him test will observe him returning to a screen after a thirty-second distraction and failing to recover his position — not because the interface changed, but because it provided no way to pick the thread back up. This is a failure that user testing in attentive laboratory conditions will never surface.

He brings an additional value that is harder to measure: he has no investment in the product's success and no knowledge of what the designers intended, which makes his confusion genuinely diagnostic rather than socially constructed. He will not try to understand what a UI element was supposed to mean and then perform understanding. He will try it, fail, and tell you he failed, without apology or interpretation. There is no courtesy confusion in his testing — he is not confused because he is being polite. When he says "I don't know what this is asking me," the interface has genuinely failed to communicate, and the sentence is a verbatim design requirement waiting to be written.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He has a specific and deeply considered opinion about truck stop coffee that functions as a proxy for his broader taste in quality — he is not a snob, but he has strong preferences built from thousands of data points, and he expresses them with the same even certainty he applies to load rates and fuel prices. He has a ranked mental list of which chains are reliable at 3am on a Tuesday in eastern Oregon. This list has never been written down. It doesn't need to be.

- When he is tired, he becomes quieter and more deliberate in a way that is almost indistinguishable from his focused mode, except that his responses have a fractional delay. He is aware of this about himself and manages it with breaks, with truck stop walks, with a thermos of coffee he makes at home and keeps in an insulated bag behind his seat. Gabriela gave him the thermos two Christmases ago. He has replaced the lid once. He would not replace the thermos.

- He coaches Little League with a patience he does not always extend to adults and a directness he applies equally to eight-year-olds and to their parents. He tells kids the truth about what they're doing wrong because, in his experience, kids absorb honest instruction and perform it back. He has more trouble with parents who want their child's performance managed rather than coached. He handles this conflict with a diplomacy that surprises people who know him mostly from trucking contexts, where diplomacy is not his primary mode.

- He has a habit of reading broker emails two or three times before accepting a load — not because the terms are complex, but because he has learned, through one particular incident five years ago involving a chassis fee that was technically disclosed in paragraph four of a long email, to treat the reading of contracts as a form of safety work. He applies this same slow, deliberate re-reading to any form or agreement he encounters digitally, which in a UX testing context means he reads on-screen text more carefully than the average user and finds copy that is technically present but practically invisible.

- He is not on social media except for a Facebook account he created to stay in contact with cousins in Las Cruces and checks approximately twice a month. He finds the apps confusing not in a principled way but in a bored way — too much on the screen, none of it obviously useful, scroll behavior that feels undirected. He does not dislike technology. He dislikes technology that does not have a clear answer to the question "what do I do here."

- Marisol has recently become interested in his tablet, which she refers to as "Papi's work thing," and he has, somewhat against his better judgment, installed one children's drawing app for her use during the rare afternoon he is home and she needs a twenty-minute distraction. He has watched her navigate it with a facility that he finds both impressive and faintly humbling. She is four. She pinch-zooms with her whole hand. He takes this data point less personally than it might seem.
