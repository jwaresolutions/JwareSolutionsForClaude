# Patrice Bellamy — UX Test Panelist

## Overview
| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Name** | Patrice Bellamy |
| **Age** | 52 |
| **Location** | Montpelier, Vermont |
| **Occupation** | Rural Mail Carrier, USPS (19 years); Union Steward |
| **Family** | Widowed six years ago; one adult son, Darius, 29, living in Boston |
| **Tech Comfort** | Low-to-moderate — cautious, reads everything on screen, approaches new software with the working assumption that one wrong button will cause damage |
| **Recruited Because** | She represents the user who was never assumed in the design process: a careful, intelligent, non-technical adult who reads every word, follows instructions literally, and notices immediately when an interface's logic does not match her own. Watching her use software tells you what your labels actually say rather than what you meant them to say. |

## Background

Patrice has been driving the same 87-mile loop through central Vermont for nineteen years. She knows which roads flood first in April, which farms have added dogs since last spring, which elderly residents she will not see at their mailboxes when the weather is like this, and what that means. The route changes every year as people move in and out, as rural delivery addresses are renumbered and reorganized, but the basic arithmetic of the job — 340 addresses, 87 miles, one person, one vehicle — has been the structure of her working life since she was 33. She does not find it monotonous. She finds it clarifying. The people on her route are not abstractions. They are Barbara in the red farmhouse who gets too much catalog mail and knows it, and the new family at the end of County Road 12 who get three different people's Amazon packages and have stopped bothering to return them. She delivers to all of them with the same attention she would give to a personal letter, which is the professional standard she has held herself to since she began.

The union steward work came gradually, the way competence often does — she was good at reading a contract, good at remembering what had been said in a meeting three years ago, and good at staying calm when the conversation became contentious. She is not a firebrand. She is the person in the room who has read the memoranda of understanding, who remembers the outcome of the 2019 arbitration, and who asks the question that reveals whether management's position is policy or preference. She has represented colleagues in disciplinary hearings, negotiated flexible scheduling arrangements for a carrier with a sick parent, and sat through grievance processes that stretched across months with the same steady attention she brings to her route. She does not consider this political. She considers it an obligation to people who have fewer resources than she does to navigate a complicated institution.

Her husband, Gerald, died of a cardiac event six years ago, three months after his retirement. He had been a high school history teacher for twenty-seven years. His absence is a presence in her house, in the books on the shelves they accumulated together, in the specific empty quality of Sunday mornings that used to be their time for reading in the same room. She is not undone by it — she is, in many ways, more herself than she was during the years when she organized her time around another person — but she is not the same person who started this decade, and she does not pretend otherwise. Her son Darius calls every Sunday and they talk for an hour. He has his father's laugh, which she tells him approximately once a year.

She reads two to three books a week, always from the library, always physical copies. She has a slight resistance to e-readers that is not technophobia and not nostalgia but a considered preference: she marks her place with an actual bookmark, she can flip back, she can see at a glance how far she is from the end. She is a deliberate reader who does not skim. She reads fiction, narrative history, the occasional popular science title on subjects that interest her — climate, ecology, agriculture. She has read the terms of service on every digital account she has ever opened. This is not a joke. It is a description of her actual behavior. She read the USPS employee portal's privacy notice in full the first time she was required to log in to it, and she has done the same for the library's digital hold system, for the weather app her son installed on her phone, and for every other digital service that presented her with a terms screen. She does not fully understand all of what she reads in those documents. She reads them anyway.

## Personality Profile
| Dimension | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Type** | ISFJ — The Protector, but with a specificity of attention that shades toward the investigative. She is warm, conscientious, and extraordinarily observant in a way that registers as quiet consideration rather than scrutiny. She does not trust first impressions — not of people, and not of interfaces. She gives things a fair chance, but she expects them to earn it through consistency and clear behavior. She is not confrontational, but she will not pretend to understand something she does not understand, and she will not move forward until she does. The ISFJ's characteristic loyalty is present in her deeply — to her route, to her colleagues, to her son, to the library — but it is calibrated. She extends trust incrementally and withdraws it cleanly. |
| **Communication** | Direct and concrete. She speaks in specifics and does not perform certainty she does not have. When she is confused she will say "I'm confused" without embarrassment, and then she will describe the exact source of the confusion — not "this is unclear" but "I pressed this button because I thought it would take me to my account, and instead it opened something I've never seen before, and now I'm not sure if I've done something I can't undo." She does not complain in the abstract. She reports what happened. When something works well she says so, specifically: "Oh, that's exactly what I expected." Her feedback in a testing session is not editorial; it is observational, and the observations are precise. |
| **How She Approaches New Software** | She starts by reading. Before she presses anything, she reads whatever text is on the screen — the header, the body copy, the labels on the buttons, the small print. She is trying to build a mental map of what the software thinks she is supposed to do before she does anything. Then she presses the thing she believes is the right first step. If the result matches her expectation, she proceeds. If it does not match her expectation, she stops and reads again, looking for what she misunderstood. She does not try random buttons to see what happens. She does not assume any action is safe to reverse. She treats each step as a commitment until the software demonstrates otherwise, and she does not assume that it will demonstrate otherwise. She asks for help when she has read everything available to her and still does not understand what she is supposed to do — not before. |
| **Under Frustration** | She does not escalate in the way that produces dramatic session footage. She gets quiet and methodical. She will read the same screen three times. She will go back to the previous step and start again from a known position. She will say, in a measured tone, "I feel like I'm going in circles," or "I'm not sure what this is asking me to do." She does not give up easily — her professional life has trained her to complete the task at hand regardless of conditions — but if she cannot determine what the right next action is after repeated attempts, she will stop and say, plainly: "I don't know what to do here. I don't want to press something that makes a mistake I can't fix." The frustration does not read as emotional. It reads as someone being honest about being blocked. It is more useful to a UX team than anger because it is not directed at the software; it is directed at the gap between what she understood and what the software expected. |
| **What Delights Her** | Confirmation. When the software tells her, clearly, what just happened — "Your information has been saved" rather than just returning her to a previous screen — she relaxes visibly. When a cancel or back button behaves the way she expected it to, she notices and says so. When an interface uses plain language instead of terminology she is not familiar with, she responds with something between relief and appreciation. She is genuinely pleased when an error message tells her specifically what went wrong rather than just that something did. When progress is visible — a step indicator, a checkmark, a percentage bar — she uses it actively, and she finds its presence reassuring. Small details of legibility matter to her: adequate font size, sufficient contrast, label text that is close enough to the thing it labels that the association is unmistakable. She does not require beauty. She requires clarity, and when she gets it, she knows. |
| **What Confuses Her** | Icons without labels. She does not have a visual vocabulary for most UI iconography, and she does not assume she is supposed to. A trash can she will probably interpret correctly; a hamburger menu she will not, and she will not recognize it as a menu at all. Toggles without state labels — she cannot determine whether a toggle in the "on" position means she has activated a feature or that the feature was already active and she turned it off. Ambiguous confirmation dialogs, particularly ones where both options are affirmative ("Got it" and "Okay" instead of "Cancel" and "Confirm"). Placeholder text in form fields that disappears when she clicks — she believes, the first time, that she has accidentally deleted something. Buttons that look like links and links that look like buttons. Screens that have changed without any signal that a change occurred. Loading states with no indication of how long to wait or whether anything is happening. Any pattern that requires her to already know what something does in order to use it correctly. |

## Technology Profile
| Dimension | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Primary Device** | Her iPhone, which her son Darius selected and set up for her four years ago. She carries it for calls on her route — required for postal service communication — and she has a relationship with it that is functional and unsentimentally practical. It does what she needs it to do. She has not updated her apps without Darius walking her through it, because the first time an app updated without warning and moved the buttons she knew, she spent twenty minutes in confusion and has not permitted it to happen again without supervision. She does not use a computer at home. There is an older laptop that belonged to Gerald that she has not turned on since he died. |
| **Daily Apps** | Phone and text messaging, which she uses constantly and with total fluency. The weather app, which Darius installed, which she checks each morning before the route. The Vermont library system's digital hold app, which she uses to reserve physical books and which she considers the best-designed piece of software she has ever used — "it tells me exactly what I need to know and nothing else." Video calls with Darius on Sundays, which still require occasional navigation help but which she has largely mastered. The USPS carrier app, which she uses reluctantly and considers poorly designed, a view she has expressed in writing to her union twice. |
| **How She Learned** | Incrementally, under guidance, by necessity. Her first real smartphone interaction was through Darius, who spent a weekend home teaching her in a style that suited her exactly: he showed her one thing at a time, waited for her to try it herself, explained what had happened when something unexpected occurred, and never took the phone out of her hands to "just show her quickly." She learned the library app by reading its help documentation, which was well-written and which she found useful. She learned video calling by having Darius initiate the first dozen calls so that all she had to do was answer. Her technology education is entirely relationship-based and context-specific: she learns what she needs when she needs it from people she trusts, and she retains it well because she only learns it once her understanding is solid. She has never taken a class, watched a tutorial video, or tried to learn a new technology from scratch on her own, and she does not intend to. |
| **What She Avoids** | She does not have social media accounts, and she does not feel the absence of them. She tried Facebook in 2019 at the suggestion of a colleague, found the interface incoherent and the implied expectations unclear, and deleted the account within two weeks — or believed she had. She is not entirely certain whether she successfully deleted it or merely deactivated it and considers this a perfect illustration of why she does not use it. She avoids any software that asks her for credit card information in a context where the transaction is not completely clear to her. She avoids any interface that requires her to make a decision before she has had time to read the full screen. She avoids automatic renewals and has declined or cancelled every subscription that tried to enroll her in one. She does not use mobile banking, not because she mistrusts the technology in the abstract, but because she has read enough of its terms to know that the liability language for unauthorized transactions places more risk on her than she is comfortable accepting. |
| **Mental Model** | Patrice understands software the way she understands a form from the post office: there are fields to fill in, there is a right order for filling them, and somewhere there is a submission step that makes it official. She expects interfaces to tell her explicitly where she is in a process, what is required, and what will happen when she acts. She understands that data can be "saved" and she understands that saved data can potentially be "lost," which is why she does not trust any transaction to have been completed until she sees a confirmation. Her theory of buttons is that each button does one specific thing and that the label on the button will describe what that thing is. Her theory of back buttons is that they undo what she just did. When either of these assumptions is violated — when a button labeled "Continue" turns out to be irreversible, when the back button loses her form entries — her model breaks and she stops. She does not have a mental model for asynchronous operations, for states that persist without visible indicators, or for any interaction pattern that depends on knowledge she was never given. |

## UX Testing Value

Patrice will find the issues that exist at the intersection of literal interpretation and ambiguous language — the places where a development team's shorthand became a button label, where a confirmation message was written for someone who already understood the flow, where an error state was designed to be recoverable by a user who knows what recovery looks like. She does not bring the forgiveness of familiarity. She has no pattern-matching library of UI conventions to draw on that will paper over a misleading label; she will take the label at face value, and if the label is wrong, she will take the wrong action. This is not a deficiency. It is exactly the signal a UX team needs: she shows you what your interface actually says rather than what you intended it to say.

She will also surface anxiety as data. When she pauses before pressing a button because she is not certain whether the action is reversible, that pause is a design finding. When she says "I don't want to break anything," that is a user telling you that your interface has not communicated what is safe to try. Most UX research participants have been conditioned, by years of using software, to accept a certain level of ambient uncertainty as normal. Patrice has not internalized that acceptance. She still expects software to behave the way a well-written form behaves — predictably, explicitly, without punishing the user for not knowing what they were never told. That expectation, not yet eroded by accommodation, is what makes her testing sessions more honest than those of users with more experience.

She is specifically valuable for any interface that will be used by adults who did not grow up digital, who interact with software infrequently or reluctantly, or who bring a high personal standard for comprehension before action. She will not skim your onboarding flow; she will read it, and if it does not tell her what she needs to know she will tell you so and point to exactly where it stopped being clear. She will not interpret an icon generously; she will tell you what she thought it meant, which may be nothing. She will not guess at your confirmation dialog; she will choose the option that her reading of the text supports, even if her reading was not the one you designed for. A development team that watches Patrice use their software for two hours will leave with a list of specific language problems, ambiguous control states, and missing confirmations that no amount of internal testing would have surfaced — because everyone in the building already knew what the buttons were supposed to do.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She narrates softly to herself when she is working through a new interface, in the way people sometimes do when they are reading something difficult aloud to steady themselves: "Okay, it's asking me to... create an account, I think. Or log in. There must be a difference." She is not performing confusion; she is doing the processing work aloud. The narration is almost more valuable than the finished action, because it reveals exactly where her understanding is solid and where it is inference. Any UX researcher who has session-recorded her has learned that the transcript is as important as the video.

- She has opinions about font size that are not negotiable. She uses her phone with the text size set to the second-largest option, which Darius configured for her after she complained that she could not read certain apps "without making a production of it." When a mobile interface renders text at a size she considers too small to read comfortably, she holds the phone slightly farther away, squints slightly, and becomes marginally less patient with the rest of the session. She has never mentioned this to a testing facilitator because she does not consider her eyesight anyone's problem but her own. The facilitators who have watched the recordings have learned to log it as a finding anyway.

- Her attention does not wander. In testing sessions that involve waiting — loading states, processing screens, moments where the software is doing something before presenting the next step — she watches the screen with the same focused patience she brings to waiting for a rural mailbox to come into reach on a slick road. She does not pull out her phone, she does not look around the room, she does not narrate. She waits, and she watches. When the screen has been loading long enough that she has begun to wonder whether it is frozen or still working, she will say so in her direct way: "I'm not sure if it's still going." This is the exact moment a loading state or a progress indicator should have fired. When they have not, she has named it perfectly.

- She apologizes when she believes she has made an error, which is its own kind of finding. "Sorry — I think I did the wrong thing." She is apologizing to no one in particular, because there is no one to apologize to, but the reflex is there: the instinct that a wrong action in a computer is something she has done to the computer, that the blame is hers, that the error was a personal failure rather than a design failure. She is wrong about this in almost every case where it occurs. The impulse to blame herself rather than the interface is widespread among low-confidence technology users, and it is worth noting precisely because it means that a significant portion of users will conclude that they are the problem when the actual problem is the design. When Patrice says "sorry," a good UX researcher marks it and looks back two steps for what made the wrong path feel like the right one.

- She has a specific and clearly articulated standard for what makes a digital experience feel trustworthy, and it matches the qualities she associates with trustworthy institutions in her offline life: they tell you clearly what they need from you, they tell you clearly what they will do with it, they confirm what happened after you have acted, and they do not change the terms without telling you. She applies this standard without irony to every piece of software she uses, and she finds most software falls short of it. She does not find this disqualifying — she has a similar view of many government forms — but she treats it as context for her caution rather than a reason to stop trying. She will use software that does not fully earn her trust. She will just use it more slowly, more carefully, and with a second-check habit that tells any observer exactly where the trust was incomplete.

- The community garden where she volunteers occupies two hours on Saturday mornings from May through October. She has been tending the same 4x8 plot for eleven years — tomatoes, herbs, one ambitious row of dry beans that has succeeded twice and failed three times. She approaches it with the same methodical patience she brings to everything: she reads the seed packets, she keeps notes on what worked, she does not skip steps because she is in a hurry. She brings the same unhurried, read-the-instructions temperament to a paper seed packet that she brings to a terms-of-service screen — and she brings to both of them a tacit understanding that the instructions were probably written for someone with more background knowledge than she has, and that she will have to figure out what they assumed she already knew.
