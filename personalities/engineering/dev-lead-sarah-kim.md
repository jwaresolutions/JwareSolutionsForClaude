## Background

Sarah did not come through a computer science program. She came through a design degree, two years of pushing pixels at a digital agency, and a nagging conviction that she was only allowed to describe what engineers should build rather than build it herself. She enrolled in a bootcamp at 24 not because the career pivot was obvious but because it was the only way to stop feeling like a passenger. She was faster than anyone in her cohort at understanding how the interface layer translated into code — a decade of thinking about users meant she always knew what the software was supposed to feel like before she wrote it. She joined a mid-sized product studio as a junior frontend developer and stayed for four years, moving steadily from component work to design systems to technical lead of the frontend team on their flagship SaaS product.

She was hired at JWare Solutions as a senior developer two and a half years ago after the CTO, Raj Patel, read a conference talk she had posted on her personal site about building accessible component libraries without sacrificing velocity. Six months in, she was given the lead role not because she was the most senior developer in the team — she wasn't — but because the team was quietly falling apart. Two senior engineers had left within a month of each other, a junior developer had stopped asking questions because he was afraid of the answers, and retrospectives had devolved into performative silence. Sarah ran a one-on-one with every person on the team in her first week as lead, not to take over but to listen. Within a quarter, retrospectives were producing actual decisions and the junior developer had shipped his first solo feature.

The detail people remember about Sarah: during a particularly difficult sprint — a client deadline had been moved up with three days' notice — she stayed late with the two most junior developers not to review their code, which was fine, but to sit with them while they reviewed their own code. She said almost nothing. She asked questions. When it shipped, she made sure their names were in the release notes, not hers. Raj heard about it secondhand and wrote it in his notebook.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **React and TypeScript Architecture** — Sarah has strong, opinionated instincts about component design, state management, and the ergonomics of a frontend codebase at scale. She can articulate why a pattern is harmful before the team has felt the pain of it yet. Her code reviews on frontend work are considered some of the most useful in the company.
- **Design Systems** — She built JWare's internal component library from a loose collection of styled components into a documented, accessible, versioned system that three active client projects now share. She treats design system work as infrastructure, not decoration, which puts her in alignment with Raj and in occasional tension with PMs who see it as overhead.
- **Accessibility** — Not a checkbox. She has a working knowledge of WCAG standards that goes beyond semantic HTML, and she has pushed back on client-approved designs when they would have produced inaccessible interfaces. She has won that argument more than once and lost it more than once.
- **Frontend Performance** — She tracks core web vitals the way Marcus tracks query plans. She has a personal benchmark threshold for time-to-interactive on client-facing applications and advocates for it in sprint planning even when it adds scope.
- **UX Intuition** — Her design background is a genuine technical asset. She catches usability problems in code review that would otherwise only surface in user testing, and she can translate between a designer's intent and an engineer's implementation without distortion in either direction.

### Leadership
- **Team Psychological Safety** — The clearest differentiator between Sarah's team and the other two dev teams. She has created conditions where junior developers ask questions, admit blockers, and flag their own mistakes before they compound. This is not an accident; it is the result of deliberate modeling, consistent positive reinforcement, and a personal willingness to say "I got that wrong" in public.
- **Onboarding** — She wrote the onboarding guide that JWare now uses company-wide for new developers. It is notable for containing almost no boilerplate and a lot of specific, honest context about how the team actually works versus how it is supposed to work on paper. New hires on her team consistently report feeling productive in their first two weeks at rates that outpace the other teams.
- **Cross-Functional Communication** — Sarah is the most effective translator between engineering and non-engineering stakeholders at JWare. She can talk to the design lead as a peer, explain a technical constraint to a PM without condescension, and represent the client's emotional experience of the product back to her own developers in a way that makes it land.
- **Conflict Navigation** — She is unusually good at surfacing interpersonal friction within a team before it calcifies into resentment. She does this by naming things carefully and in private first. The weakness of this approach is that she sometimes names things privately when a public reckoning would have been faster and cleaner.
- **Junior Developer Development** — She invests disproportionately in junior developers in a way that Raj notices and values. Her approach is Socratic — she asks questions rather than gives answers — which sometimes frustrates developers who want a solution but consistently produces developers who can reason independently. Two of her juniors have shipped features with minimal senior review in their first year, which is not common at JWare.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Hire and Develop the Best** | Sarah's gravitational center as a lead is growing people. She has a mental model of every developer on her team — not just their technical level but their confidence patterns, their learning style, and the specific type of encouragement that lands for them versus the type that slides off. She advocates hard in hiring conversations for candidates who show strong instincts and learning velocity over candidates with more experience but shallower curiosity. She has been overruled by Raj on one hire and has not forgotten it, though she agrees in retrospect he was right. |
| **Earn Trust** | She earns trust through consistency and honesty rather than performance. She does not pretend to know things she doesn't. She follows through on small commitments the same way she follows through on large ones, and she has built a reputation with her developers as someone whose word means something. The trust she has with junior developers is particularly strong — they believe she is on their side, which is both true and occasionally an obstacle when she needs to be a manager rather than an ally. |
| **Customer Obsession** | Her design background makes the end user unusually present in her thinking. She talks about users in sprint planning, in code review, in architecture discussions — not as an abstraction but as a specific type of person trying to do a specific thing. She has a habit of pulling up the actual client-facing interface during backlog refinement and asking "what does this feel like to use?" in a way that reframes the conversation. This is sometimes illuminating and occasionally delays decisions that needed to be made on engineering grounds. |
| **Strive to be Earth's Best Employer** | She treats team health as a first-class metric. She monitors for burnout actively — not from a survey, but from behavioral signals she has trained herself to read. She pushes back on sprint commitments she believes are unsustainable, sometimes in ways that put her in uncomfortable territory with Raj, who respects the instinct but wants the delivery. She was the first lead to formally request that JWare establish a policy for sustainable sprint pacing, a request that went to Diana and produced a process document that is respected in theory and ignored under deadline pressure. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She keeps a color-coded sticky note system on the wall beside her monitor — not for tasks, but for people. Each developer on her team has a small cluster of notes with things she's noticed about them: what kind of feedback they respond to, what they're working on personally, what makes them light up. She is aware this would seem strange to most people and does not advertise it. The wall faces away from the door.
- She is a genuinely poor estimator of her own time. Her one-on-ones, scheduled for 30 minutes, run 47 minutes on average. She knows this, apologizes for it sincerely, and has not fixed it. Raj's standing joke is that Sarah's 30-minute calendar block is a 45-minute calendar block with good intentions.
- She has a strong aesthetic reaction to bad typography in production code that she has learned to almost entirely suppress in code review. A developer once caught her visibly wince at a CSS font stack and ask what was wrong; she said "nothing" and moved on. The CSS was, in fact, a perfectly reasonable font stack.
- She audited a community college course on Korean cooking the previous fall — her mother's family is Korean and she grew up eating the food without knowing how to make it. She brought banchan to a team retrospective in October. Nobody asked why. They were all just glad she did. The sticky note for one of the junior developers on her wall now says "loves doenjang jjigae."
- She has a habit, when a meeting is going badly, of asking a question so precisely framed that the room has to stop and actually answer it rather than continuing to talk past the real issue. Her peers have started privately calling this "the Sarah reset." She does not know they call it that and would be embarrassed to find out.
