## Background

Alex grew up in San Jose in a Vietnamese-American family where their father ran a small computer repair shop that Alex haunted from age eight — not because they were required to, but because watching broken things become unbroken was satisfying in a way they couldn't fully articulate at the time and can articulate only slightly better now. They spent high school running the school's helpdesk, which mostly meant reimaging laptops and being the person teachers called when the projector stopped working, which turned out to be excellent training for understanding that infrastructure is only noticed when it fails. They chose Portland State over larger schools because PSU offered a full scholarship and because the IT program was practical in a way that a CS program at a bigger school might not have been — they wanted to understand how systems ran, not just how to write code for them.

Their senior capstone was a monitoring and alerting pipeline they built for a local nonprofit's internal infrastructure — a two-person project that Alex ended up doing mostly alone after their partner dropped the course, and that they finished ahead of schedule and documented so thoroughly that the nonprofit's IT volunteer coordinator said it was better documented than anything their paid vendors had ever produced. Raj Patel saw the project writeup in a Portland State senior showcase he attended at the invitation of the university's career center, asked Alex two technical questions, got answers that were more operationally sophisticated than he expected, and forwarded their resume to Tomas Rivera the same afternoon with a note that said "worth a conversation."

Tomas interviewed Alex for forty-five minutes, which is long for Tomas. He asked one question about how they would debug a service that was intermittently unreachable, and Alex talked for twelve minutes, covered four distinct failure modes, and then paused and said "I'd also want to check the thing I probably haven't thought of yet." Tomas offered them the position two days later. The rest of the engineering team found this out in the all-hands when Raj introduced Alex as the newest hire. Tomas had not mentioned the hire was in progress to anyone. This is normal for Tomas and has stopped being surprising.

Six months in, Alex is the most eager person in the building and the most likely to break something. They have taken down a staging environment once, misconfigured a deployment pipeline twice, and submitted a pull request that would have created a privilege escalation vector in a development IAM policy that Tomas caught in code review, said nothing about in the review comments, and used as the basis for a thirty-minute pairing session on IAM principles the following morning. Alex has made every mistake with full commitment and documented it afterward without being asked to. Sam O'Connell, the mid-level developer who has become their daily mentor by proximity and patience, has described working with Alex as "like working next to someone who is enthusiastic about touching every electric fence at least once." This is said with genuine affection.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **Linux Systems Administration** — Their clearest technical foundation. They are comfortable at the command line in a way that reflects years of practice, not months of bootcamp. They navigate a Linux system under pressure without the hesitation that marks most junior hires, and their troubleshooting instinct — work down from the symptom, check the obvious layer, then go deeper — is more developed here than anywhere else.
- **Scripting and Automation** — They write Bash competently and Python adequately. Their automation scripts are functional and under-commented in a way that Tomas has flagged twice. They have a strong instinct for what should be automated and a developing instinct for whether the automation is maintainable by someone else.
- **Monitoring and Observability** — Their capstone project gave them real hands-on experience here. They understand the purpose of alerting, have configured dashboards and alert thresholds in production, and have a mental model of what a healthy system looks like in a monitoring pane that most juniors build slowly over years.
- **Networking Fundamentals** — They can trace a network path, read a packet capture in broad strokes, and reason about DNS, load balancers, and port behavior in a way that reflects the practical education their IT degree provided. Their depth here is better than their depth in software engineering proper.

### Growth Areas
- **Security Posture and IAM** — Their most actively dangerous growth area. They understand security in principle and have a tendency to set permissions pragmatically in ways that work and create risks they don't see. The IAM incident in code review was not unusual; it was representative. Tomas is addressing this deliberately and systematically. Alex is receptive.
- **Infrastructure as Code discipline** — They can write Terraform and have done so. Their IaC is functional and under-structured — modules where there should be variables, hardcoded values where there should be references, resource naming that reflects the order things were created rather than what they are. They know IaC best practices exist and have not yet internalized them as a workflow.
- **Change Management and Blast Radius Thinking** — The fastest and most consequential thing Alex needs to develop. Their instinct is to try the change and see what happens. The instinct needs to become: understand the blast radius before you touch anything. Tomas has framed this for them as the difference between a soldier who fires to suppress and a soldier who fires with a target. The metaphor landed. The behavior is still being rebuilt.
- **Documentation Habits** — Their post-incident notes are excellent because the format is clear and the learning motivation is immediate. Their inline documentation of infrastructure choices is sparse, and they do not yet leave a system better-explained than they found it as a matter of practice. Tomas considers this a first-year problem. Sam considers it a right-now problem.
- **Knowing When Done Means Done** — They finish tasks and keep improving them. This is a virtue that has not yet acquired the discipline to know when further improvement is gold-plating rather than engineering. They have submitted PRs with new features added to what was a bug fix, have rebuilt something that was working because they noticed a better approach while fixing an unrelated issue, and have generally not yet developed the instinct for scope containment. It is an endearing problem for now and will become a real one.
- **Patience with Process** — They find approval gates, change windows, and documented review requirements friction rather than structure. They understand intellectually that the process exists to prevent failures they haven't seen yet. They have not had the production outage that makes the process feel protective rather than bureaucratic. Tomas is waiting for that moment rather than trying to produce it through argument.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- They keep a running Slack channel with themselves — a personal DM they use as a scratch pad — called "things I broke and why." It is the most organized document in their professional life, which says something about what they are and are not motivated by. It has thirty-one entries. Entry number four, about the IAM policy incident, has a note at the bottom that says "Tomas caught this. He turned it into a lesson instead of a write-up. Remember that." They have not deleted that note.
- They are constitutionally incapable of leaving a slow script alone. If a shell script takes longer than they think it should, they will profile it and optimize it regardless of whether anyone asked or whether the script runs more than twice a week. Sam has found three optimized scripts in the codebase whose authors were confused to find themselves credited in the commit history. He has not told Alex to stop because the scripts are actually faster now.
- They eat lunch at their desk most days, fast, and then spend the last ten minutes of lunch watching YouTube videos about infrastructure topics that have nothing to do with their current work — talks from SREcon, PagerDuty postmortems, data center walkthroughs. This is not assigned learning. Nobody told them to. They do it because they find it genuinely interesting and because they have decided that the fastest way to see the failure modes they haven't encountered yet is to find people who have already been through them.
- They have a small cactus on their desk named after a Linux kernel version — currently "Torvalds 6.1" — and have explained this naming convention to exactly one person (Sam), who nodded slowly and said "okay." The cactus is thriving. Alex considers this a good sign about the overall environment.
- The detail that surprises people most about Alex when they first learn it: they are a genuinely careful writer outside of technical contexts. Their incident notes are better than most senior developers'. Their slack messages in technical channels are fast and approximate. The gap between these two modes is wide enough that Aisha Mohammed, upon reading one of Alex's post-incident write-ups, went to Tomas's desk and said "did you write this?" Tomas said no and did not elaborate. Alex does not know this conversation happened and would find it embarrassing and motivating in roughly equal measure.
