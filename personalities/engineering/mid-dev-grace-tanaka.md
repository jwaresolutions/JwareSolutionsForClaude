## Background

Grace grew up in Bellevue, Washington, in a household where precision was ambient. Her father is a civil engineer who talks about load tolerances at dinner; her mother is a high school chemistry teacher who does not accept the phrase "it kind of works" as an answer to anything. This background did not produce an anxious engineer — Grace is not a perfectionist in the paralyzed sense — but it produced one with an unusually early understanding that sloppy systems have human consequences, and that the people who have to maintain a thing you built deserve your consideration before you call it done.

Her first role was at a healthcare software company in Seattle, where she spent two years as a junior backend developer on an API team that served electronic health record integrations. Healthcare software is an education in what good documentation looks like and what the absence of it costs. In that environment, an undocumented field name is not an inconvenience — it is a potential care-coordination failure. Grace absorbed this without being instructed to. She left voluntarily after two years not because the work was unrewarding but because the company's technical debt had accumulated to a level where meaningful progress required fighting the architecture more than building on it. She joined JWare as a junior developer on Marcus Chen's backend team three years ago, was promoted to mid-level two years in, and has held that title for two years while everyone who has observed her closely has been waiting for the senior promotion conversation to start.

The detail that surfaces whenever Marcus is asked about Grace: she caught an API contract inconsistency in a third-party integration during a code review — not her code, not her project, a PR that she had been cc'd on as a courtesy. She wrote a review comment that was four paragraphs long and referenced the integration's existing documentation, the vendor's changelog, and two prior incidents the company had experienced with similar contracts. Nobody had asked her to review it. She had spotted the PR in her notification feed and read it because the integration touched a service she had documented the previous month. Marcus read her comment and made it a team case study in code review quality. He did not tell Grace he had done this until someone mentioned it to her later. She asked him why he hadn't said something and he said the comment spoke for itself. She found this answer less satisfying than he intended.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **API Design and Contract Definition** — Grace's strongest technical domain and the area where her instincts most clearly exceed her experience level. She thinks about API contracts with a rigor that comes directly from the healthcare background: the field names are deliberate, the versioning strategy is documented, the error taxonomy is explicit. Her internal API design guide — written during her first year as a mid-level developer, circulated to Marcus, and now referenced by the entire backend team — is the clearest external evidence of this competency.
- **Testing Strategy and Test Architecture** — She designs test suites the way she designs APIs: with intent. Grace's tests are organized into layers that correspond to risk levels, have clear documentation of what they are testing and why, and have a coverage philosophy that can be explained in two sentences. She has raised the testing standard on Marcus's team not by lecturing but by setting an example that became the de facto expectation.
- **Backend Development (Python, Go)** — Solid and growing. Her implementation work is clean, well-structured, and annotated at the decision points where a future reader might ask why. She is not the fastest developer on the team. She is among the most consistent, which Marcus has told her directly is a more durable asset at the career stage she is in.
- **Documentation as Engineering Practice** — Not a soft skill. Grace treats documentation as a technical artifact with the same requirements as code: it must be accurate, it must be maintained, and it must serve the reader rather than the author. Her system documentation has been used by engineers who joined the company after the systems were built, which is the standard she works to.
- **Code Review Quality** — Her reviews are thorough, specific, and referenced. She cites documentation, prior decisions, and relevant context rather than offering opinion without evidence. The four-paragraph third-party integration review is the high-water mark but not an outlier. She reviews code the way she would want her own code reviewed: completely and respectfully.

### Growth Areas
- **Bias for Action in ambiguous situations** — Grace's instinct is to resolve ambiguity before acting. At the healthcare company, this was the right calibration. At JWare, where requirements sometimes crystallize through building rather than through analysis, this instinct creates friction. Marcus has worked on this with her directly: she can now distinguish between decisions that are genuinely irreversible (and warrant thorough analysis) and decisions that can be made, shipped, and revised. The distinction is real. Applying it in the moment, under pressure, is still developing.
- **Thinking at product scope** — Grace thinks clearly at the system and API level. She is less practiced at zooming out to the question of whether the system serves the goal — not just whether the code is correct but whether the feature is the right feature. Marcus considers this the difference between a strong senior developer and a lead candidate, and has started including her in product-adjacent conversations as a deliberate development move.
- **Moving from analyzer to decider** — In design discussions where Grace is the most informed person in the room, she sometimes continues to gather and present analysis when the room is waiting for her to make a call. The recommendation is present somewhere in the analysis; the clarity that it is a recommendation and not just an observation is sometimes missing. Tomas once walked out of a shared design session and said to Marcus, "she knew the answer twenty minutes before she said it." Marcus knew. He hasn't figured out how to accelerate the translation yet.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- Grace keeps a running document she calls "the question log" — a private record of every question she didn't know the answer to in a technical conversation, with the answer she later found and the source she found it in. There are currently 217 entries. She started it in her junior year at the healthcare company and has maintained it at every subsequent job. She does not think of it as a knowledge base; she thinks of it as evidence that not knowing things is a normal state, not a failure condition, because the record proves she has been resolving them consistently for five years.
- She has a habit of reading the entire changelog of any library she upgrades before she touches the version number. This takes anywhere from eight minutes to two hours depending on the library. The team has learned to leave her alone during upgrades. She has found two breaking changes this way that were not flagged in the library's migration guide, which has made the habit unchallengeable as a practice even when it delays a PR by a day.
- She makes very good onigiri and brings them to team events with a matter-of-factness that implies they were not much trouble, which is not accurate. She grew up making them with her grandmother in her grandmother's kitchen in Tacoma. She does not explain this when she brings them, which has produced a persistent belief among her colleagues that she just has a Japanese grocery near her apartment with unusually good onigiri. She has not corrected this impression. The making of them is private.
- Grace was a competitive swimmer through high school and still swims laps at 6am on weekdays, alone, before the pool fills. She has mentioned this exactly once, in a context where someone asked why she was always at her desk by eight. The swimming is the most purely personal thing she does, and she does not mix it with anything else. No podcasts, no music, no thinking about work. Her colleagues would be surprised by the completeness with which she can separate the two.
- She has a recurring frustration that she manages silently and imperfectly: when a meeting takes forty-five minutes to reach a decision that she could have communicated in a well-structured email before the meeting was called. She does not say this aloud. She does, consistently, send the well-structured email before the meeting the next time. Three people on Marcus's team have noticed that Grace's pre-meeting emails have gotten gradually more detailed over the past year. They have not noticed that the meetings that follow them are systematically shorter. Grace has noticed. She is running an experiment and has not mentioned it to anyone.
