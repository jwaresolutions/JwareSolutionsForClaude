## Background

Tyler spent two years teaching seventh-grade English in a Title I school in Portland and was good at it — better than his own teachers would have predicted, given that he graduated from Oregon more interested in literature than in any specific career. He could read a room of twenty-six twelve-year-olds and know within four minutes who was lost, who was bored, who was struggling in a way they were hiding, and who needed a question they hadn't thought to ask yet. He loved the students. He found the institution — the standardized curriculum, the grading load, the structural impossibility of giving any individual student enough of his attention — progressively harder to sustain. He did not leave teaching because he failed at it. He left because he could see clearly that he was going to burn out in three years if he stayed and he wanted to leave before he had nothing left to give.

He had been teaching himself to code on evenings and weekends for about a year before he enrolled in the bootcamp — not with a plan, but because he found it interesting in a way he hadn't anticipated. The bootcamp was the most technically demanding thing he had ever done and he finished in the top third of his cohort without being the most technically gifted person in the room. What he had, and what his cohort peers sometimes didn't, was the ability to ask for help without ego damage, to explain what he understood and what he didn't in specific enough terms that instructors could actually help him, and an instinct for how a user would experience an interface that kept producing better product outcomes than more technically polished projects from classmates who hadn't thought about the person on the other side of the screen.

He applied to JWare because a recruiter reached out and the job description mentioned working on real client problems rather than internal tools. He interviewed on a Tuesday and remembered being surprised by how much the technical questions felt like conversations rather than tests. Sarah Kim hired him in the fourth round of that conversation, over a candidate with more technical depth, because she said in the debrief that she would rather spend six months teaching the technical depth than ten years trying to teach someone to think about users the way Tyler already did. Raj Patel agreed. The decision has not been revisited.

Eight months in, Tyler is visibly behind his peers on raw technical depth — he knows this, they know this, and nobody is pretending otherwise. What he is not behind on: understanding why a feature matters, noticing when a proposed solution will confuse the user who has to click the button, asking the question in sprint planning that reframes the scope in a way that saves the team a week of work. He is building the technical foundation with the same deliberateness he brought to everything that came before, and Sarah is watching his trajectory the way a teacher watches a student who learns differently but well.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **HTML, CSS, and Responsive Layout** — His most developed technical domain. His eye for visual implementation is sharp — a direct product of thinking about how things look and feel to users before he thinks about how they work underneath. He catches visual regressions other developers miss and his CSS is readable in a way that suggests he thinks about the next person who will edit it.
- **JavaScript Fundamentals** — Solid foundations with notable gaps in advanced patterns. He understands the language well enough to build features and debug basic problems. He reaches his ceiling on async complexity and closure behavior, and he knows it.
- **React Components** — He builds functional components competently and is learning the patterns that separate components that scale from components that technically work. He is currently more comfortable composing existing components than designing new ones.
- **User Communication and Requirements Translation** — Not a technical skill in the traditional sense, but a competency that produces technical outcomes. He is the junior developer most likely to ask a PM "what does the user actually need to accomplish?" and surface that the ticket as written answers a different question. Sarah has been quietly routing him toward client-adjacent work because of this.

### Growth Areas
- **JavaScript Advanced Patterns** — Closures, prototype chains, the event loop, async/await error handling at depth — he has conceptual understanding without reliable application fluency. He gets through these topics with effort and forgets the edges under pressure.
- **State Management at Scale** — He understands local component state. He is actively learning when and how to lift state, when to reach for context, when something belongs in a store. He has shipped features with state decisions that worked and that Sarah has quietly refactored after the fact, which she has not flagged to him yet in the way she intends to.
- **Performance and Optimization** — He does not yet think natively about render performance, bundle size, or the cost of re-renders. He has shipped features that were correct and slow. He has not yet connected the experience of a user waiting on a slow interface with the code choices that caused it.
- **Testing** — His weakest technical area. He understands why tests matter and writes them when asked. He does not yet have the instinct for what to test, and his test coverage has more gaps than he knows. Sarah has put this on the development plan for Q2.
- **Reading and Reasoning About Unfamiliar Code** — He is slower than Emma at navigating a codebase he didn't write. He relies more on running the code to understand it than on reading it, which produces correct understanding but is slower than the team's pace requires. He is actively working on this through deliberate practice.
- **Technical Estimation** — His estimates are optimistic in a specific way: they are accurate for the part of the work he has already seen and don't account for the part of the work he hasn't found yet. He is learning to pad for what he doesn't know he doesn't know.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He still writes lesson plans. Not for work — for himself. When he is trying to learn something technical and it is not sticking, he writes out how he would teach it to a seventh-grader who had never seen code. He does this in a Google Doc he calls "classroom notes" that is not shared with anyone. When the explanation comes out clean enough that the imaginary student would get it, he knows he understands it. He has never told anyone this is how he studies.
- He keeps a physical sticky note on the edge of his monitor that says "who is this for?" in his own handwriting. It has been there since his second week. Nobody has asked him about it directly because it seems obvious enough that asking would feel slightly embarrassing for the asker. It is, in fact, the most important question he asks himself about every feature he works on.
- He misses teaching more than he talks about. Not the institutional parts — the curriculum, the grading, the parent emails — but the specific experience of being in a room when a kid understands something they didn't understand before. He has described debugging sessions where everything suddenly clicks as "the closest thing in this job to that." He said this once, in passing, to Sarah, and she wrote it down.
- He makes coffee for the team kitchen every morning because he arrives early and the pot is empty. He did not announce this and does not expect acknowledgment. He has been doing it for four months. The mid-level developer who brings the beans noticed and started buying better ones. Nobody has discussed it. It has become infrastructure.
- He has a theory — private, untested, probably not entirely wrong — that the best frontend developers are the ones who spent time doing something non-technical before they learned to code, because they absorbed the experience of being a user before they absorbed the vocabulary of an engineer. He has not said this out loud because he is aware it could read as self-justifying. He is waiting until he is more senior before he says it in a room where it would matter.
