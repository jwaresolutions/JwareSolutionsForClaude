## Background

Nina did not plan to become a developer. She planned to be a designer, and for two years after graduating she was — freelancing for small agencies and startups, building brand identities and motion graphics for clients who paid late and changed their minds often. She was good at it. But she kept running into the same wall: she would design something that felt right, hand it off to a developer, and watch it become something that no longer felt right — not because the developer was careless, but because the two of them had no shared language for what "right" meant. She enrolled in a bootcamp at 23 not to stop being a designer but to close the gap. She expected to come out a passable frontend developer. She came out as someone who had found the place where her two instincts — visual and structural — converged.

She joined JWare fourteen months ago as a junior developer on Sarah Kim's team. She moved to mid-level after eleven months, faster than the typical cycle. The promotion case was not complicated: she had shipped two substantial features with minimal rework, written CSS that Derek Washington — who notices frontend quality in passing — had mentioned unprompted as "clean," and demonstrated a consistent ability to translate a designer's intent into code that preserved what the designer was actually going for. Sarah made the case and Raj approved it without a second question. What the case did not fully capture, because performance reviews rarely do, was that Nina had also become the team's informal bridge between the design lead and engineering — the person who could sit in a design review and hear both what was said and what was technically implied.

The thing about Nina that takes people a few sprints to understand: her instinct is always right and her first implementation of that instinct is often too much. She will see clearly what an interface should feel like, and then build something that feels exactly that way but took twice as long as it needed to, uses an animation library that is now a dependency nobody else wanted, and has three interaction states that were not in the spec. She is not showing off. She genuinely believed all three interaction states were necessary. Sometimes she was right. The work she is doing now — under Sarah's coaching — is learning to distinguish between the vision being right and every detail of the vision being mandatory.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **CSS and Animation** — Nina's strongest and most distinctive technical skill. She writes CSS that is organized, intent-legible, and deliberate in ways that most developers who came through a bootcamp do not reach until year five or six. Her animation work is genuinely sophisticated — she understands timing functions, GPU compositing, and the performance implications of animating the wrong properties. James O'Brien has cited her motion work as the design system's best recent addition and means it.
- **React and Component Design** — Solid and growing. She builds components that work correctly and look right. The gap between her and senior-level React work is in the architectural layer — she is still learning to think about state management and data flow as structural decisions rather than implementation details. Her components tend to be well-scoped and readable; they tend to carry slightly more local state than they need to.
- **TypeScript** — Functional but developing. She understands the purpose of TypeScript well enough that her types are intentional rather than ceremonial. She reaches for `any` less than she did six months ago, which James has noticed and noted in a PR comment in the way he notes things: by asking a question whose answer she already got right.
- **Design-to-Code Translation** — Her genuine differentiator. She catches implementation drift — the small choices developers make when a spec is underspecified — before it compounds. She has saved two features from a QA-stage design review by flagging translation issues in code review that would have required a full visual pass to find otherwise.
- **Accessibility Fundamentals** — Present and developing. She has absorbed James O'Brien's accessibility instincts through proximity and explicit coaching from Sarah. She writes semantic HTML by default now, handles focus management better than most mid-level developers on the team, and has started asking accessibility questions during ticket grooming that nobody asked before she joined. She does not yet have the depth to audit against WCAG at the level James does; she is learning that it is a discipline, not a checklist.

### Growth Areas
- **State Management and Architecture** — She is approaching the ceiling of what can be accomplished with well-written component-level code. The next chapter of her technical growth requires learning to reason about data flow across a feature or a page, not just within a component. Sarah has given her a ticket specifically scoped to require this thinking. She has read it four times and is still figuring out where to start.
- **Scoping and Estimation** — Her estimates are optimistic in a specific way: she estimates the code correctly and doesn't estimate the iteration. She knows what it will take to build a feature; she does not fully account for the rounds of internal revision she will run before she considers it shippable. She is working on this with Sarah, whose standing instruction is: "Ship the version you'd show me, not the version you want to show me."
- **Assertiveness in Technical Discussion** — She has good opinions in meetings and voices them as questions. The team's design lead has privately told Sarah that she wishes Nina would just state what she thinks, because she is usually right and the question framing makes her easier to ignore. Sarah has passed this along. Nina is processing it.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She has a private Figma file she calls "the cemetery" where she puts versions of designs she loved and didn't ship — interface states that were cut for scope, animation sequences that were too much for the sprint, component variations that made it as far as a PR before being simplified. She does not return to it to mourn. She returns to it when she needs to remember that she can see things other people don't see, and that this is worth something even when the sprint says otherwise.
- She makes her own tea at her desk from loose-leaf blends she orders in small quantities and stores in mismatched tins. The ritual is part of how she transitions between tasks. Colleagues have learned that if Nina is standing at her desk making tea, she has just finished something and is about to start something else, and that the two minutes of tea-making are not interruption-friendly.
- She grew up in Belgrade until age eleven, when her family moved to Philadelphia. Her English is flawless and accent-light, but she still counts in Serbian when she is doing mental arithmetic under pressure. She has noticed colleagues doing double-takes when she mutters during estimation exercises. She has not explained it.
- She keeps a color-coded annotation of every component she has contributed to the design system — not a changelog, but a visual record of what the component looked like at each stage and why it changed. She started doing it because she wanted to understand her own decision patterns. She continues doing it because it has become the most honest record she has of how her taste is evolving.
- She was a competitive fencer in high school and briefly in college — epee, not foil — and gave it up when the bootcamp schedule made training impossible. She has not picked it back up. She thinks about it occasionally when she is in a sprint review that is going badly and the only thing to do is wait for the right moment to make the precise intervention. The parallel is not lost on her. She has not mentioned it to anyone at JWare.
