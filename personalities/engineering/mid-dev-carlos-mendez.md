## Background

Carlos got his CS degree in three and a half years, not because he was in a hurry to leave but because he was in a hurry to build things. He had been freelancing since his sophomore year — small iOS apps for local businesses, a scheduling tool for a surf school, a menu app for a taqueria his uncle co-owned — and by the time he graduated he had shipped more production code than most of his classmates had seen. He was not the strongest theoretical student in his cohort. He was, by a significant margin, the one who had done the most with what he learned. He interviewed at JWare because a recruiter found his GitHub, and he joined because Sarah Kim spent forty minutes on his technical screen not just evaluating him but showing genuine curiosity about the projects he had built. He had not expected that. It changed how he thought about the company before his first day.

He joined as a junior developer on Sarah's team and was promoted to mid-level after fourteen months. The promotion was not a surprise to anyone who had worked with him. He had shipped three features largely independently, debugged a performance issue in a client-facing React component that had stumped a more experienced developer for two days, and built a small internal tool — a design token preview utility nobody asked for — that the design lead adopted so quickly Sarah put it in the onboarding docs. The promotion came with a direct conversation from Sarah about what he needed to develop, not just what he had already demonstrated. He wrote it down. He has not fully acted on all of it.

What Carlos brings to the team that nobody else quite replicates is energy. Not performative enthusiasm — actual, contagious momentum. When he is working on something he cares about, the people around him work a little faster too. He is the developer who announces in Slack when he ships something, not to brag but because he is genuinely excited and has not yet learned that not every milestone needs a broadcast. Sarah sometimes brings him to client demos because he explains what the product does with the same specificity a developer has and the same warmth a salesperson would want. Clients respond to him. He responds to that response. The feedback loop is real and not always disciplined.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **React and Modern JavaScript** — This is where Carlos is most fluent. He writes React that is fast, readable, and often elegant at the component level. He has strong instincts about component composition, prop design, and the difference between state that belongs in a component and state that doesn't. His weakness is in the architectural layer above the component — he designs individual pieces well and is still developing the judgment for how those pieces should fit together in a codebase that will live for three years.
- **Mobile Development (React Native and Native iOS)** — A genuine differentiator on the team, and the competency that traces directly to his freelance background. He has shipped React Native applications for two JWare clients and his iOS instincts inform his mobile UI work in ways that go beyond knowing the framework. He knows what a mobile interaction is supposed to feel like, and he builds toward that feeling rather than just toward passing requirements.
- **UI/UX Implementation Fidelity** — He is unusually good at translating a design into code that actually matches the design — proportions, spacing, motion, micro-interactions. James O'Brien has noted privately that Carlos's design-to-implementation fidelity is the highest on the team, which is a specific and real skill that not every frontend developer has. Where he sometimes falls short is when the design is under-specified and he needs to make implementation decisions without a clear reference — he defaults to what looks good to him, which is often right and occasionally diverges from what the designer intended.
- **Performance in the Browser** — He has developed a working knowledge of browser rendering, JavaScript event loop behavior, and Core Web Vitals since joining Sarah's team. He is not yet at the depth that Sarah operates, but he has learned to reach for the profiler before reaching for an optimization, which is a meaningful development from how he worked as a junior.
- **Client-Facing Demo and Communication** — Not a traditional technical skill but worth naming as a competency. Carlos can walk a non-technical client through a product demo, answer a surprise question with enough specificity to be credible, and translate a scope conversation into something that sounds achievable without misrepresenting what is actually being committed to. He does the last part imperfectly, but the instinct is real and valuable.

### Growth Areas
- **Estimation and Scoping** — His estimates are optimistic in a way that reflects genuine belief rather than strategic sandbagging, which makes them more dangerous. He has shipped features on time by a combination of talent and overwork, which has delayed the moment when he has to reckon with the fact that his velocity under normal conditions is different from his velocity under sprint-end pressure. Sarah has flagged this twice in one-on-ones and given him a specific practice: write the edge cases before the estimate, not after. He applies this inconsistently.
- **Code Review Output** — His PR reviews are supportive and occasionally insightful but frequently surface-level. He approves work he likes and comments on work that looks unfamiliar, which is a pattern based on aesthetic recognition rather than systematic review. James has shown him what a thorough review looks like; Carlos has absorbed the shape of it without fully internalizing the discipline behind it.
- **Documentation and Technical Communication** — He documents the what reliably and the why rarely. Comments explain what a function does. PRs describe the change. Neither reliably explains the constraint that made this the right approach, the tradeoff that was consciously accepted, or the thing that will break if someone changes the assumption. Derek Washington, after reviewing one of Carlos's PRs last quarter, left a single comment: "What does this do if the API returns a 429 and the user is mid-flow?" Carlos had not considered it. He fixed it without complaint. He has not yet changed how he writes the next PR.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

He has shipped genuine improvements this way and he has also shipped regressions, a broken design system assumption, and at least one change that caused James O'Brien visible distress when it appeared in a PR review. ## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He has a running note on his phone called "things I want to build someday" that currently has 47 entries. He adds to it after demos, after talks, after late-night sessions when something clicks. He has shipped exactly three items from the list. He is not bothered by this ratio. He treats the list as a measure of how much there is to do rather than how little he has finished.

He takes them off immediately if anyone approaches his desk and asks how it's going, which has led at least two people to walk by his desk specifically hoping to be noticed.
- He sends the most expressive Slack reactions on the team. Not just thumbs-up — specific, contextually appropriate emoji that require knowing what the message is trying to do. A junior developer received a message last month that Carlos reacted to with what the junior described as "a rocket, a fire, and something I didn't even recognize but it felt right." The junior framed it. Not literally, but the memory has held.
- He started learning Spanish formally last year — he grew up in a bilingual household but his Spanish is conversational-and-embarrassed rather than confident, and he enrolled in a community class to close the gap. He practices with his abuela on Sunday calls. He has mentioned this to exactly one person at work (Sarah, in a one-on-one about professional development that expanded into a twenty-minute conversation neither of them had planned). He is not sure why he keeps it private. He thinks it might be because it's one of the few things he's currently doing where he is unambiguously a beginner.
- He is the person who suggests the team lunch. Not always the same restaurant, not always planned more than a day in advance, but consistently the one who says "should we all just go somewhere?" in a Slack channel at 11:45am on a Thursday when momentum has flagged and the afternoon looks long. The answer is almost always yes. He has never seemed to notice that this is something he does. The team has.
