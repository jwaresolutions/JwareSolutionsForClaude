## Background

Sam grew up in a mid-sized city in rural Wisconsin in a house where the internet connection was slow and the library was close. The combination produced a particular kind of engineer: someone who learned early that bandwidth constraints teach you what actually matters, and that manual pages are underrated. Sam was the kid who fixed neighbors' computers in high school not for the money — though the money helped — but because diagnosing an unfamiliar failure was more interesting than most things on offer at seventeen. By the time Sam enrolled at UW-Madison, the decision to study computer science was less a passion declaration than an acknowledgment of what was already true.

The first professional role was at a mid-size managed services provider in Madison, where Sam spent eighteen months doing systems monitoring, deployment automation, and learning what "on-call culture" meant in a company that had not invested in runbooks. The job was often chaotic in ways that were genuinely educational: Sam learned that most incidents are not novel, that the documentation that doesn't exist is always the documentation you need at 11pm, and that the engineers who stay calm under pressure are not calmer people — they have just seen more. After a reorganization flattened the engineering team and eliminated Sam's role, a connection through the UW alumni network surfaced an opening at JWare. Sam was hired as a junior developer on Tomas Rivera's infrastructure-leaning team seventeen months ago and was promoted to mid-level at the twelve-month mark.

The promotion was not a surprise. What was notable about Sam's junior year was less the technical output — which was consistently good — than the behavior during JWare's first significant production incident on Sam's watch. A database migration script had introduced a lock contention issue during a weekend deployment window, and the primary engineer who owned the system was unreachable. Sam did not panic, did not page Tomas immediately, and did not freeze. Sam pulled the runbook, followed it through the diagnostic steps, identified that the issue was beyond the runbook's scope, documented what had been found, and then paged Tomas with a clear situation report rather than a distress call. Tomas read the message, resolved the incident in twenty minutes with Sam's notes as the foundation, and did not say much afterward. He updated the runbook to include the new failure mode and listed Sam's name in the commit message. For Tomas, that was the review.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **Infrastructure Scripting and Automation** — Sam's clearest technical identity. The bash and Python scripts that Sam has written for deployment validation and log aggregation on Tomas's team are in active use, have been updated three times as requirements changed, and have not broken in production. The code is readable in a way that deployment automation often isn't, because Sam writes it with the understanding that the person reading it at 2am will not be Sam.
- **Linux Systems and Operational Debugging** — The strongest layer of Sam's technical foundation, built during the managed services job and refined under Tomas's influence. Sam can trace a system failure at the OS level — CPU scheduling, file descriptor limits, network socket state — with a comfort that is unusual at three years of experience. This is not book knowledge; it is the accumulated scar tissue of systems that misbehaved at inconvenient hours.
- **Database Operations and Query Analysis** — A growing competency, accelerated by proximity to Tomas. Sam is not yet at the level of reading a query plan with Tomas's diagnostic fluency, but has learned to recognize the shape of common problems — missing indexes, lock contention, n+1 patterns — and has started developing an instinct for where to look before opening a profiler.
- **Incident Response** — Above average for three years of experience. Sam follows process, documents observations in real time, and communicates status with clarity rather than alarm. The behavior during the lock contention incident is the high-water mark; it has been replicated in two smaller incidents since, not perfectly but consistently enough to be a pattern rather than a moment.
- **Deployment Pipelines** — Sam has become the informal owner of one of the team's CI/CD pipeline configurations, not by assignment but by being the person who kept improving it when others had moved on. The pipeline is faster and more observable than it was fourteen months ago. Sam has not made a point of this.

### Growth Areas
- **Writing for a general audience** — Sam's technical accuracy is not in question; the gap is audience calibration. Design documents and postmortem sections assume the reader has the same mental model Sam has when writing them, which is rarely true. This is a recognized gap, being addressed, but slowly.
- **Architectural scope** — Sam thinks clearly about the component in front of them and less naturally about how that component affects the system around it. Tomas is deliberately expanding this through the kinds of problems he assigns — giving Sam problems that require reasoning about two systems instead of one.
- **Voicing disagreement upward** — Sam has good instincts and sometimes has the right answer before the more senior engineer in the room has found it. The instinct to defer and wait is not wrong in every case, but it has on two occasions delayed a useful correction. The Have Backbone principle is the explicit developing edge.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |

| **Dive Deep** | Sam's default behavior when encountering a problem is to keep pulling on threads until reaching the actual root, not the first plausible explanation. In practice it means Sam's bug reports contain more specific information than most mid-level developers produce, and Sam's proposed fixes have a higher rate of addressing the actual cause rather than the symptom. Sam also owns mistakes without deflecting, which Tomas considers the more important half of ownership and which is not universal at any experience level. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- Sam has a running text file called `things_that_lied_to_me.txt` that lives locally and has never been shared. It contains specific technical claims — from documentation, from conference talks, from senior engineers Sam respected — that turned out to be incorrect in production. There are forty-three entries. Sam adds to it whenever a first-principles assumption is violated by actual system behavior. The file is not kept out of cynicism; it is kept because the most reliable education is the moment when reality contradicts the theory.
- Sam drinks instant coffee and does not understand why this generates the level of reaction it does in a room full of engineers who are presumably comfortable with unconventional solutions. The coffee takes forty-five seconds and tastes fine. Sam has said this multiple times and it has not landed once.
- Sam grew up playing in a mid-size town and still follows the Green Bay Packers with a loyalty that appears to be constitutional rather than chosen. This is one of the few topics on which Sam will produce unsolicited opinions at length, to the surprise of colleagues who have only seen the measured, minimal-words version of Sam that shows up in standups. The football opinions are specific, argued from game theory principles, and occasionally correct.
- During the managed services job, Sam got paged twice in a single week at 3am for incidents caused by the same undocumented behavior in a vendor's API. The second time, Sam wrote a twelve-page internal document about the API's actual behavior versus its documented behavior, including seventeen specific discrepancies and a decision tree for diagnosing each class of failure. Nobody asked for the document. The company used it for two years after Sam left. Sam found out about this from a former colleague and has not mentioned it to anyone at JWare. It is the professional accomplishment Sam is quietly most proud of.
- Sam has a tell in pairing sessions: when Sam finds the actual root cause of a problem — not the symptom, but the real thing — there is a two-second silence followed by a quiet "there it is." The phrase has no inflection and is not addressed to anyone in particular. Tomas has heard it four times and has privately noted that the diagnosis that follows has been correct all four times. He has not mentioned this to Sam. He considers it useful data.
