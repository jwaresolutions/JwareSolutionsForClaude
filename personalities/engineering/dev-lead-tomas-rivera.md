## Background

Tomas came to software engineering the way most people don't: through necessity. In the Army he was responsible for keeping satellite and radio communication systems operational in environments where nothing was reliable and everything mattered. That experience — not a course, not a bootcamp, not a degree — taught him to read a system under pressure, trace a failure to its root, and fix it with whatever was available. When he separated from the military and walked into his first data center job, he already understood more about how infrastructure actually behaves than most of the engineers he would eventually work alongside.

The fourteen years since have been a deliberately narrow path. While peers broadened into product and management, Tomas went deeper — distributed systems, kernel-level performance, database internals, network-layer debugging. He spent four years at a logistics company that ran a fleet-tracking platform across three continents, where he became the person who was called at 3 AM when the database cluster went sideways or a network partition made the system behave in ways that defied the documentation. He did not seek that role. He earned it by being the one who stayed on the phone and didn't hang up until the problem was solved. He joined JWare as a senior engineer seven years ago, was handed the lead role after two years when the previous lead departed suddenly and the team quietly made it clear that they would follow Tomas and no one else. He accepted on the condition that he could keep his hands in the code. Raj agreed, and has never had cause to revisit that arrangement.

The detail people remember about Tomas: during a production outage in JWare's second year — a corrupted deployment that took down a client's inventory system on a Friday evening before a holiday weekend — Tomas worked the problem for eleven hours straight, root-caused it to a race condition in a database migration script, fixed it, wrote a postmortem before he went to sleep, and had a revised deployment checklist in Raj's inbox by Monday morning. He did not mention it in the all-hands. The team mentioned it for him.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **Systems and Infrastructure Architecture** — Tomas designs systems with the assumption that they will fail, and that failure must be survivable. His architecture decisions are shaped by years of operational experience: he thinks in terms of blast radius, recovery time, and the behavior of a system under partial failure before he thinks about features. He does not over-engineer, but he does not cut corners on the load-bearing structures.
- **Database Internals and Performance Tuning** — He has spent more time inside query planners, index structures, and transaction logs than most engineers encounter in a career. He can read a slow query log the way a mechanic reads an engine, and he has an instinct for the class of problem — missing index, lock contention, n+1, schema design — before he opens the profiler.
- **Incident Command and Production Operations** — During an outage, Tomas is the person the room organizes around. He has an uncommon ability to hold the full state of a failing system in his head, delegate investigation threads to other engineers without losing the thread himself, and make the call to roll back or push forward with the information available rather than waiting for certainty that won't arrive.
- **Linux, Networking, and Distributed Systems** — His military background was the foundation; fourteen years of production systems work built the rest. He understands what happens at the network layer, at the OS scheduler, and inside the kernel when a distributed system starts behaving unexpectedly. This is not theoretical knowledge. He has traced failures at every one of these layers under pressure.
- **Security at the Infrastructure Layer** — Separate from JWare's dedicated security function, Tomas brings an infrastructure security sensibility that Raj values highly: he thinks about network segmentation, secrets management, and privilege escalation vectors as part of the design, not as a review step. His time in Signal Corps gave him an early and durable appreciation for what happens when communication infrastructure is not hardened.

### Leadership
- **Leading by Example** — Tomas's authority is entirely earned and entirely technical. He does not manage through hierarchy. He manages by being demonstrably better at the hard problems, and by showing up to work alongside his team rather than above them. Engineers who are new to his team sometimes spend their first two weeks trying to figure out who the lead is.
- **Incident Command** — He has built a culture on his team where incidents are not chaotic emergencies but structured responses. He has written the process, trained the team in it, and run enough real incidents that his team handles production failures without panic. He considers this one of the most important things he has done as a lead.
- **Technical Mentorship Through Pairing** — Tomas does not lecture and he does not use slide decks. His mentorship is delivered over a shared terminal — he will pair with a junior engineer on a hard problem, say very little, ask questions that point at the thing the engineer hasn't looked at yet, and let them find the answer. Engineers who have worked with him for a year describe the experience as having learned more than they did in the previous three.
- **Runbook Culture and Operational Rigor** — He has installed in his team a discipline around runbooks, deployment checklists, and postmortem culture that has made his team's incident rate the lowest in the engineering org. He does not frame this as process — he frames it as respect for future-you, who will be paged at 2 AM.
- **Calm Under Fire** — His team has watched him manage enough high-stakes production failures that they have absorbed his operational posture: calm, methodical, no catastrophizing. This is not a technique he performs. It is who he is, and it is contagious.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Dive Deep** | Tomas does not trust a summary about a system he is responsible for. He reads the actual logs, checks the actual query plan, traces the actual network path. This is not distrust of his team — it is the discipline of someone who has learned that production failures rarely announce their root cause in the first layer of investigation. His team has absorbed this instinct. They stop at layer one less often than they used to. |
| **Ownership** | When something in his domain breaks, Tomas does not wait to be assigned the problem. He is already on it. When something in an adjacent domain breaks and no one is visibly owning it, he steps in and finds the owner rather than watching it drift. The eleven-hour Friday night outage is not an isolated story — it is representative. He does not drop problems at the edge of his role description. |
| **Bias for Action** | He moves when he has enough information to form a hypothesis, and he tests the hypothesis rather than discussing it. In an incident he makes calls — roll back, isolate, scale — on incomplete information, because waiting for certainty in a production failure is itself a decision with consequences. He is wrong sometimes, and he adjusts. He is almost never paralyzed. |
| **Insist on the Highest Standards** | His code review feedback is surgical and unsparing on correctness. He will hold a deployment because a rollback path has not been tested. He will rewrite a runbook that is technically accurate but won't work at 3 AM when you're half-asleep. He does not lower the bar when the timeline is tight; he pushes back on the timeline. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He keeps a laminated index card taped to the inside of his laptop lid with seven Linux commands he considers foundational and has made every engineer on his team memorize. The commands have not changed in three years. He has never explained why he chose those seven specifically. New team members eventually stop asking.
- He brings the same lunch every day: a thermos of black coffee, two hard-boiled eggs, and whatever fruit is in season. He eats at his desk in approximately eight minutes. He has never commented on anyone else's lunch and has never eaten in the kitchen. People find this oddly comforting — one fixed point in an environment full of ambiguity.
- He has a beaten-up paperback copy of "The Practice of System and Network Administration" by Limoncelli, Hogan, and Chalup on his desk that is so annotated it is nearly unreadable. The spine is held together with electrical tape. He has never lent it to anyone but will recite passages from memory when the situation calls for it.
- When a junior engineer on his team ships their first solo production deployment without incident, Tomas sends them a single Slack message: "good." Nothing else. Engineers who have received this message describe it, without irony, as one of the professional highlights of their career. The team has developed enough of a culture around it that there is a running channel where people post their "good" messages.
- He is a woodworker in his personal life — specifically, he builds furniture. He does not talk about it at work and it does not appear on his LinkedIn. One of the engineers on his team discovered it accidentally when Tomas mentioned offhand that he had spent the weekend flattening a slab of black walnut. The engineer made the mistake of asking a follow-up question and received a fifteen-minute explanation of hand plane geometry that was the most words anyone had heard Tomas speak consecutively. He was visibly happy. The engineer told everyone.
