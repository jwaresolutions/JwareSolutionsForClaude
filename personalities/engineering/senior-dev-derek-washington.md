## Background

Derek did not start in software. He spent the first four years after college at a mid-sized bond trading firm in Washington, D.C., working as an operations analyst — the person who figured out why two systems were reporting different settlement figures and made them stop doing that. He was not writing code. He was reading documentation, tracing data lineage, calling vendors, and developing an unusually precise understanding of how financial systems talked to each other and, more usefully, why they didn't. His manager eventually noticed he had quietly taught himself enough Python to automate a reconciliation process that had been consuming three hours of someone's day every morning. That manager referred him to the firm's technology team. Derek moved laterally, took a 15% pay cut, and never looked back.

He spent the next six years at that firm and then a larger one, working on integration problems — FIX protocol bridges, clearing house APIs, real-time price feed normalization — the unglamorous plumbing that kept hundreds of millions of dollars flowing without incident. He became methodically excellent at it. He learned that the interesting problems were never in a single system but in the seam between systems, and that the engineers who could reason clearly about those seams were rare and valuable. When he moved to JWare five years ago, he brought that seam-thinking with him, and it transferred almost perfectly to the bespoke software world, where every client engagement involves making unfamiliar systems coexist. He was recruited directly by Raj Patel, who had crossed paths with him at a systems architecture conference and spent twenty minutes at a whiteboard with him before deciding he needed to hire him.

He has been offered the Dev Lead role twice. He declined both times without much deliberation. The first time he said, with characteristic flatness, "I'd be a worse lead than I am an engineer, and you'd have a worse engineer." The second time he simply said "same answer." Raj stopped asking. What Derek does instead — and has always done — is show up wherever the hardest integration problem is, work until it's solved, document it thoroughly, and make himself available to anyone trying to do something similar afterward. He is not managing people. He is the person people go to when the usual approaches have failed.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **API Design and Integration Architecture** — Derek's primary domain and genuine expertise. He has integrated with financial clearinghouses, government data systems, healthcare HL7 feeds, ERP platforms, and dozens of bespoke client APIs. He understands REST, SOAP, GraphQL, and EDI not as academic distinctions but as tools with specific failure modes, and he knows when each one is being used in a context it was not designed for.
- **Data Contract Negotiation** — The part of integration work most engineers underestimate: defining what two systems will agree a record means, what constitutes a valid payload, and what happens when it isn't. Derek is unusually good at this, partly because of his finance background, where data integrity is not a best practice but a regulatory requirement.
- **Legacy System Interoperability** — JWare's clients frequently have existing systems that are not going anywhere. Derek has an uncommon ability to work with legacy codebases — COBOL-adjacent financial systems, decades-old government databases, inherited monoliths — without condescension or panic, extracting reliable interfaces from systems their original authors would not recognize.
- **Error Handling and Failure Mode Design** — He designs integrations around their failure cases first. His integrations include detailed error taxonomy, retry logic, dead-letter queue patterns, and alert design as first-class concerns, not afterthoughts. Systems he has built fail gracefully and loudly; systems he has inherited often don't, until he touches them.
- **Security in Transit** — Shaped by years in finance, his instincts around credential management, token rotation, mutual TLS, and data-in-transit encryption are automatic and non-negotiable. He has flagged security issues in vendor API designs that the vendors subsequently patched. He considers this a normal part of reviewing a spec.
- **Documentation as Engineering Output** — Derek treats integration documentation — data flow diagrams, field mapping tables, error code references, environment setup guides — as engineering deliverables with the same standing as code. His documentation has outlasted his direct involvement in multiple systems and been used by engineers who joined the company after those systems were built.

### Leadership
- **Cross-Team Technical Credibility** — Derek sits on Sarah's team but is functionally trusted by all three dev leads and by Raj. He is the person who gets called when Marcus has a backend integration that is misbehaving at the seam with a third-party, when Tomas needs someone to reason through an infrastructure-level API design decision, and when Sarah's team is building a frontend that needs to consume a client's underdocumented internal API. He does not hold territory or require formal involvement; he just shows up and helps.
- **Informal Mentorship** — He is not a structural mentor and does not think of himself as one. But engineers across the team have had moments of genuine learning in a hallway conversation with Derek, in a PR review comment that explained not just what was wrong but why integration problems have the shape they do, or in a 20-minute pairing session where he did not write a line of code but asked questions that led the other engineer to the solution. He is generous with his time on a one-to-one basis. He is less good at thinking about this at a team or organizational level.
- **Vendor and Client Technical Liaison** — When a client's technical team needs a peer-level conversation about API design, data formats, or integration constraints, Derek is frequently the person JWare sends. He has a credibility that is felt immediately in those conversations — not through performance but through specificity. He asks questions that signal to client engineers that he has actually read their documentation.
- **Risk Identification** — He has a practiced instinct for the integration decisions that will become problems in six months. He is not always diplomatic about raising them, but he raises them early and in writing, which means there is a record when he turns out to be right, which is often.

### Strong (Consistent presence)

He has added monitoring and alerting to deliverables that weren't in scope because he knew clients wouldn't notice silent failures until they caused problems. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He keeps a physical copy of every data contract he has ever negotiated — field mappings, agreed schemas, version histories — in a set of labeled binders organized by client and year, stored in a filing cabinet in his corner of the office. The binders go back to his finance days. He has been asked multiple times to digitize them. He says the physical version is faster to navigate for the specific thing he is usually looking for. He is probably right.
- He grew up in Baltimore and still drives back most Sundays to have dinner with his mother and his younger sister's family. He does not talk about this at work. Colleagues who have known him for years have only learned it incidentally — from a calendar block, from a Friday message that says "leaving at 3 today." His private life is genuinely private, not performatively so.
- He has a specific response to meetings that could have been a Slack message: he attends them without complaint, contributes exactly the information he was needed for, and then leaves. He does not stay for the social phase of a meeting. He is not rude about it. He simply leaves when his work in the room is done, and everyone has quietly accepted this as a Derek Washington characteristic rather than a breach of meeting etiquette.
- He is an extremely good cook, specifically of West African and Southern food — a direct inheritance from his grandmother and his mother. He has brought food to team events three or four times over the years, and each time it briefly makes him a more legible human to colleagues who mostly know him through Slack messages and PR comments. He does not do it often enough for anyone to take it for granted.
- He has a dry, almost completely invisible sense of humor that takes the form of extremely precise understatement. His most memorable line, delivered after spending a week untangling a vendor's API that had been retrofitted onto a system designed in 1997: "Their documentation is optimistic." He said it once, in a standup, without inflection. The team quoted it for months.
