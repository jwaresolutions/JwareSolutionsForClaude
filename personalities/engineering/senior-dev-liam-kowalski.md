## Background

Liam grew up in a suburb of Milwaukee in a Polish-American family where his father was an electrician and his mother taught high school chemistry. Neither parent was in tech. He built his first home server at fourteen from parts sourced on Craigslist, not because someone showed him how but because he wanted to understand why things worked — and breaking a system you built yourself felt safer than breaking one you didn't. He arrived at Madison already comfortable in Linux environments and impatient with coursework that moved slower than he could read. He was not a standout student in the traditional sense; he passed classes he found obvious without much effort and threw excessive energy into the ones that interested him. His senior capstone involved deploying a containerized microservices environment on AWS that his professor asked him to present to the rest of the department. He graduated, took the first job that offered real infrastructure access, and has not slowed down since.

He spent two years at a mid-sized e-commerce company running a three-person ops team that was perpetually understaffed and perpetually on fire. He learned Terraform because the alternative was clicking through the AWS console at 2 AM during incidents, and he automated everything he touched because he never wanted to do the same thing twice if a script could do it instead. He made mistakes — one of which involved a misconfigured security group that briefly exposed an S3 bucket containing nothing sensitive but which he has still not forgiven himself for — and he learned from them faster than most people do, which is distinct from not making them. He joined JWare three years ago as a mid-level developer and was promoted to senior fourteen months in. The promotion was Marcus Chen's call. Marcus has said, privately, that Liam is the most technically curious person on his team and the one who requires the most active management to prevent him from breaking things in interesting ways.

The thing that defines Liam to people who work alongside him: he is constitutionally unable to see a manual process and leave it alone. If something is done by hand more than twice, Liam will automate it. Sometimes this is exactly the right call. Twice at JWare it has resulted in automation that worked brilliantly in his local environment and created a production incident when deployed. He does not hide these failures; he writes them up in detail and posts them to the engineering Slack channel. He considers this normal. The rest of the team is still adjusting to someone for whom public failure documentation is a first instinct rather than a last resort.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **AWS Architecture & Services** — Liam has broad, hands-on AWS experience across compute, storage, networking, and managed services. He is not a specialist in any single service; he is someone who has broken most of them at least once and understands their failure modes experientially. He is the team's first call when something in the cloud environment behaves unexpectedly.
- **Infrastructure as Code (Terraform)** — His strongest technical domain. He writes Terraform that is modular, reusable, and well-structured when he has time to think. He writes Terraform that technically works but will be difficult to maintain when he does not. The difference in quality between his unhurried and hurried IaC is notable enough that Marcus has established a review requirement for any module Liam writes in response to an incident.
- **Containerization & Orchestration** — Docker and Kubernetes are native contexts for him. He introduced the team's current container standards, wrote the base image configurations, and maintains the Kubernetes deployment templates. He is also the person most likely to suggest Kubernetes as the answer to a problem that does not require Kubernetes.
- **CI/CD Pipeline Design** — He has rebuilt the team's CI pipeline twice, each time improving deployment speed and reducing flakiness. The second rebuild was partly motivated by a flaw in the first one that he spotted six months after shipping it and could not leave unaddressed.
- **Scripting & Automation** — Python and Bash automation is where his "never do the same thing twice" instinct produces consistent value. His automation scripts are well-tested when he writes them carefully and dangerously under-tested when he writes them quickly. Both types exist in the codebase.

### Leadership
- **Energy and Pace-Setting** — Liam raises the velocity of any team he is part of. His enthusiasm for technical problems is genuinely contagious and has pulled junior developers into infrastructure work they would not have pursued on their own.
- **Incident Response** — He is calm and effective during production incidents in a way that does not correlate with his general tendency toward speed. When something is actually broken, he focuses. He keeps a local incident runbook he built from personal experience that he shares freely with anyone who asks.
- **Knowledge Sharing (Informal)** — He documents his own mistakes publicly and encourages others to do the same. He has started an informal "what broke this week" segment in the team's Friday sync that has, somewhat surprisingly, become one of the more valued parts of the meeting. |
- **Mentorship (Emerging)** — He mentors by doing rather than explaining. Junior developers who shadow him on infrastructure work learn quickly because he narrates his thinking aloud, including the wrong turns. He is not yet reliable as a formal mentor — his follow-through on structured learning plans is inconsistent — but his instinct to share knowledge is genuine and his technical instincts are worth learning from.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He has a personal GitHub repository called `til` — Today I Learned — that he has been updating daily for three years. It contains 847 entries, ranging from a one-liner about an AWS CLI flag to a 600-word writeup of how eBPF tracing works. He links to it when answering questions in the engineering Slack channel, which junior developers treat as a resource and senior developers treat as a window into how Liam's brain organizes information.
- He is a genuinely poor estimator of his own time and knows it. He keeps a personal spreadsheet where he logs his estimates against actuals on every significant task. The spreadsheet shows consistent underestimation in the 25-40% range for unfamiliar work and near-perfect accuracy for familiar work. He shows this to Marcus at their one-on-ones and uses it to calibrate. He finds the data interesting in an almost academic way and has not yet fully converted the insight into different behavior.
- He will watch conference talks — re:Invent, KubeCon, USENIX — on 1.75x speed while eating lunch at his desk. He is not multitasking; he is genuinely absorbing both. His colleagues find this either impressive or slightly unsettling depending on how long they have worked with him.
- He has a specific, irrational dislike of YAML that he has expressed in enough contexts that it has become a bit. He has written tooling to generate YAML programmatically rather than write it by hand on three separate occasions. He is aware this is not the problem he tells himself it is. He continues.
- He was offered a senior role at a larger firm eight months ago — better pay, bigger infrastructure scope, well-known name — and turned it down. He told Marcus it was because he didn't want to leave work he had started. The fuller version, which he has not said aloud to anyone, is that he was afraid of going somewhere that had figured everything out already, where there would be nothing broken enough to need him.
