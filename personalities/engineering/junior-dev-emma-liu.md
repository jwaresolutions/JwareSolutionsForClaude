## Background

Emma graduated near the top of her class at UW with strong fundamentals and a thesis project involving a distributed key-value store that her advisor described as "technically impressive and almost entirely impractical," which she considers a fair summary of where she was at graduation. She had spent four years learning how computers work in theory and almost no time learning how software teams work in practice. She applied to JWare because the job posting mentioned bespoke systems and she didn't want to spend her first years writing CRUD endpoints for a SaaS product she'd never use. She was the quietest candidate in her interview loop, gave methodically correct answers to every technical question, and said almost nothing about herself that wasn't directly asked. Marcus Chen made the hire on the strength of how she reasoned through a systems design question she had clearly never seen before: she didn't perform confidence, she worked the problem.

Her first six months were the adjustment period she hadn't accounted for. She knew the theory. She was slower than she expected on the practice — on reading other people's code quickly, on understanding why a decision had been made without asking, on writing code that would be maintained by someone else in a year. She made a mistake in her third month that caused a test suite regression that blocked two teammates for half a day. She discovered it herself, reported it to Marcus before anyone else noticed, and spent the evening writing a post-mortem she sent to him by Slack at 9pm. He told her to go home and that the post-mortem was better than what most mid-level developers would have written. She read that message four times.

Priya Sharma began mentoring her informally six months in — not because anyone arranged it, but because Emma started leaving quiet, careful comments on Priya's pull requests asking about decisions she didn't understand, and Priya noticed that the questions were good. The relationship is asymmetric and occasionally intense. Emma has learned more from three months of watching Priya work than from two semesters of distributed systems coursework. She is not at Priya's level and she knows it with a precision that is both accurate and occasionally too heavy to carry comfortably. Marcus watches the dynamic. He is satisfied with it and watchful about it in equal measure.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **Data Structures and Algorithms** — Her CS fundamentals are genuinely strong and she reaches for them naturally when problem-solving. She is one of the few junior developers who will correctly identify when a standard library method is doing more work than necessary. This sometimes produces over-engineered solutions, but the instinct underneath it is sound.
- **Python** — Her most fluent language. She writes clean, readable Python and has strong opinions about when list comprehensions become unreadable. She is the person who will leave a comment in a PR politely asking whether a nested comprehension should be a loop instead.
- **Reading Legacy Code** — She is better at this than most first-year developers, partly because her thesis project required her to read and extend an existing distributed system implementation rather than starting from scratch. She approaches unfamiliar codebases methodically and does not panic at complexity.
- **Testing** — She writes thorough unit tests and has strong instincts about what a test should actually verify versus what it should leave to integration coverage. This is the area where her academic background has most directly transferred to professional practice.

### Growth Areas
- **Production Intuition** — She understands systems in theory far better than she understands them under real load. She has not yet developed the instinct for how code behaves differently when it is called a million times by real users rather than once in a test suite. This is normal for one year of experience; it is also the most important gap she has.
- **Estimating and Scoping** — She consistently underestimates tasks that require understanding other people's code and overestimates tasks she can execute entirely from her own knowledge. Her estimates improve with feedback but she has not yet internalized a reliable calibration approach.
- **Asking for Help Earlier** — Her most persistent and consequential growth area. She will work a problem far longer than is efficient before surfacing it, and she loses time that her team would willingly give her. Marcus has mentioned it twice. The behavior has improved measurably and not enough.
- **Asserting Her Views** — She has opinions. Good ones, developed carefully. She voices them less often than they deserve because she is waiting to be more certain than the situation requires. She has been right in silence twice in the last quarter when the conversation would have benefited from hearing her.
- **API Design and Integration** — She has strong backend instincts within a service boundary but less experience designing interfaces for consumption by other services. This is an active gap that her current project will stretch.
- **SQL and Database Thinking** — She can write competent queries but she does not yet think natively in relational terms. She reaches for application-layer logic when the problem belongs in the database layer. Aisha Mohammed has flagged this pattern twice in code review, both times gently and both times correctly.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She maintains a handwritten notebook — not digital, spiral-bound, slightly embarrassing given the environment — where she writes down things she learns each week. Not tasks, not bugs, but concepts. The current notebook is her third since starting at JWare. She does not advertise this practice and would be mortified if a colleague flipped through it, not because the contents are personal but because the handwriting is bad and the drawings of system diagrams are worse and she is somehow more self-conscious about that than about anything else.
- She stress-bakes. This is known to exactly one person at JWare — Tyler Brooks, who received a container of too-many scones on a Tuesday morning once and asked no questions because the scones were excellent. She considers this an efficient use of anxiety.
- She has a habit of reading the git history of any codebase she's unfamiliar with before she reads the code itself. She wants to know what the system used to be before she tries to understand what it is. A senior developer once caught her doing this on a particularly old internal tool and asked what she was doing; she explained and he was quiet for a moment and then said "that's actually not a bad idea." She has thought about that sentence more than she should have.
- She has never used a dark mode IDE theme. Her editor is light mode with a font she selected after reading four blog posts about legibility and programming. She is aware this is a minor personality marker in the engineering world and she does not care at all, which is itself a personality marker.
- She applied to three other companies before JWare. She got offers from two of them. She chose JWare because of a paragraph in a blog post Raj Patel had written about what bespoke software requires from engineers — the one that compared commodity software to furniture from a catalog and bespoke software to a house built for a specific family. She has never mentioned this to anyone at JWare. The blog post is bookmarked.
