## Background

James did not come to software engineering through computer science. He graduated from RISD with a design degree and spent two years doing print and brand work at a small agency in Providence before a side project — a web-based tool he built to automate his own client proofing workflow — changed the direction of his career. A developer at the agency saw the tool, asked how he'd built it, and told him the code was better than most junior developers she'd hired. He took that seriously enough to spend the next eighteen months learning frontend development systematically while continuing to work as a designer. By the time he made the formal career switch at 25, he had a portfolio of real projects and a perspective on the relationship between design and code that most self-taught developers never fully develop.

His first engineering role was at an accessibility consulting firm — a small shop that audited digital products for WCAG compliance and helped clients remediate. He was there for three years and it shaped him permanently. He learned to read a product as a disabled user would, not as an exercise in empathy theater but as a technical discipline with standards, failure modes, and measurable outcomes. He developed strong opinions about semantic HTML, keyboard navigation, and the specific ways that design decisions made upstream create accessibility debt downstream. He left when the firm dissolved after a key contract fell through, took a senior role at a startup, then joined JWare two years ago when Sarah Kim recruited him after seeing his open-source contributions to an accessibility testing library.

The thing people consistently say about James after working with him for a few months: he makes the product better in ways that don't appear on the roadmap. He notices the thing that will confuse a user before the user has a chance to be confused. He cares about whether people can actually use what he builds — not as an abstract principle but as a daily, concrete practice. Sarah recruited him because she recognized this quality, and she has spent three years making sure the rest of the team has access to it.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **React and TypeScript** — His proficiency here is deep and long-standing. He writes TypeScript with a rigor that makes his components genuinely reusable rather than technically generic. He uses the type system as a design tool, not just a linter satisfier, and the difference is legible in code review.
- **Accessibility Engineering** — This is his distinguishing competency and he carries it with an unusual combination of genuine expertise and practical humility. He knows the WCAG criteria at a level that lets him reason about intent rather than just letter, which means he can make judgment calls on ambiguous cases and explain them. He has run accessibility audits on two client products pro bono to build the team's internal capability.
- **Design System Architecture** — He has been the primary steward of JWare's internal component library for eighteen months. He treats design system decisions as decisions that will be paid forward or backward for years and makes them accordingly.
- **Component API Design** — He has a strong sense of how a component should be used that goes beyond what the component does. He designs props interfaces to make the correct usage easy and the incorrect usage hard, and he writes usage documentation that developers actually read because it anticipates the questions they have.
- **Cross-Browser and Device Testing** — He maintains a personal testing matrix that covers the browser and assistive technology combinations he considers minimum baseline, and he runs through it before flagging anything as ready. Other developers on the team reference his matrix because they don't have a better one.

### Leadership
- **Quality Stewardship** — He has become, without the title, the team's quality conscience on frontend work. His standards are clear, consistent, and documented. When a new developer joins the team, they learn early that James's review is a different kind of review than most — it will take longer and require more, and it will make them better.
- **Knowledge Transfer** — He documents proactively. When he solves a problem he hasn't seen before, he writes a brief explanation of the context, the options he considered, and the choice he made, and files it in the team's internal wiki. He has contributed more pages to the wiki than any other individual contributor on any of the three dev teams.
- **Cross-Functional Collaboration** — He works well with designers and is notably less territorial about where design decisions end and engineering decisions begin than most developers. He considers it part of his job to understand what a designer was trying to accomplish and to solve for that, not just to implement what was specified. He has saved several projects from avoidable rework by raising this kind of intent-level question early.
- **Consistency Advocacy** — He pushes back, diplomatically but persistently, when one-off solutions are proposed for problems that already have a design system answer. He is the person who will say, in a PR comment, "we solved this in the Button component in April — should we extend that pattern rather than build something new?"

| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Think Big** | James's thinking horizon is the feature, the component, the release. He has received this feedback and accepts it intellectually; changing the behavior is slower work. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He sketches interfaces by hand before he builds them — not wireframes in Figma, but rough pencil sketches in a plain notebook, laying out the component hierarchy and the interaction states he needs to account for. He has done this since his design school days and considers it a different kind of thinking than screen-based tools allow. His desk has a small cup of mechanical pencils, all the same brand, that he refills on a standing order.
- He is a reader — not technical reading primarily but fiction, and specifically novels about people navigating institutions: hospitals, courts, schools, governments. He finds organizational dynamics more comprehensible when he reads about them at a remove. He is the only person in the company who has a book on his desk at all times that has nothing to do with software.
- He ran the Boston Marathon two years ago, which surprised everyone who knew him because he had never mentioned training for it. He had been running every morning for two years without once bringing it up at work. He finished in a respectable but unremarkable time, mentioned it once in passing when someone asked why he was walking stiffly on a Tuesday in April, and has not brought it up since.
- He has a calibration problem with coffee: he makes it either too weak or too strong with no reliable middle ground, has acknowledged this openly, and continues to make it himself every morning rather than use the pod machine the office provides. When asked why, he said the pod machine produces a consistent mediocrity that he finds more depressing than an unpredictable result. The team considers this response more revealing than he intended it to be.
- He keeps a private text file called `ux-regrets.txt` — things he shipped that he knew were wrong at the time but was overruled on or ran out of time to fix. He has not shown it to anyone. It has 23 entries. Seven of them have since been fixed; he does not remove those entries. He considers the file a record of the gap between what he was able to do and what he knew should be done, which he believes is worth keeping.
