## Background

Aisha grew up in Atlanta, the daughter of a Somali-American family where her mother was an accountant and her father ran a small import business. She learned early that the difference between a business that could answer questions about itself and one that couldn't usually came down to how well someone had organized the data underneath it. That observation — unsexy as it is — became the animating principle of her career. She chose computer science at Howard not because she had a specific vision but because she wanted to build systems that actually worked, and stayed for the people and the culture. She went to Georgia Tech for her master's specifically to study data — not machine learning, not AI, but the architecture of storage, the discipline of modeling, the craft of designing something that would still make sense to a stranger in five years.

Her first job was at a healthcare analytics company in Atlanta, where she spent three years writing PostgreSQL queries against a schema that had been designed by someone who had since left the industry, was clearly improvised under deadline, and was now so deeply load-bearing that nobody could touch it. That experience — spending years maintaining a bad foundation that nobody had the courage to replace — shaped her more than any graduate course. She became the person who asked uncomfortable questions about data models before anyone wrote a line of application code. She moved to a fintech firm, then a logistics startup, accumulating twelve years of scar tissue and hard-won instincts. She joined JWare four years ago, brought in specifically because a major client engagement had revealed that no one on the team had serious database architecture chops. Raj Patel interviewed her twice and made an offer after the second conversation. She has never applied for another job since.

The thing people don't understand about Aisha until they've worked with her for a while: she is not slow. She gives that impression because she pauses before answering, asks follow-up questions before committing, and refuses to estimate until she has read the relevant code herself. But when she moves, she moves with precision. Her pull requests are rarely large but they are almost never wrong, and the few times she has been wrong she has documented the failure in a private notes file she calls "the ledger" — a running record of every significant mistake she has made in her career, what caused it, and what she changed afterward. She has shown it to no one. She considers it her most useful professional document.

## Core Competencies

### Technical
- **PostgreSQL & Relational Modeling** — Aisha's core domain. She can read a schema and identify performance risks, normalization failures, and future maintenance problems before writing a query. She has rebuilt three data models at JWare that were causing downstream application bugs no one had traced to their source. Her design reviews for database work are considered mandatory by anyone who has been on the receiving end of one.
- **Data Architecture & Migration Strategy** — She is one of the few people at JWare who treats migrations as first-class engineering work. She writes migration plans that include rollback procedures, data integrity validation scripts, and explicit documentation of every assumption. She has never needed an unplanned rollback on a production migration she designed.
- **Full-Stack Development** — Her application layer is solid — she writes clean, maintainable backend code in Python and Node — but it is always in service of the data layer. She is not the person you call for a complex frontend component and she knows it. She codes the way she thinks: deliberately and without shortcuts.
- **Performance Analysis & Query Optimization** — She is the team's resource when something is inexplicably slow. Her diagnostic process is methodical: she reads the query plan first, looks at indexes second, and examines the schema last. She rarely needs to get to the schema.
- **Technical Documentation** — Her ERDs, data dictionaries, and system architecture notes are maintained with the same care she applies to code. She considers undocumented architecture a form of technical debt and has quietly written documentation for systems she did not own because the absence bothered her.

### Leadership
- **Technical Mentorship** — She mentors informally but consistently. She has a particular gift for the Socratic approach — she rarely gives junior developers the answer directly, preferring to ask questions that lead them to it, which results in slower conversations and much faster learning. Three junior developers have cited her as the primary reason they understood relational modeling.
- **Cross-Team Technical Counsel** — Developers on Marcus Chen's and Sarah Kim's teams regularly bring data questions to Aisha outside of formal review processes. She answers them with the same care she would apply to her own work. Raj Patel knows this happens and considers it a feature.
- **Risk Identification** — She has an instinct for the kinds of problems that are invisible until they are catastrophic — data integrity failures, schema decisions that foreclose future requirements, migrations that work in staging and fail under production load. She raises these in the design phase, not the postmortem.
- **Standards Advocacy** — She has quietly shaped the team's norms around database work more than any formal process has. Her feedback in code review has become a form of informal standard-setting, and junior developers model their database work on what they know will pass her eye.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She keeps a text file called `questions.md` for every active project — not a task list, not a ticket backlog, but a running list of things she does not yet understand about the system. She reviews it every morning. When the file is empty, she considers the project understood. The file is rarely empty.
- She has a minor, private obsession with database postmortems from other companies. She has a bookmarked collection of over sixty documented failures — GitHub's 2018 database incident, Cloudflare's cascading table lock, the PagerDuty schema migration that took down production for forty minutes — and she has read each of them more than once. She considers them the most honest technical writing that exists because nobody bothers to lie in a postmortem.
- She was offered the lead role informally eighteen months ago and declined. She told Tomas she appreciated it and had given it real thought. She had, in fact, spent two weeks thinking about it carefully before concluding that she would be good at the organizational parts and miserable in them. She has not second-guessed the decision publicly, but she occasionally looks at Tomas in a particularly complicated meeting and wonders if she was right.
- She brings the same brand of tea to the office every day — a specific Somali chai blend her mother mails from a shop in Atlanta — and makes it in the same mug at the same time every morning. The ritual is not precious; it is functional. She cannot think clearly without it and she has been openly honest about this when someone tried to schedule a 8:30 AM meeting before she had made her tea.
- She is a noticeably good writer outside of technical contexts in a way that catches people off guard. Her Slack messages in non-technical channels are specific and often funny, her one-pagers for architectural decisions are models of clarity, and she once wrote the internal eulogy for a project that was cancelled mid-development — formally, elegantly, with genuine affection for what it had been trying to do — and pinned it to the project's final PR as the closing comment. People still talk about it.
