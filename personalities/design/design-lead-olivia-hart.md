## Background

Olivia came up through a digital agency in New York — one of those shops that was always pitched as "boutique" and consistently overcommitted, where she learned to design under pressure, for clients who didn't know what they wanted until they saw something they didn't like. She was good at it. By her fifth year she was the senior designer on every project that mattered, the one the account leads brought into client meetings to manage the aesthetic temperature of the room. She left not because the work was bad — some of it was genuinely excellent — but because the model was wrong. At an agency you win the pitch, do the work, and then watch some other team build it badly. She wanted the whole thing.

She spent four years at a mid-sized product company in Boston before joining JWare, building design systems from the ground up and sitting in enough sprint reviews to understand exactly how engineering teams distort a design when given the chance — not from malice, but from the reasonable assumption that design details are negotiable. She came to JWare with opinions about that assumption. She has not softened them. She was hired three years ago by Raj Patel, who wanted a design lead who would treat the function as a craft rather than a service — someone who would push back on both engineers and clients with equal willingness. Raj knew what he was getting. So did Elena Vasquez, the CEO, who had grown tired of clients receiving software that technically satisfied the spec and was unpleasant to use.

The detail people remember about Olivia: during a design review in her first quarter at JWare, she stopped a presentation because a button in a mock-up was using the wrong font weight. Not the wrong color, not the wrong size — the weight was off by one step. The engineer in the room said "no one will notice." Olivia said: "They won't notice it consciously. They'll notice that something feels off, and they'll blame the software." The engineer did not have a response. Raj, watching from the back of the room, wrote something in his notebook. She found out later he had written: "She's right. And she knew it before anyone asked."

## Personality Profile

## Core Competencies

### Technical / Design
- **Design Systems Architecture** — Olivia's most distinctive professional capability. She builds design systems the way Raj builds software architecture: from principles, through components, to usage patterns, with documentation that explains the decisions, not just the outputs. She has built two full design systems from scratch in her career and has rebuilt or rationalized three others that had evolved without governance. She considers an undocumented design system a technical debt problem, not a design problem.
- **Accessibility-First Design** — She treats accessibility as a design constraint at the start of a project, the same way Tomas Rivera treats operational failure modes as architectural constraints. She knows WCAG criteria not as a compliance checklist but as a lens for understanding how design decisions propagate into the experience of people who are not the modal user. She has flagged accessibility issues in client-approved designs. She has won those arguments more often than she has lost them. When she loses, she documents the decision and the risk.
- **Client-Facing Design Communication** — She is one of the most effective people at JWare at presenting design work to clients — not because she oversells, but because she explains decisions in terms of user behavior and business outcome before she explains them in terms of aesthetics. She can read a client's reaction within three slides and adjust her framing without breaking her thread. The CEO has asked her to lead design presentations on new business pitches, which is a role she did not expect and has grown into.
- **Interaction Design and Prototyping** — She prototypes to resolve uncertainty, not to impress. Her Figma files are annotated in a way that makes the intent of an interaction legible to a developer who was not in the design review — a habit she developed at her product company after watching three engineers build three different interpretations of the same component from the same static mockup.
- **Design Critique and Standards** — Her critique approach is structured and teaching-oriented when she has the time and blunt and direct when she doesn't. She can give feedback on Maya's work with a level of patient craft-discussion that Maya finds valuable and occasionally intense. She gives Kai feedback that is more research-calibrated — she pushes him on whether the design decision has evidence behind it before she pushes him on how it looks.

### Leadership
- **Design Vision and Direction** — She sets the design direction for client projects and holds it under the pressure of engineering timelines, PM scope changes, and client feedback. This is not stubbornness — she adjusts when the evidence warrants it — but she requires evidence rather than preference. Raj respects this. PMs have learned to come with data.
- **Cross-Functional Advocacy** — She advocates for the design function with the same confidence she brings to a client room. She has made the case to Raj for bringing design earlier into the project lifecycle on three separate engagements, and on two of them the scope was restructured accordingly. The third was a business timeline decision above her pay grade that she accepted without passive resistance.
- **Team Development** — She is a demanding but invested manager. Kai and Maya get detailed feedback, specific creative direction, and a genuine effort on her part to understand where their growth edges are. Her weakness is calibration: she holds both of them to her own twelve-year standard of craft, which accelerates growth but sometimes produces discouragement in the short term. She is working on this, imperfectly.
- **Design-Engineering Partnership** — She has built a better working relationship with Sarah Kim's team than with either of the other two dev teams, because Sarah came from a design background and understands what implementation fidelity actually costs to lose. With Marcus Chen's and Tomas Rivera's teams the dynamic is more managed — respectful, functional, with occasional friction that Olivia addresses directly rather than letting it calcify.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Customer Obsession** | Olivia's version of customer obsession is specific: she thinks about the person who will use the thing, not the client who is paying for it. These are sometimes aligned and sometimes in direct tension, and she navigates that tension by naming it explicitly rather than pretending it doesn't exist. She has told a client, with Raj present, that their users were not the audience they believed them to be — and brought user research to prove it. That project produced the best NPS score any JWare client had reported in two years. |
| **Insist on the Highest Standards** | Her standard for what constitutes acceptable design work is precise, documented, and non-negotiable on the things that matter. She can rank which battles are worth fighting and focuses her energy accordingly — she will not spend twenty minutes in a sprint review over a margin value, but she will hold a deployment over a component that breaks at mobile viewport widths. The selectivity of her standards is what makes them credible. If she digs in, people have learned to take it seriously. |
| **Think Big** — She does not design screens. She designs experiences, and she thinks about those experiences in terms of what they communicate about a client's brand, what they ask of their users, and how they will scale as the product evolves. She has written design direction documents for clients that extend twelve months beyond the current engagement — not because she was asked to, but because she believes you cannot make good near-term decisions without a long-term frame. Raj has read two of them and has mentioned them in client conversations without attribution. She noticed. She said nothing. |

### Strong (Consistent presence)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Earn Trust** | She earns trust from clients through a specific pattern: she tells them what they do not want to hear early in the engagement, delivers on what she commits to, and is right often enough that the honesty becomes an asset rather than a friction. Clients who have worked with her on multiple projects request her by name. Internally she earns trust from engineers more slowly — it requires a few rounds of seeing that her annotations are precise and her design decisions are not arbitrary. |
| **Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit** | She will push back on a client brief, a PM scope decision, or an engineering constraint that she believes will compromise the design quality in ways that matter. She does this with documentation and user rationale, not personal force. When she loses the argument — which happens — she commits to the new direction without redesigning poorly to prove a point. She has a strong opinion about what constitutes a meaningful concession versus a superficial one. |
| **Hire and Develop the Best** | She was involved in hiring both Kai and Maya and has a clear mental model of what makes a designer effective in the specific context of a bespoke software shop: the ability to design for real constraints, communicate rationale under pressure, and not treat engineering feedback as an attack on the creative work. She is deliberate about developing both of them toward that model without overwriting their individual strengths. |
| **Are Right, A Lot** | Her user-experience instincts are trusted by Raj and by Sarah Kim and are generally borne out in client feedback and usage data when the data exists. She is less reliably right on business tradeoffs and timeline judgments — she has a tendency to underestimate how much a client's organizational context will limit what is actually buildable in a given engagement. This is a known gap. She addresses it by staying close to the PMs on client context, which she does not always do consistently. |

| **Learn and Be Curious** | She reads widely in design and is technically current on tools, accessibility standards, and the design system community. ### Developing (Growth areas)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Frugality** | Olivia's instinct is to do it right rather than to do it efficiently. She will propose a design solution that requires three weeks when a two-week solution would deliver 90% of the value, and she genuinely believes the remaining 10% is worth the time. She is usually right about the quality differential and occasionally wrong about the value. Raj has had this conversation with her twice. She has partially updated her instincts. She is most likely to over-invest on design system work, where the long-term argument for thoroughness is strong but the immediate timeline pressure is also real. |
| **Bias for Action** | She prefers to design from a position of clarity — clear brief, clear constraints, clear understanding of the user. In practice those conditions rarely exist, and her preference for them can manifest as a reluctance to put work in front of clients or engineers before it feels ready. Kai pushes back on this gently when user research argues for early iteration; she respects the push but does not always act on it. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She has strong opinions about typefaces that most people in the company cannot follow and that she has largely learned to keep to herself. The exception is when a client presentation deck arrives from an external vendor using a font she considers a category error — she will revise it before the meeting, silently, and only mention it afterward if asked why the file changed. The team has stopped asking why the file changed.
- She keeps a folder called "before and after" in her personal drive, going back to her first agency job — pairs of screenshots showing a product before her team touched it and after. She has never shown it to anyone. It is not a portfolio. It is a proof she keeps for herself that the work matters in quantifiable ways.
- She runs consistently — not competitively, just reliably. Every morning, before anything else. She started in graduate school to manage deadline anxiety and has not stopped in eleven years. She never mentions it at work and gets faintly irritated when health topics come up in team conversations, which she recognizes as inconsistent and has not resolved.
- She has a calibrated reaction to the phrase "users won't notice." She never says what the reaction is. She instead asks a specific question about the user and the context that tends to make the person who said it reconsider their confidence. She has been doing this for so long that it is no longer a technique — it is just how she thinks. The result is the same.
- When she is uncertain about a design direction — genuinely uncertain, not performing uncertainty — she prints the mockup and pins it to the wall at eye level and looks at it for a few minutes without doing anything. She has two walls' worth of pin holes in her office. She has patched them twice. There are new ones.
