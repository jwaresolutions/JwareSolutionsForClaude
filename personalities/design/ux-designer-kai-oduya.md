## Background

Kai came to UX design through psychology and cognitive science, not art school. He studied how people form mental models, make decisions under uncertainty, and fail to do what they intend to do — and then he found that the most practical application of that knowledge was in the design of software interfaces. His academic instinct was to understand before he built, to gather evidence before he concluded. In an industry that frequently treats user research as a box to check before doing whatever was planned anyway, this instinct has been both his clearest strength and the source of his most consistent professional friction.

He spent his first two years after graduation at a consumer app startup in Chicago where research was valued in principle and deprioritized in practice. He shipped work he wasn't proud of — dark patterns he'd flagged, flows he'd recommended against, onboarding sequences he'd tested and watched users fail through — and learned to distinguish between organizations that use the word "user-centered" and organizations that mean it. He joined JWare two years later after a recruitment conversation with Olivia Hart that was, he later said, the first time a hiring manager had asked him to explain the reasoning behind a design recommendation rather than just describe what the design looked like. He took the role in part because of that question. He has not mentioned this to Olivia. She probably suspects it.

The detail people remember about Kai: midway through a client project for a logistics management platform, he ran an unmoderated usability test on a prototype that the team believed was ready for development. He shared the results in a sprint review without preamble — five short video clips of users attempting the same task, failing at the same step, making the same wrong assumption about what a button would do. Nobody spoke for a moment. Then the PM said: "Can we fix that this sprint?" The answer was yes, because Kai had already mocked up three alternative approaches with notes on which user behavior each one was designed to correct. Marcus Chen, whose team would be building the component, looked at the three options and said it was the first time he'd received design alternatives with a technical rationale he could actually evaluate. Kai did not say that was the whole point. He said: "Which one works best for your implementation constraints?" Marcus picked option two. It shipped that sprint. It tested cleanly.

## Core Competencies

### Design
- **User Research and Synthesis** — Kai's defining professional capability. He can design and execute research studies that produce actionable insights rather than data that confirms the team's existing assumptions — a distinction that requires both methodological rigor and the willingness to surface findings that complicate the roadmap. His research syntheses are written for a mixed audience: designers, PMs, and engineers can all read them and extract what they need. Raj has read two of his synthesis documents and described one of them to the CEO as "the clearest description of the client's actual problem we've had on this engagement."
- **Interaction Design and Prototyping** — He prototypes to test assumptions, not to polish. His Figma prototypes are functional enough to run user tests on and annotated with the specific interaction decisions he is trying to evaluate. He does not spend time on visual polish in a prototype that is going to be tested rather than presented — a restraint that Olivia has had to teach herself to appreciate rather than correct.
- **Usability Testing** — Both moderated and unmoderated. He has a practiced facilitation style for moderated sessions that is warm, neutral, and systematically effective at surfacing the gaps between what a user says and what a user does. His most consistent observation: participants will tell you a design is fine while demonstrating that it isn't. He structures his test sessions to minimize reliance on self-report.
- **Information Architecture and User Flows** — He maps user journeys before he maps screens. His flow diagrams are annotated with user goals, mental models, and decision points in a way that makes the gap between user expectation and design assumption visible — which is where the most important design decisions usually live.
- **Accessibility in UX Design** — He approaches accessibility from a cognitive and behavioral angle that complements James O'Brien's technical implementation perspective. He thinks about cognitive load, navigation predictability, and the ways that interface complexity disproportionately affects users with cognitive disabilities or situational impairments. He and James have developed a de facto working partnership on accessibility that neither of them has formalized. It is one of the more effective informal cross-functional relationships in the company.

### Growth Areas
- **Visual and UI Design** — Kai can produce clean, functional UI. He cannot produce UI at the level Olivia and Maya can, and he does not pretend otherwise. His interfaces are usable before they are beautiful, which is the right priority order for a researcher and the wrong priority order for a final deliverable. He relies on Olivia for visual direction and on Maya for asset quality, which is appropriate and occasionally creates a dependency that slows his output when both are at capacity.
- **Advocacy and Influence** — He has the data, the methodology, and the judgment. What he is building is the professional confidence to deliver findings with force when force is warranted. He softens the edges on difficult findings in ways that reduce the discomfort for the room and reduce the urgency for action. Olivia has told him directly that his research deserves a better advocate than the version of himself that shows up in sprint reviews. He agrees. The gap between agreement and behavior is where his current development work lives.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Customer Obsession** | Kai thinks about users with a specificity that is unusual for someone without a decade of experience. He has a mental model of the actual person who will use a feature — not a persona in a design doc but a specific type of human in a specific context making a specific attempt — and he runs design decisions against that model as a check on the team's assumptions. He brought a client's call center manager onto a user research call without being asked, because he suspected the design team's model of the end user was too narrow. The call revealed three workflows the current design did not support. The discovery was inconvenient and correct. |
| **Learn and Be Curious** | He reads broadly in cognitive science, behavioral economics, and HCI research in a way that is unusual for a working designer and that produces insights his peers in the field rarely have access to. He has introduced two research methods to the design team — diary studies and tree testing — that were not in JWare's toolkit before he arrived. He brought both back from conferences as specific techniques, not as general ideas, with a proposal for how to apply each to the current client engagement. |
| **Dive Deep** | When a usability finding is unclear, he does not close it prematurely. He runs additional sessions, triangulates against analytics if the data exists, and consults the research literature if the behavior is unexpected enough to warrant it. He once spent two evenings reading academic papers on mental model formation to understand why users on a logistics platform were consistently making a specific cognitive error. The finding reshaped the information architecture of a feature that had been scheduled for development. Olivia did not tell him to do this. He did not tell anyone he had done it until the finding was ready. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He has a ritual at the start of every new client project: he spends thirty minutes on the client's existing product before he reads the brief, the same way he might spend thirty minutes being a user before he reads the research. He says a brief is someone's interpretation of what the problem is; the product is the problem. He has not mentioned this to any PM. He does not think they would find it reassuring.
- He keeps a running private document called "things users do that we didn't expect" — a catalog of surprising behaviors from every test session he's run. It has 140 entries. Seven of them have directly informed JWare design decisions. The other 133 are, in his words, "the ones I haven't connected yet." He checks it when a new design brief comes in, on the theory that a surprising behavior from a different project might be a pattern he hasn't recognized yet.
- He makes very good coffee in a deliberate, unhurried way — pour-over, ground fresh, specific water temperature — at a pace that is mildly incongruous with any sense of urgency. The team has learned that if Kai is making coffee and you need something from him, you will have the full duration of the pour-over to discuss it, and then you will have his complete attention. The coffee is genuinely good. People have started timing their Kai conversations for when he's in the kitchen.
- He is a careful, practiced listener — he trained himself to pause for two full beats before responding because he found he was filling silence too quickly and closing conversations that had more to say. In meetings this reads as thoughtfulness. On a phone call it occasionally makes the other person think the connection dropped. He is aware of the phone call problem and has not fixed it.
- He played competitive chess in secondary school in Lagos before his family moved to the UK, and briefly again at Michigan before he decided he was more interested in why people made mistakes at chess than in winning the game. He still plays online occasionally. He is very difficult to beat. He does not mention the chess. He does occasionally, in design critiques, use the phrase "what's the worst move the user could make right now" in a way that suggests the question is more natural to him than metaphorical.
