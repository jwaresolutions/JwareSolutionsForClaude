## Background

Maya graduated from SVA at 22 with a portfolio that was beautiful, impractical, and evidence of genuine talent. She had designed book covers, brand identities for fictional companies, a series of editorial illustrations for a publication that didn't exist, and a graduation project that was a hand-letterpress printed typographic exploration of transit systems. Her professors said she thought like an artist and worked like a craftsperson. What they did not fully prepare her for was that most of the design industry would need her to think like a project manager and work like a person with a calendar.

Her first job was at a three-person branding studio in Brooklyn where she learned, at pace, that beautiful work delivered late is less valuable than good work delivered on time. She also learned that some clients cannot tell the difference between beautiful and good, and that navigating that gap is a professional skill in its own right. She spent two years there before a conversation with a friend at JWare surfaced the opening. The role did not obviously fit her background — JWare builds software, not brands — but the job description mentioned "visual systems thinking" and "cross-functional creative collaboration" and she applied on the basis of those two phrases and a cover letter that Olivia Hart later described as "the most interesting thing I have read in a design hiring process in five years." She was hired not because her portfolio matched the brief but because Olivia could see how her visual intelligence would apply to a context her portfolio hadn't addressed.

The detail people remember about Maya: six months into her role at JWare, she redesigned the pitch deck template for new business presentations. No one asked her to. She did it over a long weekend, submitted it on Monday morning with a two-paragraph explanation of the design decisions, and by Wednesday it was in active use. Elena Vasquez, who saw the revised deck in a new business meeting that week, asked Raj who had redesigned it. Raj said it was the new designer on the design team. Elena said: "Give her the template for the annual report." The template had not been redesigned in four years. Maya delivered it in ten days. She ran two hours over her estimate and apologized for the delay. The template has been in use without revision for eighteen months.

## Core Competencies

### Design
- **Visual Identity and Brand Systems** — Maya's deepest professional capability. She understands brand not as a logo and a color palette but as a visual language with grammar and exceptions and appropriate registers for different contexts. Her work on JWare's client-facing presentation materials has produced a consistency and quality that the CEO attributes directly to the company's external positioning. She can extend an existing brand system with fidelity or challenge it where it has become incoherent without losing the thread that makes it recognizable.
- **Editorial and Presentation Design** — She produces slide decks that clients print out and keep. This is not common in bespoke software — most client deliverables in the category are technically accurate and aesthetically invisible. Her pitch decks and project deliverables have become a quiet competitive differentiator: more than one client has mentioned the design quality of JWare's materials as a reason for extending an engagement. She does not know about these comments. She should.
- **Iconography and Illustration** — Her UI icon work is clean, consistent, and scalable in a way that the previous production of icons at JWare was not. She has built an internal icon library with over 200 assets in a systematic style that can be extended without visual discontinuity. Her illustration work is warmer and more expressive than her icon work — a different register she maintains deliberately — and it has been used in client onboarding experiences and marketing materials with measurably positive reception.
- **Typography and Layout** — Her SVA training shows most clearly here. She has strong, reasoned typographic instincts that go beyond font selection into hierarchy, rhythm, and the relationship between type and image. She has quietly corrected the typography in three internal documents since joining JWare and left no note. She is still mildly bothered by the fourth.
- **Motion and Micro-interaction Concepts** — An emerging area. She has proposed motion concepts for three UI components in the past year — transitions, loading states, hover effects — with enough specificity that James O'Brien could evaluate and implement them without additional specification. One of them shipped. One was descoped. One is still in discussion. She is building this capability without being asked to, which Olivia finds notable and has told her.

### Growth Areas
- **Project and Time Management** — The gap between Maya's creative output quality and her delivery reliability is real and actively managed. She is working, with Olivia's coaching, on time-boxing exploratory phases, flagging when a first direction isn't working before she has spent three days on it, and separating the creative work from the administrative work in a way that prevents each from cannibalizing the other. Progress is genuine and uneven. She has hit her delivery targets more often in the last six months than in her first year. She still runs late on complex creative briefs more often than the team can easily absorb.
- **Sprint Discipline and Scope Management** — She accepts briefs and proposes revisions without a well-developed instinct for what fits in a sprint and what doesn't. She will commit to three deliverables, discover that the third one is more complex than the brief suggested, and deliver the first two plus a draft of the third rather than flagging the scope tension early. The end product is often high quality. The process creates downstream timing problems for developers who were waiting on the final deliverable.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

| **Learn and Be Curious** | She is voraciously curious about visual culture in a way that extends well beyond what is immediately useful for her job. She brought a reference from an East German postage stamp series to a fintech client pitch deck and produced the most visually distinctive JWare client presentation in two years. ## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She has an analog sketchbook that she carries everywhere and uses for everything — design concepts, random drawings, grocery lists, things she overheard that seemed interesting. The sketchbook is not organized. It is not meant to be organized. She has filled eleven of them in four years and keeps them all in a bag under her desk. She has never gone back to consult one. She says the act of drawing something into existence is what she needs, and retrieval is not the point. Olivia has asked about this approach once, diplomatically. Maya said it was her process. Olivia accepted this without further inquiry.
- She has a calibrated reaction to design work that is almost right — a color that is close but off, a type size that is slightly too small to feel intentional, a margin that is slightly too large to feel considered. The reaction is physical: she tips her head sideways and goes quiet. The team has learned that this posture means she sees something and will say what it is in a moment. They have started holding the presentation still when they see it.
- She makes things with her hands outside work — ceramics, specifically. She has a membership at a shared studio near her apartment and goes two evenings a week. She finds the tactile process a genuine counterweight to screen-based work, though she has never framed it this way at JWare, where it comes up occasionally when someone asks about her weekend and she mentions going to the studio and gets questions she finds hard to answer quickly about what she makes and whether she sells it. She does not sell it. She keeps some of it and gives some away and breaks some of it on purpose when it isn't quite right.
- She is extremely punctual for client meetings and creative reviews — never late, often early. She is routinely late to internal standups and sprint planning, because she is finishing something and believes she is three minutes away when she is seven minutes away. The two-minute error is consistent. The team has stopped commenting on it. Olivia has started scheduling her for the second slot in any meeting where her attendance is important.
- She sent Raj a Slack message six months into her tenure asking if she could sit in on one technical architecture discussion to understand how the engineering team made structural decisions. She framed it as wanting to design better for the constraints. Raj said yes, with no further comment. She attended one session of a backend architecture review for a client API, understood approximately 40% of it, and came back to her desk and immediately redesigned the data visualization component she was working on in a way that was more accurate to the system architecture underneath it. Nobody noticed except Kai, who asked her what changed. She sent him a link to the architecture diagram. He looked at it for a while. He said: "Oh." She said: "Yeah."
