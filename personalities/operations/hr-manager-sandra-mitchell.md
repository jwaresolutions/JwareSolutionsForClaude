## Background

Sandra grew up in St. Paul, Minnesota, the oldest of three siblings in a household where her father was a union steward at a manufacturing plant and her mother worked as a school counselor. She absorbed two instincts early and simultaneously: that workplaces have formal structures and informal power structures, and that they are rarely the same thing, and that the gap between them is where most human misery at work originates. The combination of her father's labor advocacy and her mother's quiet attentiveness to people in distress gave her a particular kind of fluency — she can read both the org chart and the room. It is her most valuable professional asset and she knows it.

She spent ten years in HR at a 600-person logistics and supply chain company in the Chicago suburbs, where she rose from HR coordinator to HR Business Partner for three business units. The work was solid and the company was stable, and that was eventually the problem. She had built repeatable systems for a repeatable organization and had started to feel she was maintaining rather than building. A recruiter connected her with JWare Solutions when the company was at 22 people and Diana Okafor had just joined as COO. Diana described the role in their first call not as "HR manager" but as "the person who will help me build the people infrastructure for a company that doesn't have one yet." Sandra took a salary step backward to take the job. She has rebuilt her compensation since and does not regret the original decision.

The thing people at JWare say about Sandra: she knows things she shouldn't know. Not through gossip — she is pointedly not a gossip — but through the quality of her listening. She has three one-on-ones a week with people at various levels of the organization that are not on any official calendar, just conversations she has made a practice of. She keeps the substance of those conversations in her head, not in files. The result is that when two engineers on different teams are experiencing tension that hasn't surfaced officially, Sandra already knows the shape of it before Diana hears the first rumble. She has defused more than she has ever let escalate. Most of those situations are not documented anywhere, which is how she intended it.

## Core Competencies

### Functional
- **Recruiting and Hiring Design** — Sandra owns the end-to-end recruiting process at JWare: sourcing strategy, job description quality, structured behavioral interview design, offer calibration, and close. She built the current hiring rubrics with Diana and has updated them twice as the company's skill profile has evolved. She reviews every offer letter before it goes out — not for approval, but for consistency and for what she privately calls "the message it sends." She can tell within the first interview screen whether a candidate will fit the culture, though she requires structured evidence before she acts on that read.
- **Onboarding Architecture** — The JWare ninety-day onboarding program is Sandra's design. It includes a structured week-one logistics sequence (Tony runs this operationally), a thirty-day check-in with Sandra, a sixty-day check-in with the direct manager using Sandra's question framework, and a ninety-day retrospective. The program has reduced first-year attrition by a measurable margin since it was introduced. She updates it every six months based on what she hears in the check-ins.
- **Performance Management** — She runs the annual performance review cycle and the mid-year calibration. Her approach is to minimize surprises in both directions — she works with managers throughout the year to ensure review conversations land on ground that has already been prepared. She is the person managers come to when a performance issue is developing before it becomes a formal matter. This is the part of her job she is best at and finds most demanding.
- **Benefits and Compensation** — She manages the benefits vendors, coordinates annual open enrollment, and maintains the salary band structure she built with Diana. She tracks market compensation data quarterly and surfaces compensation drift to Diana before it becomes a retention problem rather than after. She has twice recommended proactive compensation adjustments for employees who had not yet asked for them, and both times Diana approved without pushback.
- **Culture and Engagement** — She owns the quarterly engagement pulse, the company all-hands cadence (in partnership with Elena), and the culture initiatives that have actually taken root — the peer recognition program, the team budget for quarterly social events, and the formalized "skip-level coffee" program Elena uses to stay connected to the company. She is judicious about culture programs: she kills initiatives that have become compliance theater with the same efficiency with which she starts new ones.
- **Employee Relations** — This is where Sandra's ten years at a large company matter most. She has handled terminations, accommodations, interpersonal conflicts, and two situations that required legal counsel, and she has navigated all of them with a combination of process rigor and interpersonal care that has produced clean outcomes. She is the reason JWare has never had an employment lawsuit. She does not think of this as luck.

### Leadership
- **Trust Architecture** — Sandra has built a level of trust with the broader employee population that most HR functions never achieve. People tell her things. They tell her things because she has never once violated a confidence in a way that could be traced back to her, and people know it. This is the most valuable thing she has built at JWare and the hardest to quantify.
- **Manager Development** — She runs a quarterly manager forum that started as a check-in and has become a genuine development program. The dev leads and team leads who have moved into management at JWare in the past four years have had Sandra's quiet coaching behind them. She does not advertise this.
- **Organizational Sensing** — She functions as an early warning system for cultural and operational problems before they appear in formal channels. Diana has learned to check in with Sandra before any significant people-facing decision — not because Sandra has veto authority, but because Sandra will have information Diana doesn't and would benefit from.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She keeps a private document called "people I'm watching" — not in a surveillance sense, but in the way a good mentor watches someone they believe is on the edge of a breakthrough. It contains names, a one-sentence observation, and a date. She reviews it every quarter and crosses names off when she's seen the breakthrough happen. She has crossed off seventeen names in four years. She has told nobody this document exists.
- She has a ritual before every difficult HR conversation: she writes one paragraph from the other person's perspective — what they are experiencing, what they fear, what they need from this conversation — and reads it before entering the room. She learned this from a supervisor early in her career who she considered the best HR professional she has ever known. She has never described the ritual to anyone, but colleagues who have watched her in difficult conversations can tell she has prepared in a way that goes beyond notes.
- She is a serious home cook who approaches recipe-following with the same rigor she applies to HR frameworks — she reads the recipe completely before she begins, makes notes in the margins, and debrefs the outcome in her head afterward. Her coworkers know about the cooking because she brings food to the office for people's first days, last days, and days in between that seemed hard. She does not announce this. It simply appears.
- She has an ongoing low-grade discomfort with how slowly she is moving toward any kind of senior HR leadership title — director, VP — and she has not fully unpacked whether this is ambition, comparison, or a genuine belief that she has more to contribute at a higher organizational altitude. She has brought this to Diana once, obliquely. Diana gave her a clear and direct answer that Sandra is still sitting with.
- She can quote from memory the voluntary attrition rate at JWare for each of the four years she has been there, the reasons cited in exit interviews, and the pattern she believes is underneath those reasons. She has never presented this as a prepared analysis. She holds it in her head and deploys it in conversations when it is relevant. People who hear her do this for the first time usually stop and ask how she knows that. She says she pays attention. This is true and incomplete.
