## Background

Helen grew up in Tacoma, Washington, the second of two daughters in a Korean-American household where her father owned a dry-cleaning business and her mother handled the books, the taxes, and the business banking. The financial operations of the family business were never hidden from Helen — she understood profit margins, receivables cycles, and cash flow seasonality before she learned to drive, not because she was trained in them but because they were the ambient language of her household. When the business had a good quarter, the family went out to eat. When it had a bad one, her mother said nothing but Helen could read it in the meal-planning. She learned early that financial numbers are a form of honesty, and that the people responsible for maintaining them are keeping a kind of faith.

She spent the first eight years of her career at KPMG in Seattle, starting in audit and moving into the controller services group that supported mid-market clients through their financial close processes, compliance filings, and internal control design. She left public accounting at 33 to take a controller role at a Series A infrastructure software company, where she built the finance function from a spreadsheet-based chaos system into a GAAP-compliant reporting environment that supported two audit cycles and a subsequent acquisition. She stayed through the acquisition integration, left when the absorbing company brought its own finance team, and spent four years as controller at a professional services firm before Elena Vasquez found her through a mutual contact and made a case for JWare that Helen initially declined twice.

The thing people at JWare say about Helen: she always knows the number. Not the ballpark, the number — the current cash position, the gross margin on the most recent project close, the exact amount by which the benefits renewal will increase costs in Q2, the days outstanding on the invoice that has been sitting in the client's AP queue for thirty-one days. She does not consult a dashboard to know these things. They are in her head because she loaded them there deliberately and because she considers holding them to be part of her job. Diana has, on three occasions, tested a number Helen stated in conversation by pulling up the system afterward. Helen has been right every time. Diana has not tested her since.

## Personality Profile
| Dimension | Description |

She does not have a strong need for approval and she does not make decisions by consensus. She trusts evidence, not intuition, and she has a lifelong relationship with the principle that the numbers tell the truth whether or not you want them to. She is not cold — she has warmth for people she knows and a dry humor that emerges in small doses — but she is serious in a way that can read as forbidding until you understand that the seriousness is about the work and not a verdict on you. |

She does not use three words when two will do, and she does not soften financial information with language that would make it feel better but less accurate. In the monthly close presentation she brings to Diana, every number has a source, every variance has an explanation, and every explanation has a recommended action. She is better in writing than in meetings, because writing allows her to say exactly what she means and no more. In meetings she answers the question asked and does not volunteer additional commentary unless she believes it is necessary, a quality that some people find reassuring and others find difficult to read. |
| **Work Style** | Deadline-anchored and deeply systematic. Her work cycle is organized around the monthly close, the quarterly reporting cycle, the annual audit, and the fiscal year budget process. Within each cycle she maintains a checklist of every task, every dependency, and every external party whose action she is waiting on. She follows up on outstanding items at fixed intervals without exception. She is one of the few people at JWare who has never missed a deadline she set, and she tracks this internally with the same precision she tracks everything else. She works in concentrated blocks, maintains a clean desk philosophy, and considers a disorganized working environment to be a signal of a disorganized thinking environment. |
| **Under Stress** | Gets quieter and more precise. Close observers note that she sharpens rather than scatters under pressure — her explanations become more exact, her documentation becomes more thorough, and her financial analysis becomes more detailed. The only visible tell is that she stops having lunch. She will go through a full day of financial crisis management without eating, which she does not notice herself and others notice acutely. |
| **Motivators** | A clean month-end close with no material adjustments. A budget-to-actual variance that she can explain completely. A client invoice that is paid on the due date, not the thirtieth day of net-30. An audit that generates no findings. The moment when Diana or Elena, reading a financial narrative Helen prepared, says "now I understand the shape of this." Watching a company she keeps the books for make money. |

Being asked to produce a financial analysis on a timeline that does not allow for the work to be done correctly. |

## Core Competencies

### Functional
- **Financial Close and Reporting** — Helen runs the monthly close process with a consistency that is, by the standards of companies her size, exceptional. Her close cycle is four business days — she has documented the exact sequence of steps, the dependencies, and the buffer time she maintains for adjustments. Diana receives the management reporting package on day five without fail. Helen considers the reliability of this cadence to be a form of organizational respect: leadership cannot manage the company in real time if the financial picture is available only sporadically.
- **Revenue Recognition and Project Accounting** — In a professional services company, revenue recognition is both technically complex and operationally consequential. Helen built JWare's project accounting methodology from scratch: she designed the time-tracking integration, the percentage-of-completion calculation, and the billing milestone reconciliation that produces clean revenue figures from a business model that would otherwise produce accounting noise. She explains this methodology to new managers with a clarity and patience that suggests she knows how non-accountants think, which she does.
- **Budgeting and Forecasting** — She runs the annual budget process as a structured, bottom-up build that requires genuine input from every function head. She does not accept top-down revenue targets without a bottom-up headcount and cost model to support them, a practice that has occasionally frustrated growth-oriented conversations but has produced budgets that are more accurate than most companies of JWare's size achieve. Her rolling quarterly forecast updates are the closest thing Diana has to a real-time view of where the year is going.
- **Cash Management** — She monitors the company's cash position daily and maintains a thirteen-week cash flow forecast that she updates weekly. She has never allowed the company to be surprised by a cash timing problem because she is watching the receivables aging, the payables schedule, and the payroll cycle simultaneously and continuously. She considers cash awareness a basic professional obligation for a controller, and she has privately been surprised by how many controllers she has met over her career who treat cash as a residual rather than a managed resource.
- **Payroll and Benefits Administration** — She runs payroll in coordination with Sandra and manages the relationship with the payroll vendor and the benefits brokers. She monitors benefits costs with the same attention she gives to any significant expense category and surfaces cost increases before open enrollment so Sandra has time to evaluate alternatives. She and Sandra have a shared understanding that compensation and benefits are both a financial line item and a people investment, which gives their working relationship a shared vocabulary that neither of them had to build from scratch.
- **Audit and Compliance** — JWare's annual financial audit is Helen's most visible deliverable, and she treats the preparation for it as a year-round practice rather than a year-end scramble. She has had two clean audits since joining. The third is on track. She maintains the internal control documentation, manages the auditor relationship directly, and considers an audit finding to be a personal failure rather than an external judgment, which means she closes every finding within the quarter and documents the closure.
- **Financial Narrative and Communication** — This is the competency most surprising to people who expect a controller to be a numbers producer rather than a communicator. Helen writes the narrative that accompanies her financial reporting — the explanations of variances, the context behind margin trends, the forward-looking observations — with a clarity that Diana has called the best financial communication she has seen from a controller in her career. Helen learned, early in public accounting, that a financial statement nobody understands is less valuable than an approximate number someone can act on. She writes toward action.

### Growth Areas
- **Think Big / Strategic Contribution** — Helen sees the financial implication of strategic decisions with precision. She sees the strategic opportunity with less clarity. She can tell you exactly what a new service line will cost to launch; she is less inclined to think through what the market looks like, whether the timing is right, or what asymmetric bets are worth taking even if the expected value is uncertain. This is appropriate epistemics for a controller and a limiting quality for a CFO, which is the role her career trajectory would otherwise point toward. Diana has told her this directly. Helen has accepted it as accurate and is not sure yet what to do with it. |
- **Invent and Simplify** — Helen's instinct is to build reliable systems and then hold them. She does not routinely question whether the system itself is the right system, and she is skeptical of finance technology changes that promise efficiency gains but require data migration and reconciliation during the transition. Two process improvements she has resisted because of transition risk have since been adopted by peer companies with measurable efficiency gains. She is aware of these cases. They have not yet updated her prior. |

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Insist on the Highest Standards** | Helen's financial work is subject to a standard she holds with something approaching moral conviction. She does not produce a number she is not certain of. She does not close a period without reconciling every variance to its source. She does not produce a financial statement with a known error and a plan to fix it next month. Her standard applies upward as well as downward: she has told Diana that a financial projection presented to the board overstated gross margin by 200 basis points and required correction before the board meeting, four hours before the board meeting. Diana corrected it. Helen considers this a routine professional responsibility. Others consider it notable courage. |
| **Dive Deep** | Her relationship with detail is comprehensive and unsentimental. She reads every line of a vendor contract before signing. She traces every budget variance to its originating transaction. She has reconstructed the full billing history of a client engagement from raw time entries when a dispute arose, producing a document so thorough that the client's own AP team acknowledged the error. She dives deep not to demonstrate thoroughness but because shallow is epistemically inadequate to her. She cannot trust a number she has not followed to its source. |
| **Frugality** | Helen is allergic to financial waste and treats every dollar of company money as a dollar that could be invested differently. She renegotiates vendor contracts at every renewal, challenges expense reports that are technically within policy but practically extravagant, and has twice recommended not filling a role for a quarter to stress-test whether the function could absorb the headcount gap. She does not confuse cost-cutting with frugality — she will spend willingly on things that produce clear return — but she will not spend on things that have not been justified. |
| **Deliver Results** | Helen delivers the financial picture of the company on time, in full, and with the accuracy required to make real decisions. She has never missed a close. She has never sent a revised financial statement because of a material error after distribution. She has never been unable to answer a direct financial question about the business. In a function where the primary output is trust in the numbers, she has never given anyone reason to distrust hers. |

### Strong (Consistent presence)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Earn Trust** | The trust Helen has built with Diana and Elena is entirely evidence-based: every number she has given them has been right, every financial warning she has sounded has been accurate, every deadline she has set has been met. She does not manage relationships — she does not read business books about executive communication or work on her influence skills — she just does the work correctly, consistently, and transparently. The trust has accumulated as a consequence rather than as a goal. |
| **Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit** | She will tell Diana, Elena, or an external auditor something they do not want to hear without softening the delivery in ways that dilute the message. She has objected to revenue recognition treatment on a project where the PM and the sales lead had both told the client the deal was closed. She was right; the deal closed three weeks later. She did not receive an apology, which she did not expect. She has flagged the same issue in subsequent quarters in writing before the same pattern repeated, which it has not. |
| **Ownership** | She owns the financial health of the company in a way that is personal and non-delegable in her mind. When the cash position is tight, she experiences it as her problem, not management's problem. When a client is past due, she is the one watching the aging report, making the first call, escalating through the PM relationship if the first call doesn't produce movement. She does not wait for Diana to ask how the receivables are doing. She reports proactively, because proactive is the only configuration that prevents surprises. |
| **Are Right, A Lot** | On financial and accounting questions her accuracy rate is effectively 100%, which is what the job requires. On business judgment questions — whether a client is worth extending a discount to retain, whether a new service line has market fit, whether a hire is structured correctly for long-term growth — she is more uncertain and more cautious than the situation often requires. She knows the difference between the two domains and is appropriately humble about where her accuracy is earned versus where it is assumed. |

### Moderate (Present but not defining)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Bias for Action** | She moves quickly on financial actions that are within her authority and slowly on decisions that require judgment in ambiguous situations. Her close cycle is fast because the process is defined. Her response to new financial scenarios — a client requesting extended payment terms, a potential acquisition conversation, a novel expense category — is thorough and sometimes slower than the situation's urgency requires. |
| **Customer Obsession** | Her customer is the company's financial integrity rather than the end client, which is the right orientation for a controller and occasionally creates friction with client-facing staff who want financial accommodations she does not consider appropriate. She understands that client relationships have financial value; she processes that value through receivables models and retention analysis rather than through relationship warmth. |
| **Learn and Be Curious** | She reads accounting literature, regulatory updates, and tax law changes with genuine attention. She does not read widely outside her domain, which is appropriate for the depth her role requires but limiting for the strategic contribution the company's growth now makes possible. Her curiosity is vertical rather than horizontal. |
| **Hire and Develop the Best** | Not currently relevant to her daily work — she has no direct reports — but she has been precise and useful in the one hiring conversation she has been involved in (for an accounting contractor during audit season). She evaluated the candidate's technical skills with a rigor that Sandra described afterward as the most useful functional input she had received from a hiring manager in two years. |

### Developing (Growth areas)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Think Big** | Helen's planning horizon is the fiscal year and, at its furthest reach, a three-year financial model. She does not think naturally about what the company could become — she thinks about what the company is and what it costs. When Diana or Elena raises a strategic question about the company's five-year direction, Helen's instinct is to identify the financial risk in the scenario rather than the financial opportunity in it. She knows this about herself. She has described it, privately, as "I see the floor before I see the ceiling." Diana has told her, once, that the company needs her to learn to see both. |
| **Invent and Simplify** | Her relationship with financial systems and processes is custodial rather than inventive. She builds reliable systems and then maintains them. She does not ask whether the system design itself could be improved, whether a different approach might produce cleaner data with less reconciliation overhead, or whether a new tool might make the reporting cycle faster without sacrificing accuracy. She treats technology change in finance as inherently risky, which it is, and under-weights the compounding cost of maintaining systems that have stopped being optimal. |

## Strengths and Blind Spots
| Strengths | Blind Spots |
|-----------|-------------|
| Financial accuracy that gives leadership genuine confidence in the numbers — Diana makes decisions from Helen's data without re-verifying it, which is the highest operational trust in the company | Interprets financial caution as a universal virtue; applies risk-aversion to strategic questions where measured risk-taking is the appropriate posture |
| Proactive financial communication — she surfaces problems before they become crises, which is the entire value proposition of a good controller | Sees cost before opportunity in new investments; can make growth conversations more conservative than the underlying economics require |
| Clean audit history and rigorous internal controls — the company has never had a material financial finding under her tenure | Slower to adopt process improvements than the efficiency gains would warrant; transition risk receives more weight than long-term friction cost |
| Cash flow visibility that prevents surprises — she maintains a thirteen-week rolling forecast and monitors it daily | Difficulty translating financial precision into strategic language; her analyses are accurate but sometimes too technical for the audience who most needs to act on them |
| Willing to deliver unwelcome financial news directly and without delay — leadership is never the last to know | Underinvests in relationships with client-facing staff; the PM team and delivery team interact with her primarily through process rather than through relationship, which limits her influence on the business decisions that drive the financial outcomes she monitors |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She tracks the company's days sales outstanding number the way a pilot tracks altitude — continuously, from a kind of background vigilance that does not require conscious attention until the number moves outside of normal range. She has mentioned the current DSO in casual conversation with Diana without being asked in the context of a completely unrelated discussion, not to report on it but because it was simply in her mind. Diana no longer finds this unusual.
- She has a relationship with the physical close binder — the hard-copy archive she maintains of every monthly close, indexed and organized by period — that her colleagues find mildly anachronistic and she finds foundational. She keeps three years of close binders in her office. She has referred to them twice in the past year to resolve disputes about financial history. Both times the binder was right. She has been asked once why she doesn't just use the accounting system. She said the accounting system can be edited and a closed binder cannot. Nobody asked again.
- She has a standing Friday afternoon ritual of reviewing the open receivables aging report and the following week's payables schedule at the same time, looking for any cash timing mismatches that need attention on Monday. This takes about twenty-five minutes and she has done it every week she has worked at JWare. She has declined exactly two social invitations because they conflicted with this Friday window. Both times she said she had something to finish up. Both times she was doing the aging review.
- She is a serious amateur astronomer who owns a six-inch refractor telescope and keeps a sky journal with observing notes going back eleven years. This is the version of herself she is most privately proud of — the one who drives to dark-sky sites on clear weekends, who can tell you the right ascension and declination of a dozen deep-sky objects from memory, and who has named her telescope after her mother. She does not discuss this at work. One of the junior engineers found her StarNet forum username by accident and said nothing. She does not know he knows. He finds it quietly wonderful.
- She believes, with a conviction she rarely expresses and never argues for, that the financial statements of a well-run company are a form of honest communication with the people who depend on it — employees, clients, investors — and that keeping them accurate is a form of integrity rather than a compliance activity. This is not a philosophy she has articulated in any meeting. It is the reason she has the career she has. It is the reason she has never, not once, signed off on a number she did not believe was right.
