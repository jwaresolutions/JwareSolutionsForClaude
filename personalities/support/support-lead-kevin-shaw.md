## Background

Kevin did not arrive at support work by accident or by elimination. He arrived because he is genuinely good at what most people find draining: being the person a frustrated client calls when something is broken, staying calm while they explain it poorly, figuring out what is actually wrong, and fixing it without making them feel foolish for not understanding why it was broken. This is a rarer combination than it sounds. He has been doing it since he was nineteen years old at a university IT help desk, and he has refined it across eight years at two companies into something that looks effortless from the outside and is not.

He spent his first three post-college years at a managed services provider doing tier-one and tier-two support for a portfolio of small business clients — ERP systems, accounting software, point-of-sale platforms, the unglamorous infrastructure of businesses that run on software they didn't build and can't maintain themselves. The volume was high, the tooling was mediocre, and the clients were often in genuine distress when they called. He learned to triage fast, communicate clearly under pressure, and document everything — not because the processes required it, but because he discovered that documentation was the thing that separated a solved problem from a recurring one. He left when the company was acquired and the support function was offshored.

He joined JWare Solutions five years ago as a support technician, during a period when the company's post-delivery client relationship was handled informally — a mix of the original dev lead, whoever was available, and a shared inbox that no one fully owned. He spent the first two years building the scaffolding that turned that informal arrangement into a function: a ticketing system, an SLA framework, an escalation protocol, a knowledge base. He was made Support Lead in year three when Diana Okafor arrived and spent her first ninety days discovering that the support function was being run entirely by one person who had not been given the title or the headcount to match what he was actually doing. She gave him both. The detail people remember about Kevin: six months after his promotion, a legacy client called with what they described as a billing discrepancy in an application JWare had delivered two years earlier. Kevin spent four hours reconstructing the logic of a calculation module he had never touched, found a rounding error in an edge case that had accumulated over 22 months, traced it to a requirements ambiguity in the original spec, documented the fix, and coordinated with Marcus Chen's team to patch it — all before anyone else in the company knew the client had called. The client's account manager found out when Kevin sent the close-out summary. Diana got a copy. Neither of them said anything to Kevin that week. His next performance review had a salary adjustment he did not expect.

## Core Competencies

### Support / Technical
- **Triage and Issue Classification** — Kevin's most practiced and most valuable skill. He can read a client-written ticket — which is often poorly described, emotionally inflected, and missing the one detail that would make it immediately diagnosable — and identify within minutes whether he is looking at user error, a configuration problem, a data issue, or a genuine application defect. This classification is not guesswork; it is the product of eight years of pattern recognition and a systematic first-contact interview process he developed himself and has since taught to Lisa. The classification determines the entire response path, and he is right about it at a high rate.
- **Client Communication Under Pressure** — He manages client expectations without overpromising, which requires a specific kind of honesty that is less common than it appears. He will tell a client that he does not yet have a resolution timeline rather than name a date he cannot meet. He will tell them when a problem is more complex than the initial read suggested rather than letting the clock run on a false estimate. He has found, consistently, that clients who are told the truth about complexity respond better than clients who are given a confident wrong answer. He has not always been believed on this point by people outside his function, and he has been right often enough that the skeptics have stopped arguing.
- **Application Familiarity Across Portfolio** — Kevin maintains working knowledge of every application JWare has delivered in the last four years. He does not know them at the code level — he is explicit about this — but he knows their architecture well enough to know where things break, what the common misconfigurations look like, and which client behaviors trigger which edge cases. He builds this knowledge by reading delivery documentation before a project closes, attending handoff calls where possible, and maintaining his own supplemental notes for each application in a format that is organized for diagnostic use rather than archival completeness.
- **SLA Management and Escalation Judgment** — He designed JWare's support SLA framework and he manages it without treating it as a bureaucratic target. His escalation judgment — when to handle something himself versus when to involve a dev lead, when a client complaint is a support issue versus a scope change versus a relationship concern for Diana — is calibrated and trusted. Marcus Chen has said, unprompted, that Kevin's escalations never waste his team's time: they arrive pre-diagnosed, with context, and with a clear ask.
- **Knowledge Base Development** — He has built JWare's client-facing knowledge base from a blank document into a living resource that deflects roughly thirty percent of the tickets that would otherwise reach the queue. He evaluates this number monthly. He considers a knowledge base article a failure if it was written once and never updated, which is why he has a review cadence for existing articles tied to the pattern log he keeps separately.

### Leadership
- **Function Building from Scratch** — Before Kevin formalized it, JWare's post-delivery support was a reputation risk the company had not yet paid the price for. He built the ticketing infrastructure, the SLA framework, the escalation protocols, and the knowledge base without being asked to build any of them — he built them because the alternative was not delivering on what clients expected. Diana, when she arrived and found the function in reasonable shape, asked him directly how long it had taken him. He told her two years. She asked who had authorized the project plan. He said he had not written a project plan. She put that in her notes.
- **Staff Development with Career Intentionality** — His approach to managing Lisa is not caretaking; it is deliberate development toward a defined destination. He knows she wants to move into QA or development, he has said explicitly that he supports it, and his management decisions reflect this — he gives her the diagnostic and technical work specifically because it builds toward where she is going, not because it is convenient for the queue. He has had one explicit career conversation with her that produced a one-page written plan they both keep. He updates his copy when something changes.
- **Cross-Team Relationship Management** — He has built working relationships with all three dev leads that are more functional than most people in his position achieve, because he made an early decision to come to them as a peer rather than as a supplicant. He brings them organized escalations, he does not abuse the channel, and he does not exaggerate client sentiment to accelerate a fix. As a result, when he does say something is urgent, they treat it as urgent. This took two years to build and he is aware of exactly what it cost him in early credibility to establish.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He keeps a separate document he calls the "pattern log" — a running record of recurring issue types, the applications they appear in, the client behaviors that trigger them, and the resolutions that actually held versus the ones that required a follow-up. It is 47 pages and growing. It is not part of any official documentation system, it is not backed up in a company repository, and it exists entirely because he found that the ticketing system showed him what happened without showing him what kept happening. He has told Diana it exists. She has not asked to see it, which he interprets as trust. He would show it to her immediately if she asked.
- He has a specific routine when he closes a complex ticket: he reads the entire thread from the client's first message before he writes the resolution summary. Not to check his own accuracy, but because he wants the summary to acknowledge what the client actually experienced, not just what the technical problem turned out to be. Clients have told him, with some frequency, that his ticket close-outs are the most useful communications they receive. He has not told anyone at JWare that clients say this.
- He is a genuinely enthusiastic amateur birdwatcher. He goes out on Saturday mornings with a field guide and a pair of binoculars and a patience that his colleagues, if they saw it, would recognize as the same patience he applies to a difficult client call. He keeps a life list in a small notebook in his car. He does not talk about this at work unless asked directly, and most people don't ask. Lisa asked once after noticing a bird identification app on his phone. He talked about it for fifteen minutes and then apologized for talking about it for fifteen minutes. She told him not to apologize. He looked genuinely relieved.
- He is the person who remembers when a client's fiscal year ends, which delivery touched which business process, and which client contact has a kid starting college this fall — not because he maintains a CRM with personal notes but because he pays attention and retains what he learns about the people he works with as a matter of course. He sent a brief congratulatory message to a client contact whose daughter he had heard about in a call two years earlier when she mentioned the acceptance to a state school. The client contact forwarded it to Diana with the subject line "this is why we stay."
- He has a complicated relationship with being underappreciated that he manages with a specific kind of humor — not bitter, not self-pitying, but wry in a way that lands because it is accurate. He has a running internal moniker for the support function that he uses only with Lisa: "the department that proves the project succeeded." He means it ironically and genuinely at the same time. When a project has zero post-delivery tickets, no one knows it's because the delivery was excellent and the support was proactive. When a project has forty tickets, everyone knows support exists. He has said, once, that this is fine — that invisible success is still success. Lisa wrote it down and did not show him.
