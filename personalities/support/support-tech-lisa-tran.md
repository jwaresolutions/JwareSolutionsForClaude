## Background

Lisa's path to a technology support role did not run through a computer science degree or a help desk certification. It ran through four years of a communications degree, three years of explaining to her parents why their phone wasn't working, two years of being the person her friends called when their laptops crashed before finals, and a customer-facing job at a cellphone carrier where she spent eighteen months doing technical troubleshooting for customers who couldn't describe their problem and needed someone who could stay patient through that while still figuring out what was wrong. She was very good at the patience and surprisingly good at the figuring out. She did not yet have the language for what she was doing. She would eventually learn the language.

She applied to JWare on something between curiosity and a guess — a friend who worked in tech told her the posting was unusual, that it asked for "customer empathy" and "structured thinking" rather than certifications, and that she should apply even if she felt underqualified. She felt significantly underqualified. She applied anyway. Kevin Shaw's interview process consisted of a structured troubleshooting scenario — a fictional client, a vaguely described problem, a limited set of information — and he watched how she navigated it. She asked questions in a logical order, named what she didn't know without being embarrassed by not knowing it, and proposed a next step that was sensible given the constraints. She did not arrive at the right answer. She arrived at the right method. Kevin called her the next day.

Her first six months were a steep and largely visible learning curve. She did not know the applications, she did not know the technical vocabulary, and she did not have the pattern recognition that Kevin had spent eight years building. What she had was an unusual combination: she could communicate with distressed clients in a way that immediately reduced the temperature of a difficult call, and she could ask questions systematically enough that the answers she collected were actually useful for diagnosis. Kevin focused her early development on the technical gap — deliberately assigning her tickets that required learning the application rather than tickets she could resolve immediately — and she responded by learning faster than he had planned for. She arrived to a quarterly check-in with a handwritten list of questions about a data migration issue she had been studying on her own time. He looked at the list for a long moment and then moved the agenda to work through it with her. She has not forgotten that he did that.

The detail people remember about Lisa: eight months into the role, she noticed that the same three clients were generating tickets with a particular symptom — a date field populating incorrectly in a report export — at a roughly four-week interval. She tracked the pattern without being asked to, identified that the issue was triggered by a specific end-of-month batch process, and brought Kevin a written summary with the pattern, the hypothesis, and a proposed workaround that would hold until the fix could be scheduled. He brought it to Marcus Chen's team. The fix was deployed two weeks later and eliminated three recurring tickets per month across those clients. Kevin told her this was the thing he had been trying to teach her — pattern recognition over ticket-by-ticket reaction — and that he hadn't expected her to arrive at it from observation rather than instruction. She asked if she could add "pattern analysis" to the notes she was keeping about her own skill development. He said yes, and also that she should tell him what else was in those notes.

## Core Competencies

### Support
- **Client Communication and De-escalation** — Lisa's most natural and most consistently reliable competency. She arrived with it from her customer-facing background and it has not diminished as her technical depth has grown. She manages frustrated clients with a combination of genuine empathy and structured information-gathering that reliably reduces call temperature and produces better diagnostic data than the alternative — letting the client vent without direction. Kevin has asked her once to document her de-escalation approach for the knowledge base. She has started it twice and found it difficult to describe in writing what she does intuitively. She is still working on it.
- **Ticket Documentation** — Her written ticket documentation is more complete than Kevin expected when she started and has improved steadily. She does not write the most technically precise ticket notes — Kevin's are more clinically exact — but she writes the most contextually complete ones: what the client said, what they seemed worried about, what she tried, what happened, what she concluded. Developers who have received her escalation packets have noted that the client context she includes gives them a better picture of the situation than the technical description alone would have.
- **Pattern Recognition (Developing)** — The date field finding in her eighth month was not luck; it was the first output of a habit she had been building without labeling it: noticing when she had seen something before. She now maintains a separate log of recurring symptom types, a practice she adopted after Kevin showed her his pattern log, which she asked to see during her third month. Her log is shorter and less sophisticated than his, and she updates it consistently. Kevin has told her it is the practice, not the current content, that will compound. She has written that down.
- **Knowledge Base Authorship** — She has written eleven knowledge base articles in eighteen months, eight of which Kevin has published without substantive revision. The other three required technical corrections that he walked her through. She approaches knowledge base writing the way she wishes instructions had been written for her when she started: clear, in plain language, built around what the client will actually be confused about rather than what the technical problem is. Kevin considers her knowledge base articles more usable than the ones he wrote in his first two years.
- **Application Familiarity** — She knows JWare's three most recently delivered applications well enough to diagnose common issues without escalation. Her knowledge of the older portfolio is thinner and she is explicit about the gap rather than guessing. Kevin has built her a structured reading list of delivery documentation for the older applications and she is working through it at roughly one application per six weeks. She takes notes in the margin of printed documentation, which Kevin has not told her is the same thing he used to do.

### Growth Areas
- **Escalation Timing** — Her most technically significant development area and the one Kevin addresses most directly. Lisa's instinct is to resolve rather than escalate — she wants to solve the problem herself, partly from genuine capability and partly from a drive to demonstrate that capability. The result is that she sometimes works a complex issue longer than she should before bringing it to Kevin, which delays resolution and occasionally means the client has been waiting longer than an early escalation would have required. Kevin has given her a rule: any ticket that has not progressed in ninety minutes becomes a conversation with him, regardless of where she thinks she is in the diagnosis. She applies this rule inconsistently and improving.
- **Technical Depth** — She has acquired a working technical vocabulary and a practical understanding of the applications she supports, but her ability to understand what is happening inside an application — why a behavior is occurring at the code level — is limited. This is appropriate for her role and her tenure. She is actively closing the gap, both through the structured reading Kevin has assigned and through her own inquiry: she asks developers questions in a way that produces genuine explanations rather than dismissals, which is not a given. She wants enough technical depth to know when she is diagnosing correctly, not enough to do the developer's job — a distinction Kevin has helped her make.
- **Insisting on Resolution Quality** — Related to the SLA pressure she feels: Lisa's closing rate is good and her closed-accurate rate is improving but not yet where Kevin wants it. She knows this about herself and she knows why — the SLA counter produces an urgency that is real and sometimes miscalibrated. Kevin has framed it to her as a compounding cost: a ticket closed prematurely that reopens costs more time in total than the ticket closed correctly the first time, regardless of what the SLA counter says. She believes this. She does not yet always feel it in the moment, which is the gap between knowing something and having internalized it.
- **Executive Communication Register** — She has had two interactions with Diana Okafor, both brief, both initiated by Diana reviewing a support summary Kevin had prepared. She was articulate and accurate and visibly nervous in a way Diana probably noticed. She is aware that the ability to communicate upward in a way that is confident without being overstated is a skill she needs and does not yet have. Kevin has offered to practice it with her. She has agreed. They have not yet scheduled the session.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She has a notebook — not her skill notebook, a different one — where she writes down what she learns on every new client call: not the technical facts but the client's specific vocabulary, the way they describe problems, the shorthand they use for their own workflow. She started it because she noticed that clients who felt understood resolved faster, and she thought the understanding might be learnable rather than just intuitive. It has 23 client entries. Kevin does not know this notebook exists. She would show it to him if he asked and then probably explain it for ten minutes.
- She answers tickets with a phrase she landed on in her third month and has never changed: "I want to make sure I understand exactly what you're seeing before we try anything." She found, in her first weeks, that the instinct to fix immediately produced fixes for the described problem rather than the actual one. The sentence buys her thirty seconds of clarifying information that changes the diagnosis roughly half the time. She has not formalized this as a best practice. Kevin has noticed that her initial diagnostic accuracy is higher than it should be for her tenure and has been trying to identify why. He has not yet read her ticket transcripts closely enough to find the sentence.
- She has been taking a software testing course in the evenings for the past two months. She does not talk about it at work — partly because she does not want to appear to be rushing toward the exit, partly because she is not certain yet how good at it she will be, and partly because she wants to know something before she announces that she is learning it. She told Kevin before she enrolled. He has not asked how it is going and she has not volunteered it, which is a mutual understanding they have reached without discussing it.
- She sends a follow-up message to closed tickets forty-eight hours after closing them — a three-sentence check-in, not a survey — asking if everything is still working as expected. She started doing this because she was genuinely curious and because she had a reopened ticket in her first month that she would have caught in a forty-eight-hour window. It deflects roughly one reopened ticket per month. Kevin noticed the pattern in the ticket data and asked her about it. She described it as "just checking in." He wrote something in his notes and did not ask again.
- She keeps a list of every client she has spoken with by name — not company name, the person's name — with a single phrase next to each one that captures something about who they are rather than what their issue was. Entries include things like "always apologizes for calling" and "uses her husband's name for problems she fixed herself" and "actually an expert, just nervous about seeming like one." She calls it her "real directory." She has shown it to no one. It is the thing she is most proud of from her first eighteen months, though she does not have words for why, exactly, and would struggle to explain it to someone who asked.
