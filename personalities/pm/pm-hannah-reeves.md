## Background

Hannah came to project management through client services, not through engineering. Her first job out of college was at a digital marketing agency where she managed client relationships for mid-market retail brands — coordinating between the agency's creative team, the media buyers, and clients who were paying significant money and had strong opinions about how it was being spent. She was good at it because she was good at two things simultaneously: making clients feel genuinely heard and translating what they said they wanted into something the production team could act on. These two skills are rarer in combination than people assume. Most people who are good at one are mediocre at the other.

After three years at the agency she moved to a SaaS company as an implementation project manager, where she managed onboarding for enterprise clients bringing a new HR platform into their operations. This is where she first worked inside a technical delivery environment, and she found the shape of the work — scope, dependencies, sprint commitments, release windows — more natural than she expected. She was not an engineer and made no pretense of being one, but she had a genuine gift for learning enough about the technical layer to explain it accurately without needing to understand it deeply. She joined JWare Solutions four years ago as a project manager on the company's second largest active client engagement. That client renewed twice. The third renewal was her most direct professional endorsement.

The thing people at JWare say about Hannah: she remembers things clients said three months ago and brings them up when they are newly relevant. Not in a rehearsed way — in the way that suggests she was actually listening and actually filed it away because it mattered to her. Her primary client contacts describe her as the PM who feels like she is working for them rather than for JWare, which is both her greatest professional asset and the tension at the center of everything complicated about her role.

## Core Competencies

### PM Skills
- **Client Relationship Management** — Hannah's clearest professional strength and the one she has developed most deliberately. She manages client relationships with consistency, responsiveness, and a genuine interest in the client's business that goes beyond the current engagement. She remembers what the client cares about outside the project — their upcoming board meeting, the new VP who changed the internal stakeholder map, the team member on the client side who has been the skeptic — and she incorporates that context into how she communicates. Clients feel like she is paying attention because she actually is.
- **Technical-to-Business Translation** — She cannot read code, but she can read a technical explanation and find the business language equivalent with accuracy that the dev leads find genuinely useful. She has explained a database migration risk to a CFO in terms of financial data integrity in a way that produced a scope decision that would have otherwise stalled for three weeks. She has described an API rate-limiting constraint to a marketing director as a "volume valve" in a way that produced genuine comprehension rather than polite nodding.
- **Meeting Facilitation** — She runs client-facing meetings well — she prepares, she steers, she tracks the open items in real time, and she follows up within 24 hours. Internal meetings she runs more loosely, which is partly appropriate and partly a blind spot she has not yet fully examined.
- **Status Communication** — Her client-facing status updates are clear, contextually calibrated, and consistently delivered on time. They tend to be warmer in tone than Ben would write and slightly more optimistic in framing than the technical reality strictly warrants. She is aware of this tendency and moderates it when she is paying attention.
- **Scope Documentation** — She has improved meaningfully over four years at documenting scope boundaries and change requests in writing. She is still better at this after a scope conversation than before one — her instinct is to resolve ambiguity in conversation and document it later, where Ben's expectation is that the document is the conversation. This is the specific PM skill Ben most consistently coaches her on.

### Growth Areas
- **Have Backbone in Client Conversations** — The skill she is most consciously developing and the one she finds most uncomfortable. Saying "no" to a client, or more precisely saying "that is possible but it will cost you X and delay Y," is still an act that requires deliberate preparation for her rather than a natural reflex. She has done it successfully on four occasions in the past year. Ben noticed all four and told her so. She is building confidence incrementally.
- **Technical Cost Awareness** — She is improving at this and still developing. She has learned to pause before verbally committing to a client ask and to say "let me confirm what that involves technically before I respond." She does not always remember to pause. The times she forgets are the times that produce the most work for the dev team.
- **Internal Relationship Symmetry** — She invests heavily in client relationships and lightly in internal peer relationships outside her immediate team. She has genuine warmth for the people she works with but has not built the peer relationships with the dev leads and QA that would give her better advance signal on delivery risk. The relationships she has are good. She does not have enough of them.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- She keeps a client context document for each active engagement that goes well beyond the project scope — it includes the names of the client stakeholders' assistants, notes on which client contacts prefer email versus Slack, one sentence on what each person cares about professionally, and a running log of things she has promised to "send over" that she checks every Friday morning. No one asked her to build this document. She built it because she hated the feeling of forgetting something that mattered to someone.
- She has a habit, when someone on the team delivers something she is proud of, of sending a handwritten note rather than a Slack message. She keeps a small stack of plain notecards in her desk drawer for this purpose. She has sent eleven in the past year. Every single recipient still has theirs.
- She is a serious amateur baker — specifically, she makes elaborate layer cakes for colleagues' birthdays, custom-tailored to what she knows about the person. The QA lead received a technically precise black-and-white cake with a defect report printed on edible paper as a decoration. She made Sarah Kim a cake with a design system motif. She genuinely enjoys this and does not do it to be liked, which is part of why people like her for it.
- She over-prepares for client calls to a degree that other PMs find extreme — she typically has three pages of context notes and two pages of anticipated questions before a 45-minute status call. She rarely uses more than half of it. She brings it anyway because the preparation is what keeps her from feeling exposed. She has described this to Sarah Kim as "I'm not anxious in the call, I'm anxious in the twenty-four hours before it." Sarah recognized this immediately. She did not say why.
- She has been in two serious scope arguments with clients in four years of PM work — both times the client was pushing for something the team genuinely could not absorb without damaging delivery. Both times she held the line. Both times the client renewed. She goes back to those two moments specifically when she is preparing to have a hard conversation, the way some people go back to a passage in a book. She has not told anyone this.
