## Background

Jordan came to project management from business analysis, which means they came to it from the numbers. Their first job out of Kelley was at a mid-size logistics firm as a junior business analyst — building models, documenting process flows, translating operational data into recommendations that the operations team could use. They were meticulous and fast, and they had a genuine talent for finding the number inside a problem that other people were describing qualitatively. The limitation of the BA role, as Jordan experienced it, was that the work ended at the recommendation. They wanted to own what happened next.

They moved into project management two years ago when JWare Solutions was hiring for a PM to support one of its three active client engagements. The job posting asked for someone with strong analytical skills and process discipline. Jordan applied the same way they approach everything: with a prepared presentation. They brought a mock project status dashboard to the final interview, built in Notion, with sample metrics they had constructed from publicly available information about JWare's industry vertical. Ben Hartley, who ran the interview, hired them on the spot and has been trying to figure out how to develop their relational skills ever since.

Jordan is the most operationally rigorous PM on Ben's team and the least naturally client-facing one. Their engagement metrics are clean, their documentation is current, and their burndown charts are accurate enough that Ben uses their project as the benchmark when coaching Hannah on internal rigor. What Jordan is building now is the layer of PM work that does not live in a spreadsheet — the read on what a client is actually worried about, the judgment call about when a data point needs a conversation instead of a number, the instinct for when process is serving the project and when it is a substitute for the harder human work. They are aware of this gap in a way that is both a strength and occasionally paralyzing.

## Core Competencies

### PM Skills
- **Metrics and Dashboard Management** — Jordan's clearest professional strength. They build project health dashboards that are accurate, interpretable, and updated without being prompted. Their burndown charts reflect reality rather than optimism because they track scope additions as scrupulously as they track completions. Ben uses Jordan's project tracker as a teaching artifact with Hannah. Diana has asked Jordan to present their dashboard format to the broader PM review as a proposed standard. Jordan prepared a seventeen-slide deck for a five-minute presentation. Ben edited it down to six slides. Jordan kept the other eleven for their own reference.
- **Risk Documentation and Escalation** — They identify and document delivery risks systematically and escalate them through the right channels with appropriate urgency. They are occasionally over-eager to escalate risks that have not yet fully materialized, which Ben is coaching them to calibrate. The underlying instinct — surface the risk before it becomes a problem — is correct. The calibration — surface it at the right altitude and at the right time — is developing.
- **Sprint Planning Support** — Jordan prepares more thoroughly for sprint planning than most PMs engage with it. They arrive with historical velocity data, a scope delta summary since the last sprint, and a list of dependency flags for the dev lead to address. The dev lead on their engagement has described this preparation as "unusual" and then, when pressed, as "actually really useful."
- **Process Documentation** — Their project documentation is the most current and navigable in the PM function. Anyone picking up Jordan's project during an absence would be oriented within twenty minutes. This is not universal across the PM team and its value has become more visible since Hannah's engagement had a coverage gap during a planned leave.
- **Internal Stakeholder Communication** — Jordan is notably more fluent in internal communication than in client-facing communication. They navigate Diana's operations reviews with confidence, respond to Ben's data requests accurately and quickly, and communicate risk flags in a format that leadership finds actionable. This competency is the most developed relative to their tenure and is a source of quiet confidence.

### Growth Areas
- **Client Relationship Development** — The most significant and most acknowledged gap in Jordan's current PM practice. They can run a client status meeting competently. They cannot yet read one — they do not reliably detect when the client is telling them something important between the lines of a positive status response, when the client's silence on an agenda item is significant, or when the professional warmth in a call is masking a building concern. Hannah has this instinct natively. Jordan is developing it empirically, which is slower and more reliable over time.
- **Flexibility in Ambiguous Situations** — Jordan's process discipline is an asset in structured environments and a liability in environments that resist structure. Clients who communicate informally, scope conversations that do not have a clear right answer, and relationships that require navigating through relationship texture rather than documented agreement — these situations expose the gap between Jordan's operational competence and their contextual judgment. They are learning this gap exists. They have not yet found a way to fill it that is not just "add another process."
- **Calibrating Escalation Thresholds** — They escalate too readily on matters they could and should resolve independently, and too formally on matters that should be resolved in a five-minute conversation. Ben has raised this directly, kindly, and with a specific framework for distinguishing information escalation from decision escalation. Jordan has been applying the framework with improving accuracy.

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- They track their own PM performance metrics in a personal spreadsheet that predates their PM role by six months — they built it as a BA and adapted it when they changed functions. The spreadsheet has fourteen tabs. Ben has seen two of them. Jordan has offered to walk him through the rest; he has not yet taken them up on it, which Jordan interprets as either trust or disinterest and cannot determine which.
- They have a habit of arriving to meetings two to three minutes early and spending those minutes reviewing their notes in a way that is visibly preparatory. Other meeting participants find this alternately impressive and pressure-inducing. Jordan does not notice either response because they are reviewing their notes.
- They are a recreational competitive runner — 5K and 10K road races — and they approach race performance with the same analytical discipline they apply to project management. Their training log is a structured spreadsheet. Their race-day plan is a documented pacing strategy with contingency notes for weather and field size. They have, on two occasions, referred to a sprint commitment as a "pacing problem" and caught themselves just after saying it. The dev lead both times knew exactly what they meant.
- They are the youngest person in most of the professional rooms they occupy at JWare and are acutely, quietly aware of it. They compensate through preparation — they are never the least-informed person in the room if they can help it — and occasionally over-compensate by being deferential in conversations where they are actually the most prepared person present. Ben has told them once to trust what they know. They wrote it down. It is in the OKR document next to Diana's comment about honest numbers.
- They are building what they privately call a "soft skills log" — a running document where they record specific interpersonal situations they found difficult, what they did, what happened, and what they would do differently. It is organized like a defect log, with a severity classification and a status field. Some entries are marked "resolved." Most are marked "in progress." The entry for "how to tell when a client is actually worried" has been open for eight months and has four paragraphs of notes. They have not yet marked it resolved. They are probably right not to.
