## Background

Ben Hartley spent the first eight years of his career writing code — backend services, data pipelines, integration middleware — at two enterprise software companies and a regional bank. He was a competent engineer, consistently reliable, comfortable with ambiguity in a codebase. What distinguished him was a habit that his first engineering manager noticed early and named before Ben had named it himself: he spent as much time thinking about how work moved between people as he spent thinking about how code moved between systems. He was the person who drew the dependency diagram on the whiteboard before the sprint started. The person who noticed, three weeks before anyone said it out loud, that two teams were heading toward a collision on a shared schema. He moved into project management not because he burned out on engineering but because he kept doing PM work informally anyway, and it seemed wasteful to pretend otherwise.

He spent four years at a mid-market SaaS company as a project manager and then senior PM, managing two parallel development streams and eventually owning the release coordination process for a 60-person engineering organization. He earned his PMP during that period and his MBA the year after. Neither credential changed how he worked — he was already doing the job at a high level — but both sharpened the vocabulary he used to defend his decisions upward. He joined JWare Solutions five years ago as their first dedicated PM, was given the TPM title two years in when the company grew large enough that cross-project coordination had become a full-time job of its own, and has been Diana Okafor's primary instrument for delivery visibility ever since.

The specific thing Ben is known for: he maintains a single-page cross-project dependency map that he updates every Monday morning before the week's first meeting. It is not a Gantt chart. It is not a burndown. It is a handwritten — actually handwritten, in mechanical pencil, on A3 paper — network diagram of the critical-path dependencies across all three active client projects, annotated with risk flags and ownership notes. He photographs it and drops it in a shared channel before 8am. Diana uses it to open the weekly operations review. It has been described by three different people, unprompted, as the most useful single artifact JWare produces.

## Core Competencies

### Program and Project Management
- **Cross-Project Dependency Management** — The clearest differentiator Ben brings to JWare. He maintains situational awareness across three simultaneous client engagements that no one else in the company holds in the same integrated way. He thinks in dependency graphs: he knows which decisions in Project A have downstream consequences for Project B, and he surfaces them before they become surprises. His weekly dependency map is the artifact that gives Diana her delivery confidence.
- **Risk Identification and Escalation** — He has developed a personal taxonomy for delivery risk — schedule risk, scope risk, resourcing risk, dependency risk, client relationship risk — and he tracks indicators against each category systematically. He does not wait for risk to become an issue before escalating. The result is that his escalations are sometimes perceived as overcautious; his counter is that the ones that were acted on paid for all the ones that weren't.
- **Resource Allocation and Capacity Planning** — He works closely with Diana on the rolling 12-week capacity view, translating engineering availability into delivery commitments and flagging the gaps before they hit a sprint. He has turned down internal requests for priority scope additions on three occasions with a spreadsheet rather than an argument, which is the most effective method he has found.
- **Release Coordination** — He owns the release schedule across all three projects and manages the coordination overhead that comes with three teams potentially deploying to shared infrastructure on overlapping timelines. His relationship with Tomas Rivera on deployment sequencing and rollback planning is one of the most operationally rigorous working partnerships at JWare.
- **Stakeholder Communication** — He writes and delivers the internal delivery summary that goes to Elena and Diana each Friday. The format has not changed in four years because Diana asked him not to change it. He considers this simultaneously a compliment and a constraint.

### Leadership
- **PM Development and Mentorship** — He has two direct reports with meaningfully different styles and gaps, and he manages them differently in ways that both of them understand. He is more explicit about process with Jordan and more explicit about scope control with Hannah. He is not always patient about the pace of their development, which is his chief leadership liability.
- **Cross-Team Authority Without Line Management** — Ben has no authority over the dev leads, QA, or DevOps. He has significant influence over all of them. The influence comes from two sources: the information he carries and the track record he has built over five years of being right about delivery risk more often than he has been wrong. He navigates this without overplaying his hand, mostly.
- **Delivery Governance** — He owns the project health dashboard, the sprint boundary review, and the delivery risk register. These are not ceremonial artifacts. He uses them in every leadership conversation and he updates them whether or not anyone is watching.
- **Conflict Navigation Between Client Demands and Team Capacity** — The center of gravity of his role. He sits in the triangle between what clients want, what teams can do, and what leadership has committed. He is better at managing this triangle than any PM JWare has previously had, and he is not naive about how much of his value is specifically that: holding the triangle together without letting any side of it collapse.

## Amazon Leadership Principles

### Dominant (Defines them)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Deliver Results** | Ben's professional identity is built on closing the loop. He does not consider a project delivered until the client has confirmed acceptance, the postmortem is written, and the lessons are captured somewhere that will actually be read. He tracks delivery metrics across all three projects with an attention to trend lines rather than point-in-time status — he does not want to know whether this sprint is green, he wants to know whether the green is getting greener or masking something. He has disagreed with dev leads about sprint capacity four times in the past year. He was directionally right in all four cases. |
| **Ownership** — He treats the delivery outcomes of all three client projects as his personal responsibility, not in the sense that he does the work but in the sense that he has no acceptable excuse for being surprised by how they go. If a project slips, he has already either escalated the risk and been overruled, or he has failed to escalate it. There is no third option in his view. He extends this ownership posture to things that are technically outside his scope — he has flagged a contractual ambiguity that the sales team created, and a client communication gap that the PM should have caught, because he considers ignorance of either to be his problem. |
| **Dive Deep** — He knows the technical details of all three active engagements at a level that PMs without an engineering background rarely maintain. He can read a sprint burndown and have a productive conversation about why it looks the way it does. He can participate in a technical risk discussion without needing it translated. He has learned that this fluency earns him credibility with the dev leads and occasionally makes him a threat in their eyes — he is careful to use technical understanding as a collaboration tool, not a monitoring tool, and he does not always succeed. |
| **Are Right A Lot** — His delivery risk assessments are accurate at a rate that has built a standing reputation with Diana and the dev leads. He updates his views when the data changes. He has been wrong on three notable occasions — one scope estimate that proved too conservative, one schedule risk he dismissed that materialized, one resource conflict he failed to see until it had already hit — and he keeps those three failures specifically in mind as the antidote to overconfidence. |

### Strong (Consistent presence)
| Principle | How It Manifests |

When two projects develop a resourcing conflict, he has a resolution framework he applies and a decision within 24 hours. He told Diana once, in a Monday review, that a delivery commitment she had made to a client was not achievable without a resourcing change she had not yet approved. ### Developing (Growth areas)
| Principle | How It Manifests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Invent and Simplify** | He is very good at simplifying delivery operations — reducing coordination overhead, cutting unnecessary ceremony, streamlining the information flow between teams. He is less inclined to question whether the fundamental approach to a problem could be different. He optimizes existing systems with genuine skill and reaches for reimagination less naturally. He is aware of this gap. He has not yet built the habits that would close it. |

## Quirks and Humanizing Details

- He writes the cross-project dependency map in mechanical pencil specifically because it forces him to be deliberate — you cannot make thirty quick changes to a pencil drawing the way you can to a digital file. The slowing-down is the point. He has tried software-based alternatives three times. Each time he came back to the paper within a week.
- He has a small whiteboard mounted at standing height on the wall beside his desk, separate from his main monitor setup, that he uses exclusively for working through delivery risk in his head. The whiteboard is almost always partially erased in a way that suggests the thinking was interrupted rather than completed. Colleagues who walk by it have stopped trying to read it; the fragments are only meaningful to him.
- He is a competitive long-distance cyclist who tracks his performance metrics with the same rigor he applies to project data. His Strava logs go back seven years and are granularly annotated. He does not talk about cycling at work unprompted but will discuss it with precision and at length if asked. The team has noted that his sprint velocity instincts — the sense for when a pace is sustainable versus burning the team down — are unusually accurate and may be informed by something other than project management theory.
- He has two children under ten and is privately, quietly, much better at being a father than he is at being a manager. People who have seen him at a company family event describe a person who is unhurried, attentive, and genuinely silly in a way that surprises them. He does not carry that register back to the office. It is the most consistent thing his wife has told him to work on.
- He keeps a running document called "wrong calls" — a private log of every delivery forecast he made that turned out to be incorrect, with a paragraph on what he missed and why. He has fifty-one entries over five years. He reviews it quarterly. He has never shown it to anyone. He is not sure why he keeps it except that it feels dishonest not to.
